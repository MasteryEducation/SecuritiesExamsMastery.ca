## Glossary for Chapter 1: The Canadian Securities Industry

Understanding the Canadian securities industry requires a solid grasp of its foundational terms and concepts. This glossary serves as a vital resource for reinforcing your knowledge, enhancing communication within the industry, and applying financial principles in real-world contexts. Each term is defined and contextualized to provide a comprehensive understanding of its role and significance in the financial landscape.

### Financial Intermediary

**Definition:** An institution that acts as a middleman between suppliers and users of capital.

**Context and Application:** Financial intermediaries, such as banks, credit unions, and investment firms, play a crucial role in facilitating the flow of funds from savers to borrowers. For instance, when a Canadian bank accepts deposits from individuals and then lends those funds to businesses for expansion, it acts as a financial intermediary. This process is essential for capital formation and economic growth.

### Capital Markets

**Definition:** Markets where long-term debt or equity-backed securities are bought and sold.

**Context and Application:** Capital markets include the stock and bond markets, where companies and governments raise long-term funds. In Canada, the Toronto Stock Exchange (TSX) is a primary venue for trading equities. Capital markets are vital for providing liquidity and enabling investors to diversify their portfolios.

### Securities Commission

**Definition:** A regulatory authority that oversees the securities industry.

**Context and Application:** In Canada, each province and territory has its own securities commission, such as the Ontario Securities Commission (OSC). These bodies regulate market activities to ensure transparency, fairness, and investor protection. They enforce compliance with securities laws and investigate violations.

### Self-Regulatory Organization (SRO)

**Definition:** An organization that sets and enforces industry regulations.

**Context and Application:** SROs, like the Canadian Investment Regulatory Organization (CIRO), are critical in maintaining industry standards and protecting investors. They oversee the conduct of their members, ensuring adherence to ethical practices and regulatory requirements.

### Primary Market

**Definition:** The market where new securities are issued and sold for the first time.

**Context and Application:** The primary market is where companies raise capital by issuing new shares or bonds. For example, when a Canadian company conducts an Initial Public Offering (IPO) on the TSX, it is participating in the primary market. This process is crucial for capital formation and business expansion.

### Secondary Market

**Definition:** The market where existing securities are traded among investors.

**Context and Application:** The secondary market provides liquidity and price discovery for securities. When investors buy and sell shares on the TSX, they are engaging in secondary market transactions. This market is essential for enabling investors to adjust their portfolios and access cash.

### Underwriting

**Definition:** The process by which investment dealers raise investment capital from investors on behalf of corporations and governments.

**Context and Application:** Underwriting involves assessing the risk of new securities and setting their price. Canadian investment banks, like RBC Capital Markets, often underwrite new bond issues for provincial governments, ensuring they are successfully sold to investors.

### Market Maker

**Definition:** A dealer who stands ready to buy and sell a particular security on a regular and continuous basis at a publicly quoted price.

**Context and Application:** Market makers provide liquidity and facilitate trading by quoting buy and sell prices. For instance, a market maker on the TSX ensures that there is always a buyer or seller for a particular stock, helping to stabilize prices and reduce transaction costs.

### Liquidity

**Definition:** The ease with which an asset can be converted into cash without affecting its market price.

**Context and Application:** Liquidity is a critical factor in investment decisions. Highly liquid assets, like publicly traded stocks, can be quickly sold at market value. In contrast, real estate is less liquid, as it may take time to find a buyer without significantly lowering the price.

### Transaction Costs

**Definition:** Expenses incurred when buying or selling securities.

**Context and Application:** Transaction costs include brokerage fees, taxes, and bid-ask spreads. Minimizing these costs is important for maximizing investment returns. For example, using a discount brokerage platform in Canada can reduce transaction fees for retail investors.

### Capital Formation

**Definition:** The process of building up capital resources through investment.

**Context and Application:** Capital formation is essential for economic growth and development. It involves the accumulation of financial and physical assets, such as machinery and infrastructure. In Canada, government policies that encourage savings and investment contribute to capital formation.

### Financial Stability

**Definition:** The condition in which the financial system is resistant to economic shocks and crises.

**Context and Application:** Financial stability is crucial for maintaining investor confidence and economic growth. Canadian regulatory bodies, such as the Bank of Canada, monitor financial stability by assessing risks and implementing policies to mitigate potential crises.

### Retail Firms

**Definition:** Investment dealers that serve individual investors and provide a wide range of services.

**Context and Application:** Retail firms offer services like financial planning, investment advice, and brokerage. In Canada, firms like TD Direct Investing cater to individual investors, helping them manage their portfolios and achieve financial goals.

### Institutional Firms

**Definition:** Dealers that exclusively serve large institutional clients like pension funds and mutual funds.

**Context and Application:** Institutional firms focus on large-scale transactions and complex financial products. They provide services such as asset management and research to clients like the Canada Pension Plan Investment Board (CPPIB).

### Integrated Firms

**Definition:** Dealers that offer comprehensive products and services across both retail and institutional markets.

**Context and Application:** Integrated firms, such as BMO Capital Markets, provide a full range of services, from retail banking to investment banking. This integration allows them to serve diverse client needs and leverage synergies across business lines.

### Self-Directed Brokers

**Definition:** Brokers that execute trades on behalf of clients without providing investment advice.

**Context and Application:** Self-directed brokers offer platforms for investors who prefer to manage their own portfolios. In Canada, platforms like Questrade allow investors to trade stocks, ETFs, and other securities independently.

### Trust Companies

**Definition:** Institutions that act as trustees for clients, managing assets such as property, stocks, and bonds.

**Context and Application:** Trust companies provide fiduciary services, ensuring that assets are managed according to clients' wishes. In Canada, companies like RBC Trust offer estate planning and wealth management services.

### Credit Unions

**Definition:** Member-owned financial cooperatives that provide traditional banking services.

**Context and Application:** Credit unions offer savings accounts, loans, and mortgages to their members. They operate on a not-for-profit basis, with profits returned to members through lower fees and better rates. Examples in Canada include Vancity and Desjardins.

### Defined Benefit Pension Plan

**Definition:** A retirement plan where employee benefits are computed using a formula based on employee earnings history and tenure of service.

**Context and Application:** Defined benefit plans provide predictable retirement income, often based on salary and years of service. Many Canadian public sector employees participate in such plans, which are managed by entities like the Ontario Teachers' Pension Plan.

### Defined Contribution Pension Plan

**Definition:** A retirement plan where the employer, employee, or both make contributions on a regular basis.

**Context and Application:** In defined contribution plans, retirement benefits depend on investment performance. Contributions are invested in a portfolio chosen by the employee. Many Canadian private sector employers offer these plans, providing flexibility in retirement savings.

### Robo-Advisors

**Definition:** Automated platforms that provide algorithm-driven financial planning services with little to no human supervision.

**Context and Application:** Robo-advisors offer low-cost investment management by using algorithms to allocate assets based on client preferences. In Canada, platforms like Wealthsimple provide accessible investment solutions for individuals seeking automated portfolio management.
