## Chapter 9: Equity Securities: Equity Transactions

Equity transactions form the backbone of the securities market, facilitating the buying and selling of ownership stakes in companies. This chapter delves into the mechanics of equity transactions, focusing on the Canadian context. We will explore the differences between cash and margin accounts, the intricacies of establishing long and short positions, and the impact of price changes on margin requirements. Additionally, we will cover trading and settlement procedures and the various types of buy and sell orders used in securities trading.

### Understanding Cash and Margin Accounts

#### Cash Accounts

A cash account is a straightforward type of brokerage account where the investor must pay the full amount for the securities purchased. This means that transactions are settled using the investor's own funds, without borrowing. Cash accounts are typically used by investors who prefer to avoid the risks associated with borrowing money to invest.

**Key Features of Cash Accounts:**
- **Full Payment Required:** Investors must have sufficient funds in their account to cover the entire cost of the purchase.
- **No Borrowing:** Transactions are conducted using the investor's own capital, eliminating the risk of margin calls.
- **Simplicity:** Cash accounts are simpler to manage and understand, making them ideal for novice investors.

#### Margin Accounts

Margin accounts, on the other hand, allow investors to borrow funds from their broker to purchase securities. This leverage can amplify both gains and losses, making margin accounts suitable for more experienced investors who understand the risks involved.

**Key Features of Margin Accounts:**
- **Leverage:** Investors can borrow money to increase their purchasing power.
- **Interest Charges:** Borrowed funds incur interest, which can affect overall returns.
- **Margin Requirements:** Investors must maintain a minimum level of equity in their account, known as the maintenance margin.

### Establishing Long and Short Positions

#### Long Positions

A long position involves buying a security with the expectation that its price will rise. This is the most common type of investment strategy, where the investor profits from an increase in the security's value.

**Example:** Consider an investor who buys 100 shares of RBC at $100 per share, expecting the price to rise to $120. If the price does increase, the investor can sell the shares at a profit.

#### Short Positions

A short position involves selling a security that the investor does not own, with the intention of buying it back later at a lower price. This strategy is used when an investor anticipates a decline in the security's price.

**Example:** An investor believes that TD Bank's stock, currently trading at $80, will drop in value. The investor borrows 100 shares and sells them. If the price falls to $70, the investor can buy back the shares at the lower price, returning them to the lender and pocketing the difference.

### Impact of Price Changes on Margin Requirements

Price changes can significantly affect margin requirements, particularly for short positions. When the price of a security in a long position falls, or the price of a security in a short position rises, the investor may face a margin call, requiring them to deposit additional funds or securities to maintain the required margin level.

**Example:** If an investor holds a long position in a stock that declines in value, the equity in their margin account decreases. If the equity falls below the maintenance margin, the investor will receive a margin call.

### Trading and Settlement Procedures

In Canada, equity transactions follow a standardized trading and settlement process. Understanding these procedures is crucial for investors to ensure compliance and efficient execution of trades.

#### Trading Procedures

- **Order Placement:** Investors place buy or sell orders through their broker, specifying the type of order and the desired price.
- **Order Execution:** Orders are executed on the stock exchange, where buyers and sellers are matched.
- **Confirmation:** Once an order is executed, the investor receives a confirmation detailing the transaction.

#### Settlement Procedures

- **T+2 Settlement Cycle:** In Canada, the standard settlement period for equity transactions is two business days after the trade date (T+2).
- **Delivery and Payment:** On the settlement date, the buyer receives the securities, and the seller receives payment.

### Types of Buy and Sell Orders

Investors can use various types of orders to execute trades, each serving different strategic purposes.

#### Market Orders

A market order is an instruction to buy or sell a security immediately at the best available price. Market orders guarantee execution but not the price.

#### Limit Orders

A limit order specifies the maximum price an investor is willing to pay for a buy order or the minimum price they are willing to accept for a sell order. Limit orders provide price control but do not guarantee execution.

#### Stop Orders

A stop order becomes a market order once a specified price, known as the stop price, is reached. Stop orders are used to limit losses or protect profits.

#### Stop-Limit Orders

A stop-limit order combines the features of a stop order and a limit order. Once the stop price is reached, the order becomes a limit order, executed only at the specified limit price or better.

### Glossary of Essential Terms

- **Cash Account:** A brokerage account where transactions are settled using the investor's own funds.
- **Margin Account:** A brokerage account that allows investors to borrow funds to purchase securities.
- **Long Position:** Buying a security with the expectation that its price will rise.
- **Short Position:** Selling a security not owned, with the intention of buying it back at a lower price.
- **Margin Call:** A demand by a broker for an investor to deposit additional funds or securities to maintain the required margin level.
- **Market Order:** An order to buy or sell a security immediately at the best available price.
- **Limit Order:** An order to buy or sell a security at a specified price or better.
- **Stop Order:** An order that becomes a market order once a specified price is reached.
- **Stop-Limit Order:** An order that becomes a limit order once a specified price is reached.

### Best Practices and Common Pitfalls

**Best Practices:**
- **Understand Leverage:** Before using a margin account, ensure you understand the risks and potential for amplified losses.
- **Monitor Positions:** Regularly review your portfolio and margin requirements to avoid unexpected margin calls.
- **Use Limit Orders:** Consider using limit orders to control the price at which your trades are executed.

**Common Pitfalls:**
- **Ignoring Margin Calls:** Failing to respond to a margin call can result in the forced sale of securities.
- **Overleveraging:** Borrowing too much can lead to significant losses if the market moves against your position.

### Conclusion

Equity transactions are a fundamental aspect of investing in the securities market. By understanding the differences between cash and margin accounts, the mechanics of long and short positions, and the various types of buy and sell orders, investors can make informed decisions and effectively manage their portfolios. As you continue to explore the world of equity securities, remember to apply these principles to your own investment strategies, always considering the unique aspects of the Canadian market.
