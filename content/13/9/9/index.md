## Glossary for Chapter 9: Equity Securities - Equity Transactions

In this section, we delve into the essential terminology and concepts introduced in Chapter 9 of the CSC® Exam Prep Guide: Volume 1. This glossary serves as a comprehensive reference tool for understanding the intricacies of equity transactions, reinforcing learning, and ensuring clarity in the financial landscape. Each term is defined with precision, reflecting current industry standards and practices within the Canadian financial market.

### Arrears
**Definition:** Arrears refer to unpaid dividends that accumulate for cumulative preferred shares. These dividends must be paid out before any dividends can be distributed to common shareholders. 

**Application:** Understanding arrears is crucial for investors holding cumulative preferred shares, as it affects the timing and certainty of dividend payments.

### Buy Order
**Definition:** A buy order is an instruction to purchase a security. It specifies the number of shares and the price at which the investor is willing to buy.

**Example:** An investor places a buy order for 100 shares of RBC at $100 per share, indicating their willingness to purchase at that price.

### Cash Account
**Definition:** A cash account is a brokerage account where the client must pay for securities in full by the settlement date. It does not allow for the borrowing of funds to purchase securities.

**Consideration:** Investors using cash accounts must ensure they have sufficient funds to cover their transactions by the settlement date.

### Confirmation
**Definition:** A confirmation is a document sent to the buyer and seller detailing the specifics of a completed trade, including the price, quantity, and date of the transaction.

**Importance:** Confirmations serve as official records of transactions, providing transparency and accountability in trading activities.

### Convertible Preferred Shares
**Definition:** Convertible preferred shares are preferred shares that can be converted into a set number of common shares, usually at the discretion of the shareholder.

**Strategic Use:** Investors may opt for convertible preferred shares to benefit from the stability of dividends while retaining the potential for capital appreciation through conversion.

### Day Order
**Definition:** A day order is an order that expires at the end of the trading day if not executed. It is used for short-term trading strategies.

**Example:** An investor places a day order to buy shares of TD Bank, which will only be valid for the current trading session.

### Dealer Member
**Definition:** A dealer member is a member of a stock exchange authorized to buy and sell securities. They facilitate transactions between buyers and sellers.

**Role:** Dealer members play a critical role in maintaining market liquidity and ensuring efficient execution of trades.

### Ex-Dividend Date
**Definition:** The ex-dividend date is the cutoff date to determine eligible shareholders for dividend payments. Investors who purchase shares on or after this date are not entitled to the declared dividend.

**Timing:** Investors seeking dividend income must be aware of the ex-dividend date to ensure eligibility for upcoming payments.

### Good Through Order
**Definition:** A good through order is an order that remains active until filled or until a specified date. It provides flexibility for investors who want to maintain their order beyond a single trading session.

**Example:** An investor places a good through order to sell shares of a company, specifying that the order remains active for one week.

### Limit Order
**Definition:** A limit order is an order to buy or sell a security at a specified price or better. It provides control over the price at which a transaction is executed.

**Practical Use:** Limit orders are useful for investors who want to ensure they do not pay more or sell for less than a predetermined price.

### Margin Account
**Definition:** A margin account is a brokerage account that allows investors to borrow funds to purchase securities, using the securities as collateral.

**Risk Consideration:** While margin accounts can amplify returns, they also increase risk, as losses can exceed the initial investment.

### Margin Call
**Definition:** A margin call is a demand by the broker for the investor to deposit additional money or securities to cover possible losses. It occurs when the account's equity falls below the required maintenance margin.

**Response:** Investors must act promptly to meet margin calls to avoid forced liquidation of their positions.

### Market Order
**Definition:** A market order is an order to buy or sell a security immediately at the best available current price. It prioritizes speed of execution over price.

**Example:** An investor places a market order to sell shares of a company, ensuring immediate execution at the prevailing market price.

### On-Stop Buy Order
**Definition:** An on-stop buy order is an order to buy a security once it reaches a specified price, often used to protect short positions.

**Strategic Use:** This order type helps investors manage risk by automatically entering a long position if the security's price rises to a certain level.

### On-Stop Sell Order
**Definition:** Also known as a stop loss order, it triggers a sale of a security once it reaches a specified price to limit losses.

**Example:** An investor sets an on-stop sell order for shares of a company at $50 to prevent further losses if the price falls to that level.

### Order Execution
**Definition:** Order execution is the process of completing a buy or sell order in the market. It involves matching the order with a counterparty and finalizing the transaction.

**Efficiency:** Efficient order execution is vital for minimizing transaction costs and achieving desired investment outcomes.

### Operating Margin
**Definition:** Operating margin is the portion of profit a company earns from its operations before deducting interest and taxes. It is expressed as a percentage of sales.

**Analysis:** Investors use operating margin to assess a company's operational efficiency and profitability.

### PRO Order
**Definition:** A PRO order refers to professional orders placed by dealers or insiders, which have priority over client orders at the same price.

**Market Impact:** Understanding PRO orders is important for retail investors, as these orders can affect the execution of their trades.

### Short Position
**Definition:** A short position involves selling securities that the investor does not own, anticipating a price decline. The investor aims to buy back the securities at a lower price.

**Risk Management:** Short selling carries significant risk, as potential losses are theoretically unlimited if the security's price rises.

### Stop Loss Order
**Definition:** A stop loss order is placed to sell a security when it reaches a certain price to limit an investor's loss.

**Example:** An investor sets a stop loss order for shares of a company at $45 to mitigate potential losses if the price drops.

### Trading Procedure
**Definition:** Trading procedure encompasses the steps involved in executing and settling a securities transaction, including order placement, execution, and confirmation.

**Importance:** Understanding trading procedures is essential for investors to navigate the complexities of the securities market effectively.
