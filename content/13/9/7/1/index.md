## 9.7.1 Types of Orders

In the dynamic world of equity transactions, understanding the different types of orders is crucial for executing effective trading strategies. Each order type serves a specific purpose and can significantly impact the outcome of your trades. This section delves into the various types of orders available to investors, providing insights into when and how to use them effectively.

### Market Order

A **Market Order** is the most straightforward type of order, instructing the broker to buy or sell a security immediately at the best available current price. This type of order guarantees execution but does not guarantee the price. Market orders are typically used when the priority is to execute the trade quickly rather than at a specific price.

#### When to Use Market Orders

- **High Liquidity Situations:** When trading highly liquid stocks where the bid-ask spread is narrow, market orders can be effective.
- **Urgency:** When immediate execution is more critical than price, such as in rapidly moving markets.
- **Entering or Exiting Positions Quickly:** Useful for investors who need to enter or exit positions swiftly without concern for slight price variations.

#### Example

Consider an investor looking to purchase shares of a major Canadian bank like RBC. If the investor places a market order, the trade will execute at the current market price, ensuring the shares are bought immediately, albeit with potential price fluctuations.

### Limit Order

A **Limit Order** specifies the maximum price you are willing to pay when buying or the minimum price you are willing to accept when selling. This order type provides price certainty but does not guarantee execution.

#### Benefits of Limit Orders

- **Price Control:** Ensures that you do not pay more than a set price when buying or receive less than a set price when selling.
- **Avoid Unfavorable Prices:** Protects against executing trades at prices that are not aligned with your investment strategy.

#### Example

Suppose an investor wants to buy shares of TD Bank but only if the price drops to $80. By placing a limit order at $80, the investor ensures that the purchase will only occur if the stock reaches that price, preventing overpayment.

### Day Order

A **Day Order** is valid only for the trading day on which it is placed. If the order is not executed by the end of the trading day, it expires.

#### Key Points

- **Short-Term Strategy:** Ideal for investors who want to capitalize on daily price movements.
- **Expiration:** Automatically expires at the end of the trading day, requiring no further action from the investor.

### Good Through Order

A **Good Through Order** remains active until a specified date, allowing more flexibility than a day order. This type of order is beneficial for investors who anticipate price movements over a longer period.

#### Advantages

- **Extended Time Frame:** Provides a longer window for the order to be executed, increasing the likelihood of fulfillment.
- **Strategic Planning:** Useful for investors with a medium-term outlook on price movements.

### On-Stop Sell Order (Stop Loss)

An **On-Stop Sell Order**, commonly known as a **Stop Loss Order**, is designed to limit an investor's loss on a security position. It triggers a market order to sell when the security's price falls to a predetermined level.

#### Protective Strategy

- **Loss Mitigation:** Automatically sells the security to prevent further losses if the price drops significantly.
- **Peace of Mind:** Provides a safety net for investors, especially in volatile markets.

#### Example

An investor holds shares of a Canadian tech company and sets a stop loss order at $50. If the stock price falls to $50, the stop loss order triggers a market sale, limiting potential losses.

### On-Stop Buy Order (Stop Buy)

An **On-Stop Buy Order** is used to buy a security once its price reaches a specified level, often used to limit losses on short positions or to enter positions in anticipation of upward momentum.

#### Strategic Use

- **Short Position Protection:** Limits losses on short positions by buying back the security if the price rises to a certain level.
- **Momentum Trading:** Allows investors to enter a position once a security demonstrates upward momentum.

### PRO Order

A **PRO Order** is a professional order placed by dealers or insiders, often given priority over client orders. These orders can influence market dynamics due to their size and the insider knowledge involved.

#### Considerations

- **Market Impact:** PRO orders can significantly impact stock prices due to their volume and priority.
- **Regulatory Oversight:** Subject to strict regulations to prevent market manipulation.

### Glossary

- **Stop Loss Order:** An order to sell a security when it reaches a certain price to limit an investor's loss on that security.
- **Limit Price:** The price specified in a limit order at which the investor is willing to buy or sell.
- **Trade Confirmation:** Documentation sent to the investor detailing the specifics of a completed trade.

Understanding these order types and their strategic applications can enhance your trading effectiveness and risk management. By selecting the appropriate order type, investors can better align their trades with their financial goals and market conditions.
