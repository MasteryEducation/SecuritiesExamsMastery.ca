## 9.7 How Securities Are Bought and Sold

In the world of securities trading, understanding how securities are bought and sold is crucial for both novice and seasoned investors. This section delves into the mechanics of trading, focusing on the various order types, the dynamics of bid and ask prices, and the strategic selection of orders to optimize investment outcomes. By the end of this chapter, you will have a comprehensive understanding of how to navigate the Canadian securities market effectively.

### Order Types Overview

When buying or selling securities, investors have several order types at their disposal, each serving different purposes and strategies. These order types can be categorized based on duration, price restrictions, and special instructions. Understanding these categories is essential for executing trades that align with your investment goals.

#### Market Order

A market order is the simplest type of order, instructing the broker to buy or sell a security immediately at the best available current price. This order type is ideal for investors who prioritize speed over price, as it ensures quick execution but does not guarantee the price at which the trade will be executed.

#### Limit Order

A limit order specifies the maximum price you are willing to pay when buying or the minimum price you are willing to accept when selling. This order type provides price control, ensuring that trades are executed only at the desired price or better. However, there is a risk that the order may not be executed if the market does not reach the specified price.

#### Stop Order

A stop order becomes a market order once a specified price, known as the stop price, is reached. This order type is often used to limit losses or protect profits. For example, a stop-loss order can be set to sell a stock if its price falls below a certain level.

#### Stop-Limit Order

A stop-limit order combines the features of a stop order and a limit order. Once the stop price is reached, the order becomes a limit order, which will only be executed at the specified limit price or better. This order type provides more control over the execution price but may not be filled if the market moves quickly.

#### Duration-Based Orders

Orders can also be categorized based on their duration:

- **Day Order:** Valid only for the trading day on which it is placed. If not executed, it expires at the end of the day.
- **Good-Till-Cancelled (GTC):** Remains active until executed or cancelled by the investor.
- **Immediate-Or-Cancel (IOC):** Requires that any part of the order that can be filled immediately is executed, and the rest is cancelled.
- **Fill-Or-Kill (FOK):** Must be executed immediately in its entirety or not at all.

### Strategies for Order Selection

Selecting the appropriate order type is a strategic decision that can significantly impact investment outcomes. Different market conditions and investor goals necessitate different order types. Here are some strategies to consider:

- **Volatile Markets:** In highly volatile markets, limit orders can protect against unfavorable price movements by ensuring trades are executed at a desired price.
- **Long-Term Investments:** For long-term investors, GTC orders can be useful for setting target buy or sell prices without the need to monitor the market constantly.
- **Risk Management:** Stop-loss orders are essential for managing risk and protecting against significant losses in a declining market.
- **Liquidity Considerations:** In markets with low liquidity, market orders may lead to significant price slippage, making limit orders a better choice.

### Bid-Ask Dynamics

The bid-ask spread is a fundamental concept in securities trading, representing the difference between the highest price a buyer is willing to pay (bid) and the lowest price a seller is willing to accept (ask). Understanding this dynamic is crucial for effective trading.

#### Bid-Ask Spread

The bid-ask spread can be influenced by several factors, including market liquidity, volatility, and the specific security being traded. A narrow spread often indicates a liquid market with high trading volume, while a wide spread may suggest lower liquidity and higher volatility.

#### Market Makers

Market makers play a vital role in maintaining liquidity by continuously quoting bid and ask prices. They profit from the spread by buying at the bid price and selling at the ask price. In Canadian markets, major banks like RBC and TD often act as market makers, ensuring smooth trading operations.

### Importance of Order Types

Choosing the appropriate order type is critical for optimizing trade execution. The right order type can influence both the speed and price of execution, impacting overall investment performance. Here are some key considerations:

- **Execution Speed:** Market orders provide the fastest execution but may result in less favorable prices during volatile market conditions.
- **Price Control:** Limit orders offer price control, ensuring trades are executed at desired levels, but may result in missed opportunities if the market does not reach the specified price.
- **Risk Management:** Stop and stop-limit orders are essential tools for managing risk and protecting against adverse price movements.

### Practical Example: Trading in Canadian Markets

Consider a scenario where a Canadian investor wants to purchase shares of a major Canadian bank, such as TD Bank. The investor believes the stock is undervalued and wants to buy at a specific price. By placing a limit order, the investor can specify the maximum price they are willing to pay, ensuring they do not overpay in a volatile market.

Alternatively, if the investor is concerned about potential downside risk, they might use a stop-loss order to sell the shares if the price falls below a certain level, thereby limiting potential losses.

### Conclusion

Understanding how securities are bought and sold is fundamental to successful investing. By mastering the various order types and their strategic applications, investors can navigate the complexities of the Canadian securities market with confidence. Whether managing risk, optimizing execution, or aligning trades with investment goals, the right order type can make a significant difference in achieving desired outcomes.

### Glossary

- **Market Order:** An order to buy or sell a security immediately at the best available current price.
- **Limit Order:** An order to buy or sell a security at a specific price or better.
