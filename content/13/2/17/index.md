## Glossary for Chapter 2: The Capital Market

Understanding the capital market is crucial for anyone involved in financial services, investment, or securities trading. This glossary provides a comprehensive overview of key terms and concepts that are foundational to navigating the capital market, particularly within the Canadian context. By familiarizing yourself with these terms, you will enhance your ability to analyze financial scenarios, communicate effectively in the industry, and apply these concepts to real-world situations.

### Alternative Trading System (ATS)
An Alternative Trading System (ATS) is an electronic marketplace that facilitates the matching and execution of trades outside traditional exchanges. ATSs provide a platform for trading securities in a more flexible and often more cost-effective manner. They are crucial for increasing market efficiency and liquidity by offering additional venues for trading beyond the conventional stock exchanges.

**Example:** In Canada, ATSs like Alpha Exchange and MATCHNow allow for trading Canadian equities, providing investors with alternatives to the Toronto Stock Exchange (TSX).

### Auction Market
An auction market is a market where buyers and sellers compete to buy and sell securities at specified prices. In this market, transactions occur when a buyer's bid price matches a seller's ask price. The TSX operates as an auction market, where prices are determined by supply and demand dynamics.

**Example:** The TSX uses a continuous auction system where trades are executed throughout the trading day as orders are matched.

### Bid-Ask Spread
The bid-ask spread is the difference between the highest price a buyer is willing to pay (bid price) and the lowest price a seller is willing to accept (ask price). It is a key indicator of market liquidity and transaction costs.

**Example:** If the bid price for a stock is $50 and the ask price is $51, the bid-ask spread is $1. A narrower spread often indicates higher liquidity.

### Bid Price
The bid price is the maximum price a buyer is willing to pay for a security. It reflects the demand side of the market and is a critical component in determining the bid-ask spread.

**Example:** An investor willing to purchase shares of RBC might place a bid at $100 per share, indicating the highest price they are prepared to pay.

### Ask Price (Offer)
The ask price, or offer, is the minimum price a seller is willing to accept for a security. It represents the supply side of the market.

**Example:** A seller looking to offload shares of TD Bank might set an ask price of $102, indicating the lowest price they are willing to accept.

### Capital Allocation
Capital allocation is the process of distributing financial resources among various investments to achieve optimal returns. Effective capital allocation is essential for maximizing shareholder value and ensuring the efficient use of resources.

**Example:** A Canadian pension fund might allocate capital across equities, fixed-income securities, and alternative investments to balance risk and return.

### Capital Formation
Capital formation is the process of building up capital resources through investment. It is a critical component of economic growth, as it involves the accumulation of financial assets that can be used for productive purposes.

**Example:** The issuance of new shares by a Canadian corporation to raise funds for expansion is an example of capital formation.

### Dealer Market
A dealer market is a market where multiple dealers post prices at which they are willing to buy or sell securities. Unlike auction markets, dealer markets involve intermediaries who facilitate trades by maintaining inventories of securities.

**Example:** The Canadian bond market operates primarily as a dealer market, with dealers quoting prices for government and corporate bonds.

### Equity Securities
Equity securities represent ownership in a company. They entitle the holder to a share of the company's profits and assets. Common stocks are the most prevalent form of equity securities.

**Example:** Purchasing shares of Shopify Inc. gives investors equity ownership in the company, along with voting rights and potential dividends.

### Fixed-Income Securities
Fixed-income securities are debt instruments that provide regular interest payments and return of principal at maturity. They are typically less volatile than equities and are used for income generation and capital preservation.

**Example:** Canadian government bonds are a popular fixed-income investment, offering periodic interest payments and principal repayment at maturity.

### Liquidity
Liquidity refers to the ability to quickly convert an asset into cash without significantly affecting its price. High liquidity is desirable as it allows for easier entry and exit from investments.

**Example:** Blue-chip stocks on the TSX, such as those of major banks, are considered highly liquid due to their active trading and large market capitalization.

### Market Maker
A market maker is an entity that provides liquidity by being ready to buy and sell securities at publicly quoted prices. Market makers play a crucial role in ensuring smooth market operations and reducing price volatility.

**Example:** In Canada, market makers on the TSX help maintain orderly trading by continuously quoting bid and ask prices for various securities.

### Mutual Funds
Mutual funds are pooled investment funds managed by professionals that invest in a diversified portfolio of securities. They offer investors access to a broad range of assets and professional management.

**Example:** A Canadian mutual fund might invest in a mix of domestic and international equities, bonds, and other securities to achieve diversification.

### Over-the-Counter (OTC) Market
The OTC market is a decentralized market where securities are traded directly between parties without a central exchange. It is often used for trading less liquid securities or those not listed on formal exchanges.

**Example:** Many small-cap Canadian stocks and certain derivatives are traded OTC, providing flexibility but often with less transparency.

### Primary Market
The primary market is where new securities are issued and sold to investors for the first time. It is a critical component of capital formation, allowing companies and governments to raise funds.

**Example:** An initial public offering (IPO) of a Canadian tech company on the TSX represents a primary market transaction.

### Secondary Market
The secondary market is where existing securities are traded among investors after the initial issuance. It provides liquidity and price discovery for securities.

**Example:** The TSX serves as a secondary market for trading shares of Canadian companies, facilitating the buying and selling of previously issued stocks.

### Self-Regulatory Organization (SRO)
A Self-Regulatory Organization (SRO) is an organization that sets and enforces industry regulations. SROs play a vital role in maintaining market integrity and protecting investors.

**Example:** The Canadian Investment Regulatory Organization (CIRO) is an SRO that oversees investment dealers and trading activity in Canada.

### Stock Exchange
A stock exchange is a centralized marketplace where stocks and other securities are publicly traded. Exchanges provide a regulated environment for buying and selling securities.

**Example:** The TSX is Canada's primary stock exchange, offering a platform for trading equities, ETFs, and other securities.

### Treasury Bills/Bonds
Treasury bills and bonds are government-issued securities used to raise capital. Treasury bills are short-term instruments, while bonds are long-term.

**Example:** The Government of Canada issues treasury bills with maturities of up to one year and bonds with longer maturities to finance public expenditures.

### Unlisted Securities
Unlisted securities are not listed on formal stock exchanges and are traded OTC. They may offer unique investment opportunities but often come with higher risks.

**Example:** Shares of a private Canadian company traded through an OTC platform are considered unlisted securities.

### Underwriting
Underwriting is the process by which investment dealers raise investment capital from investors on behalf of corporations and governments. Underwriters assess risk and help set the price for new securities.

**Example:** A Canadian investment bank underwriting an IPO for a mining company would evaluate the company's prospects and market conditions to determine the offering price.

### Volatility
Volatility is the degree of variation in the price of a security over time. High volatility indicates significant price fluctuations, while low volatility suggests stable prices.

**Example:** The Canadian dollar's exchange rate may experience volatility due to changes in commodity prices or economic data releases.

By mastering these terms, you will be better equipped to navigate the complexities of the capital market, make informed investment decisions, and communicate effectively within the financial industry.
