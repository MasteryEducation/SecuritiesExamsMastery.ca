## 4.5.1 Determinants of Interest Rates

Interest rates are a fundamental component of the financial landscape, influencing everything from personal savings to corporate investments and government borrowing. Understanding the determinants of interest rates is crucial for financial professionals, as these rates impact economic activity, investment decisions, and financial markets. This section delves into the key influences on interest rates, the role of the Bank of Canada, and the impact of global economic factors.

### Key Influences on Interest Rates

#### Supply and Demand for Capital

Interest rates are primarily determined by the supply and demand for capital. When the demand for capital increases, perhaps due to businesses seeking funds for expansion or consumers looking for loans, interest rates tend to rise. Conversely, if the supply of capital increases, such as through higher savings rates or foreign investment inflows, interest rates may decrease.

**Example:** Consider a scenario where Canadian businesses are experiencing a boom and require more capital to expand operations. This increased demand for capital can lead to higher interest rates as businesses compete for available funds. On the other hand, if Canadians increase their savings, the supply of capital rises, potentially lowering interest rates.

#### Default Risk

Default risk is the risk that a borrower will not be able to make the required payments on their debt. Lenders demand higher interest rates to compensate for higher default risks. This risk premium varies across different borrowers and financial instruments.

**Example:** A Canadian corporation with a strong credit rating may secure loans at lower interest rates compared to a startup with uncertain prospects. The higher perceived default risk of the startup necessitates a higher interest rate to attract lenders.

#### Foreign Interest Rates

International interest rates can influence domestic rates through capital flows and exchange rate adjustments. If foreign interest rates rise, investors may move their capital abroad to seek higher returns, leading to a depreciation of the domestic currency and potentially higher domestic interest rates to retain capital.

**Example:** If the U.S. Federal Reserve raises interest rates, Canadian investors might find U.S. bonds more attractive, leading to capital outflows from Canada. To counteract this, Canadian interest rates might need to rise to retain investment within the country.

#### Inflation Expectations

Inflation expectations play a critical role in determining nominal interest rates. According to the Fisher equation, nominal interest rates are the sum of real interest rates and expected inflation. If inflation is expected to rise, lenders demand higher nominal rates to maintain their real returns.

**Example:** If inflation in Canada is expected to increase due to rising commodity prices, lenders will likely demand higher interest rates to ensure their returns are not eroded by inflation.

### Assessing Central Bank Policies

#### The Role of the Bank of Canada

The Bank of Canada plays a pivotal role in setting interest rates through its monetary policy. By adjusting the overnight rate, the Bank influences short-term interest rates and, consequently, economic activity. The Bank uses these tools to achieve its primary objectives: controlling inflation and supporting economic growth.

**Example:** During an economic downturn, the Bank of Canada may lower the overnight rate to reduce borrowing costs, encouraging spending and investment. Conversely, to combat high inflation, the Bank might raise rates to cool economic activity.

#### Impact on Borrowing Costs and Economic Activity

Changes in interest rates affect borrowing costs for consumers and businesses. Lower rates reduce the cost of loans, stimulating spending and investment, while higher rates increase borrowing costs, potentially slowing economic activity.

**Example:** A reduction in interest rates can lead to increased mortgage borrowing, boosting the housing market and related industries. Conversely, higher rates might dampen consumer spending and business investments.

### Considering Global Economic Factors

#### Global Economic Conditions and Policies

Global economic conditions, such as economic growth rates, trade policies, and geopolitical events, can influence national interest rates. Central banks worldwide monitor these conditions to adjust their policies accordingly.

**Example:** A global economic slowdown might prompt central banks, including the Bank of Canada, to lower interest rates to stimulate domestic demand and counteract external economic pressures.

#### Transmission Mechanism of Global Interest Rate Changes

Changes in global interest rates can affect domestic financial markets through various channels, including exchange rates, capital flows, and investor sentiment. These changes can have significant implications for domestic monetary policy and economic stability.

**Example:** If the European Central Bank implements a significant rate cut, it could lead to a stronger Canadian dollar as investors seek higher returns in Canada, affecting export competitiveness and prompting the Bank of Canada to adjust its policy stance.

### Glossary

- **Monetary Policy:** The process by which a central bank controls the money supply and interest rates to influence the economy.
- **Exchange Rate Depreciation:** A decrease in the value of a currency relative to another currency.
- **Exchange Rate Appreciation:** An increase in the value of a currency relative to another currency.

### Conclusion

Understanding the determinants of interest rates is essential for navigating the financial landscape. By considering factors such as supply and demand for capital, default risk, foreign interest rates, and inflation expectations, along with the role of the Bank of Canada and global economic influences, financial professionals can make informed decisions. As you continue to explore the intricacies of interest rates, remember to apply these principles to real-world scenarios, enhancing your ability to anticipate and respond to changes in the financial environment.
