## Glossary for Chapter 4: Overview of Economics

Understanding the fundamental economic concepts is crucial for anyone involved in financial markets or economic analysis. This glossary serves as a comprehensive guide to key terms discussed in Chapter 4 of the CSC® Exam Prep Guide: Volume 1. By familiarizing yourself with these terms, you will enhance your ability to analyze economic scenarios, make informed financial decisions, and communicate effectively within the industry.

### Gross Domestic Product (GDP)

**Definition:** The total monetary value of all goods and services produced within a country over a specific period, typically annually or quarterly.

**Application:** GDP is a primary indicator of a country's economic health. For instance, Canada's GDP growth rate is closely monitored to assess economic performance and guide policy decisions. A rising GDP indicates economic expansion, while a declining GDP may signal a recession.

**Example:** In 2022, Canada's GDP was approximately CAD 2 trillion, reflecting the total economic output of the nation.

### Consumer Price Index (CPI)

**Definition:** A measure that examines the weighted average of prices of a basket of consumer goods and services, such as transportation, food, and medical care.

**Application:** CPI is used to assess price changes associated with the cost of living. It is a key indicator of inflation, influencing monetary policy decisions by the Bank of Canada.

**Example:** If the CPI increases by 2% over a year, it suggests that the average price level of consumer goods and services has risen by 2%.

### Business Cycle

**Definition:** The natural rise and fall of economic growth that occurs over time, consisting of periods of expansion and contraction.

**Application:** Understanding the business cycle helps investors and policymakers anticipate changes in economic conditions and adjust strategies accordingly.

**Example:** The Canadian economy experienced a recession during the global financial crisis of 2008-2009, followed by a period of recovery and expansion.

### Unemployment Rate

**Definition:** The percentage of the labour force that is unemployed and actively seeking employment.

**Application:** The unemployment rate is a critical indicator of labour market health. High unemployment may indicate economic distress, while low unemployment suggests a robust job market.

**Example:** As of 2023, Canada's unemployment rate was approximately 5.5%, reflecting the proportion of the labour force without jobs.

### Interest Rates

**Definition:** The amount charged by lenders to borrowers for the use of money, expressed as a percentage of the principal.

**Application:** Interest rates influence borrowing, spending, and investment decisions. The Bank of Canada sets the benchmark interest rate, affecting rates across the economy.

**Example:** A mortgage with an interest rate of 3% means the borrower pays 3% of the loan amount annually as interest.

### Inflation

**Definition:** The rate at which the general level of prices for goods and services is rising, eroding purchasing power.

**Application:** Inflation impacts consumer purchasing power and investment returns. Central banks aim to maintain stable inflation to ensure economic stability.

**Example:** If inflation is 2%, a basket of goods that cost CAD 100 last year would cost CAD 102 this year.

### Exchange Rate

**Definition:** The value of one currency in terms of another, determined by the foreign exchange market.

**Application:** Exchange rates affect international trade and investment. A strong Canadian dollar makes imports cheaper but can hurt exports by making them more expensive abroad.

**Example:** If the exchange rate is 1.25 CAD/USD, it means 1 US dollar is equivalent to 1.25 Canadian dollars.

### Balance of Payments

**Definition:** A statement that summarizes a country’s transactions with the rest of the world for a specific time period, including trade, investment, and financial transfers.

**Application:** The balance of payments provides insights into a country's economic interactions with the global economy, influencing currency value and economic policy.

**Example:** A surplus in Canada's balance of payments indicates more exports than imports, contributing positively to the economy.

### Phillips Curve

**Definition:** An economic concept that shows an inverse relationship between the rate of unemployment and the rate of inflation.

**Application:** The Phillips Curve suggests that lower unemployment can lead to higher inflation and vice versa, guiding monetary policy decisions.

**Example:** During periods of low unemployment, wage pressures may increase, leading to higher inflation.

### Natural Rate of Unemployment

**Definition:** The rate of unemployment consistent with a stable rate of inflation, factoring in frictional and structural unemployment.

**Application:** The natural rate serves as a benchmark for assessing labour market efficiency and inflationary pressures.

**Example:** If Canada's natural rate of unemployment is 4%, it indicates the lowest sustainable unemployment rate without causing inflation to rise.

### Disinflation

**Definition:** A decrease in the rate of inflation, meaning prices are still rising but at a slower rate.

**Application:** Disinflation can signal a cooling economy, prompting central banks to adjust interest rates to maintain economic stability.

**Example:** If inflation falls from 3% to 2%, the economy is experiencing disinflation.

### Deflation

**Definition:** A sustained decrease in the general price level of goods and services.

**Application:** Deflation can lead to reduced consumer spending and economic stagnation, as falling prices may delay purchases.

**Example:** During the Great Depression, many countries experienced deflation, exacerbating economic challenges.

### Interest Rate Premium

**Definition:** The extra yield that investors demand to compensate for the risk of lending or investing.

**Application:** Interest rate premiums reflect the perceived risk of an investment, influencing borrowing costs and investment decisions.

**Example:** A corporate bond may offer a higher interest rate than a government bond due to the higher risk of default.

### Labour Force Participation Rate

**Definition:** The percentage of the working-age population that is part of the labour force, either employed or actively seeking employment.

**Application:** This rate provides insights into the availability of labour and potential economic growth.

**Example:** A participation rate of 65% indicates that 65% of the working-age population is engaged in the labour market.

### Frictional Unemployment

**Definition:** Short-term unemployment that arises from the process of matching workers with jobs.

**Application:** Frictional unemployment is a natural part of a dynamic economy, reflecting job transitions and new entrants into the labour market.

**Example:** Recent graduates searching for their first job contribute to frictional unemployment.

### Structural Unemployment

**Definition:** Long-term unemployment due to changes in the economy that create mismatches between skills and job requirements.

**Application:** Structural unemployment highlights the need for retraining and education to align workforce skills with market demands.

**Example:** Technological advancements may render certain jobs obsolete, leading to structural unemployment.

### Cyclical Unemployment

**Definition:** Unemployment correlated with the business cycle, rising during recessions and falling during expansions.

**Application:** Cyclical unemployment reflects economic fluctuations, guiding fiscal and monetary policy responses.

**Example:** During a recession, cyclical unemployment increases as businesses reduce hiring.

### Seasonal Unemployment

**Definition:** Unemployment related to the climate and seasons or certain times of the year when demand for a particular job typically decreases.

**Application:** Seasonal unemployment affects industries like agriculture and tourism, requiring workforce adjustments.

**Example:** Ski resorts experience seasonal unemployment during the summer months when demand for winter sports declines.

### Labour Market Indicators

**Definition:** Statistics that provide information on the performance and trends of the labour market, including unemployment rate and participation rate.

**Application:** These indicators help assess economic health and guide policy decisions to promote employment and growth.

**Example:** A rising participation rate may indicate increased confidence in the job market.

### Demand-Pull Inflation

**Definition:** Inflation that is caused by an increase in aggregate demand, leading to higher prices.

**Application:** Demand-pull inflation occurs in a growing economy, prompting central banks to manage demand through interest rate adjustments.

**Example:** A booming economy with high consumer spending can lead to demand-pull inflation.

### Cost-Push Inflation

**Definition:** Inflation caused by increases in the cost of production and supply, leading to higher prices for consumers.

**Application:** Cost-push inflation can result from rising wages or raw material costs, impacting pricing strategies and profit margins.

**Example:** An increase in oil prices can lead to cost-push inflation as transportation and production costs rise.

### Natural Unemployment Rate

**Definition:** The unemployment rate consistent with a stable rate of inflation, similar to the natural rate of unemployment.

**Application:** Understanding the natural unemployment rate helps policymakers balance employment and inflation goals.

**Example:** If the natural unemployment rate is 5%, efforts to reduce unemployment below this level may lead to inflationary pressures.
