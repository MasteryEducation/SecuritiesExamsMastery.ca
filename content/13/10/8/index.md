## Glossary for Chapter 10: Understanding Derivatives

In Chapter 10 of the CSC® Exam Prep Guide: Volume 1, we delve into the intricate world of derivatives, a crucial component of modern financial markets. This glossary serves as a comprehensive reference tool, providing clear and concise definitions of key terms introduced in the chapter. By familiarizing yourself with these terms, you can reinforce your understanding of derivatives and their applications within the Canadian financial landscape.

### American-style Option
An **American-style option** is a type of options contract that can be exercised at any time before its expiration date. This flexibility allows the holder to capitalize on favorable market movements. For example, if you hold an American-style call option on shares of a Canadian company like Shopify, you can exercise your option whenever the market price exceeds the strike price, maximizing potential gains.

### At-the-money
An option is considered **at-the-money** when its strike price is equal to the current market price of the underlying asset. This situation is pivotal in options trading as it often indicates a neutral position where the option has no intrinsic value but may still possess time value.

### Arbitrage
**Arbitrage** involves the simultaneous purchase and sale of an asset in different markets to profit from price discrepancies. For instance, if a stock is trading at a lower price on the Toronto Stock Exchange (TSX) compared to the New York Stock Exchange (NYSE), an arbitrageur could buy the stock on the TSX and sell it on the NYSE, capturing the price difference as profit.

### Assigned
In the context of options, being **assigned** means being obligated to fulfill the terms of the contract. For example, if you write a call option and the buyer exercises it, you are assigned and must sell the underlying asset at the agreed-upon strike price.

### Call Option
A **call option** is a financial contract that grants the holder the right, but not the obligation, to purchase an underlying asset at a specified price (strike price) within a certain timeframe. Investors use call options to speculate on rising asset prices or hedge against potential increases in costs.

### Covered Call
A **covered call** strategy involves writing a call option while simultaneously holding an equivalent amount of the underlying security. This approach generates additional income through option premiums while potentially limiting upside gains if the stock price rises significantly.

### Convertible Preferred Shares
**Convertible preferred shares** are a type of preferred stock that can be converted into a predetermined number of common shares. This feature provides investors with the potential for capital appreciation while maintaining the benefits of preferred shares, such as fixed dividends.

### Expiration Date
The **expiration date** is the date on which an option or futures contract becomes void, and the right to exercise it ceases to exist. Understanding expiration dates is crucial for managing options positions effectively and avoiding unwanted assignments.

### Forward Contract
A **forward contract** is a non-standardized, over-the-counter (OTC) derivative agreement to buy or sell an asset at a specified price on a future date. Unlike futures contracts, forwards are customizable and typically used for hedging purposes.

### Good Faith Deposit
A **good faith deposit** is an initial payment made to secure a forward contract. This deposit demonstrates the buyer's commitment to the agreement and helps mitigate counterparty risk.

### Hedging
**Hedging** involves using derivatives to offset potential losses in other investments. For example, a Canadian wheat farmer might use futures contracts to lock in a selling price for their crop, protecting against price fluctuations in the agricultural market.

### Intrinsic Value
The **intrinsic value** of an option is the value it would have if exercised immediately. For a call option, this is the difference between the underlying asset's market price and the strike price, provided the market price exceeds the strike price.

### Long Position
A **long position** in derivatives involves owning a contract, such as a call or put option, with the expectation that the underlying asset's value will increase or decrease favorably. Investors take long positions to benefit from anticipated market movements.

### Marking to Market
**Marking to market** is the daily process of settling gains and losses based on the current market price of a derivative. This practice ensures that margin accounts reflect the true value of open positions, reducing credit risk.

### Margin Account
A **margin account** is a brokerage account that allows investors to borrow funds to purchase securities. This leverage can amplify returns but also increases risk, making margin accounts suitable for experienced investors.

### Naked Call
A **naked call** involves writing a call option without owning the underlying security. This strategy exposes the writer to unlimited risk if the asset's price rises significantly, as they must purchase the asset at market prices to fulfill the contract.

### Option Premium
The **option premium** is the price paid by the buyer to the writer for the option contract. It reflects the option's intrinsic and time value and compensates the writer for assuming risk.

### Option Strategies
**Option strategies** involve combinations of buying and selling options to achieve specific investment goals, such as income generation, risk management, or speculation. Examples include straddles, strangles, and spreads.

### Open Interest
**Open interest** refers to the total number of outstanding derivative contracts that have not been settled. It provides insight into market activity and liquidity, helping traders gauge market sentiment.

### Put Option
A **put option** is a contract that gives the holder the right, but not the obligation, to sell an underlying asset at a specified price within a certain timeframe. Investors use put options to hedge against potential declines in asset prices or to speculate on bearish market movements.

### Short Position
A **short position** in derivatives involves selling a contract, such as a call or put option, with the expectation that the underlying asset's value will decrease or increase unfavorably. Short positions can be used for speculation or hedging purposes.

### Sweetener
A **sweetener** is an additional feature, such as warrants, included in a financial offering to make it more attractive to investors. Sweeteners can enhance the appeal of securities by providing potential upside benefits.

### Time Value
The **time value** of an option is the portion of its price attributable to the time remaining until expiration. It reflects the potential for the option to gain intrinsic value as market conditions change.

### Underlying Asset
The **underlying asset** is the financial instrument upon which a derivative's price is based. Common underlying assets include stocks, bonds, commodities, and currencies.
