## 10.4.1 Buying Call Options

In the world of derivatives, call options offer investors and corporations a versatile tool for both speculation and risk management. This section delves into the strategic use of call options, focusing on how they can be employed to speculate on price increases and hedge future purchases. We will explore practical examples, define key terms, and provide insights into the Canadian financial landscape.

### Speculating on Price Increase

Call options provide a unique opportunity for investors to speculate on the future price increase of a stock. By purchasing a call option, an investor gains the right, but not the obligation, to buy a specific stock at a predetermined price (the strike price) before a specified expiration date. This strategy can lead to higher percentage returns due to the leverage inherent in options trading.

#### Leverage and Potential Returns

Leverage is a powerful concept in finance, allowing investors to amplify their potential returns. When buying call options, the investor pays a premium, which is significantly lower than the cost of purchasing the underlying stock outright. This smaller initial investment can lead to substantial percentage gains if the stock price rises above the strike price.

For example, consider an investor who believes that the stock of a major Canadian bank, such as RBC, will increase in value. Instead of purchasing 100 shares at $100 each, totaling $10,000, the investor buys a call option with a strike price of $105 for a premium of $2 per share. The total cost of the option is $200 (100 shares x $2).

If the stock price rises to $115, the investor can exercise the option to buy the shares at $105, selling them at the market price of $115. The profit from this transaction is $800 [(115 - 105) x 100 shares - $200 premium], representing a 400% return on the initial $200 investment.

### Hedging Future Purchases

Corporations often use call options as a hedging tool to lock in maximum purchase prices for commodities or stocks they plan to acquire in the future. This strategy helps manage price volatility and ensures budget predictability.

#### Corporate Hedging Example

Imagine a Canadian manufacturing company that relies on copper as a raw material. Concerned about potential price increases, the company buys call options on copper futures. By doing so, the company secures the right to purchase copper at a predetermined price, mitigating the risk of rising costs.

If copper prices rise, the company can exercise the call options, purchasing copper at the lower strike price, thus protecting its profit margins. Conversely, if prices fall, the company can let the options expire, buying copper at the lower market price without incurring losses beyond the premium paid.

### Example of Speculation

Let's revisit the speculative scenario with RBC stock. Suppose the investor's analysis predicts a significant upward trend due to favorable economic conditions in Canada. The investor purchases a call option with a strike price of $105, expiring in three months, for a premium of $2 per share.

As anticipated, RBC's stock price climbs to $120 within two months. The investor exercises the option, buying 100 shares at $105 and selling them at the current market price of $120. The profit calculation is as follows:

- **Cost of Option:** $200 (100 shares x $2 premium)
- **Profit from Sale:** $1,500 [(120 - 105) x 100 shares]
- **Net Profit:** $1,300 ($1,500 - $200)

This example illustrates how call options can be a lucrative speculative tool, offering significant returns with limited initial investment.

### Example of Risk Management

Consider a scenario where the same investor buys a call option on RBC stock, anticipating a price increase. However, contrary to expectations, the stock price remains stagnant or decreases.

In this case, the investor's risk is limited to the premium paid for the option. If the stock price does not exceed the strike price by expiration, the option expires worthless, and the investor loses only the $200 premium. This limited downside risk makes call options an attractive risk management tool.

### Glossary

- **Leverage:** Using borrowed capital or derivatives to increase the potential return on an investment.
- **Premium:** The cost paid to acquire an option.

### Best Practices and Common Pitfalls

**Best Practices:**
- Conduct thorough research and analysis before purchasing call options.
- Use call options as part of a diversified investment strategy.
- Monitor market conditions and adjust positions as necessary.

**Common Pitfalls:**
- Overleveraging can lead to significant losses if the market moves against expectations.
- Ignoring expiration dates can result in missed opportunities or losses.
- Failing to account for transaction costs and taxes can impact overall returns.

### Conclusion

Buying call options is a versatile strategy that can enhance investment portfolios through speculation and risk management. By understanding the mechanics of call options and their applications in the Canadian financial market, investors and corporations can make informed decisions that align with their financial goals.
