## 10.4.4 Writing Put Options

Writing put options is a sophisticated strategy employed by investors to generate income or acquire stocks at a desired price. This section delves into the nuances of writing put options, focusing on the differences between cash-secured and naked puts, and provides practical examples to illustrate these concepts in the context of the Canadian financial market.

### Covered vs Naked Puts

Understanding the distinction between cash-secured puts and naked puts is crucial for managing risk and aligning with investment goals.

#### Cash-Secured Puts

A cash-secured put involves writing a put option while setting aside sufficient cash to purchase the underlying asset if the option is exercised. This strategy is often used by investors who are willing to buy the stock at a lower price and want to earn a premium in the meantime.

**Key Characteristics:**
- **Risk Management:** The risk is limited to the difference between the strike price and the premium received, as the investor has the cash ready to purchase the stock.
- **Income Generation:** Investors earn a premium for writing the option, which can enhance returns if the option expires worthless.
- **Market Outlook:** Typically used when the investor has a neutral to bullish outlook on the underlying asset.

#### Naked Puts

Writing a naked put involves selling a put option without having the funds set aside to purchase the underlying asset if exercised. This strategy carries a higher risk, as the investor may be forced to buy the stock at the strike price without having the necessary capital.

**Key Characteristics:**
- **Higher Risk:** The potential loss is significant if the stock price falls substantially below the strike price.
- **Speculative Strategy:** Often used by investors with a strong conviction that the stock price will not fall below the strike price.
- **Margin Requirements:** Typically requires a margin account, as brokers need assurance that the investor can cover potential losses.

### Cash-Secured Put Writing: A Practical Example

Consider an investor interested in purchasing shares of Royal Bank of Canada (RBC) but only at a lower price than the current market value. The investor decides to write a cash-secured put option with a strike price of $120, expiring in one month, and receives a premium of $3 per share.

**Scenario:**
- **Current Stock Price:** $125
- **Strike Price:** $120
- **Premium Received:** $3 per share
- **Cash Reserved:** $120 per share

If the stock price remains above $120, the option expires worthless, and the investor keeps the premium, earning a return without purchasing the stock. However, if the stock price falls below $120, the investor is obligated to buy the shares at the strike price. In this case, the effective purchase price is $117 ($120 strike price - $3 premium), which is lower than the initial market price.

### Naked Put Writing: A Risky Example

Now, consider an investor who writes a naked put option on Toronto-Dominion Bank (TD) with a strike price of $80, expiring in one month, and receives a premium of $2 per share. The investor does not have the funds set aside to purchase the shares if assigned.

**Scenario:**
- **Current Stock Price:** $85
- **Strike Price:** $80
- **Premium Received:** $2 per share

If the stock price drops to $70, the investor is obligated to purchase the shares at $80, resulting in a significant loss. The effective purchase price is $78 ($80 strike price - $2 premium), but the market value is only $70, leading to a loss of $8 per share.

### Glossary

- **Cash-Secured Put:** Writing a put option while having sufficient funds to purchase the underlying asset if exercised.
- **Naked Put:** Writing a put option without having the funds to buy the underlying asset, leading to significant risk.

### Best Practices and Common Pitfalls

**Best Practices:**
- **Risk Assessment:** Always assess your risk tolerance and ensure you have the necessary capital or margin to cover potential obligations.
- **Market Analysis:** Conduct thorough market analysis to make informed decisions about strike prices and expiration dates.
- **Diversification:** Use put writing as part of a diversified investment strategy to mitigate risks.

**Common Pitfalls:**
- **Overleveraging:** Avoid writing more options than you can cover, especially with naked puts.
- **Ignoring Market Trends:** Stay informed about market trends and economic indicators that could impact the underlying asset's price.
- **Lack of Exit Strategy:** Have a clear exit strategy in place to manage positions if the market moves against you.

### Conclusion

Writing put options can be a powerful tool for generating income and acquiring stocks at favorable prices. However, it requires a solid understanding of the risks involved, especially when writing naked puts. By employing strategies like cash-secured puts and adhering to best practices, investors can effectively manage risk and enhance their investment portfolios.

For further exploration, consider resources such as the Canadian Securities Administrators (CSA) guidelines on derivatives trading and the Investment Industry Regulatory Organization of Canada (IIROC) rules on options trading.
