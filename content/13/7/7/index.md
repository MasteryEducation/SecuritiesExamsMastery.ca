## Glossary for Chapter 7: Fixed-Income Securities

Understanding the intricacies of fixed-income securities, particularly bond pricing and trading, is crucial for any finance professional or student preparing for the CSC® Exam. This glossary provides comprehensive definitions and explanations of key terms encountered in Chapter 7, offering insights into their practical applications and relevance in the Canadian financial landscape.

### Present Value (PV)
**Definition:** The current value of future cash flows discounted at an appropriate interest rate.

**Application:** Present value is a fundamental concept in bond pricing, as it helps determine the fair value of a bond by discounting its future cash flows (coupon payments and principal repayment) to the present. For instance, when evaluating a Canadian government bond, the present value calculation assists in assessing whether the bond is priced attractively relative to its expected returns.

### Bond Price
**Definition:** The current market price of a bond, expressed as a percentage of its par value.

**Application:** Bond prices fluctuate based on interest rate changes, credit ratings, and market demand. For example, if the Bank of Canada raises interest rates, existing bond prices typically decrease, as newer issues offer higher yields. Understanding bond price movements is essential for making informed trading decisions.

### Yield to Maturity (YTM)
**Definition:** The total return expected on a bond if held until it matures, considering both interest payments and capital gain or loss.

**Application:** YTM is a critical measure for investors comparing bonds with different coupon rates and maturities. For instance, when choosing between corporate bonds issued by RBC and TD, investors can use YTM to assess which bond offers a better return relative to its risk.

### Current Yield
**Definition:** The annual income (interest or dividends) divided by the current price of the bond.

**Application:** Current yield provides a snapshot of a bond's income-generating potential. It is particularly useful for income-focused investors evaluating bonds within a fixed-income portfolio, such as those held in a Registered Retirement Savings Plan (RRSP).

### Duration
**Definition:** A measure of the sensitivity of a bond's price to changes in interest rates, expressed in years.

**Application:** Duration helps investors understand the interest rate risk associated with a bond. A bond with a longer duration is more sensitive to interest rate changes. For example, Canadian pension funds often consider duration when managing their bond portfolios to balance risk and return.

### Yield Curve
**Definition:** A graph that plots the yields of bonds with equal credit quality but different maturities, reflecting investor expectations about future interest rates.

**Application:** The yield curve is a valuable tool for predicting economic trends. An upward-sloping yield curve suggests economic growth, while an inverted curve may indicate a recession. Canadian investors use the yield curve to guide their bond investment strategies.

### Expectations Theory
**Definition:** A theory suggesting that long-term interest rates reflect expected future short-term rates.

**Application:** Expectations theory helps explain the shape of the yield curve. For instance, if investors anticipate rising interest rates, long-term bond yields may increase, steepening the yield curve.

### Liquidity Preference Theory
**Definition:** A theory stating that investors demand a premium for holding longer-term bonds due to higher risk and lower liquidity.

**Application:** This theory explains why long-term bonds typically offer higher yields than short-term bonds. Canadian investors may prefer short-term bonds during periods of economic uncertainty to maintain liquidity.

### Market Segmentation Theory
**Definition:** A theory that asserts different segments of the bond market are dominated by different types of investors, leading to distinct supply and demand dynamics.

**Application:** Market segmentation theory highlights the role of institutional investors, such as insurance companies and pension funds, in influencing bond market behavior. Understanding these dynamics helps investors anticipate market movements.

### Accrued Interest
**Definition:** The interest that has accumulated since the last coupon payment date, paid by the buyer to the seller in a bond transaction.

**Application:** Accrued interest is crucial in bond trading, ensuring that sellers receive compensation for interest earned before the sale. For example, when trading Canadian corporate bonds, accrued interest calculations ensure fair transactions.

### Discount Bond
**Definition:** A bond trading below its par value, resulting in a capital gain for the investor when held to maturity.

**Application:** Discount bonds offer opportunities for capital appreciation. Investors may purchase discount bonds during periods of rising interest rates, anticipating price increases as rates stabilize.

### Premium Bond
**Definition:** A bond trading above its par value, resulting in a capital loss for the investor when held to maturity.

**Application:** Premium bonds may appeal to investors seeking higher current income, despite the potential for capital loss. Understanding the trade-offs between income and capital preservation is essential for effective bond portfolio management.

### Strip Bond
**Definition:** A zero-coupon bond created by separating coupons from the principal, sold at a discount and redeemed at par.

**Application:** Strip bonds are popular among Canadian investors seeking predictable returns without periodic interest payments. They are often used in tax-advantaged accounts like Tax-Free Savings Accounts (TFSAs) to maximize tax efficiency.

### Convertible Bond
**Definition:** A bond that can be converted into a predetermined number of the issuing company’s equity shares.

**Application:** Convertible bonds offer the potential for capital appreciation through equity conversion. They are attractive to investors seeking a balance between fixed income and equity exposure, particularly in growth-oriented sectors.

### Credit Rating
**Definition:** An assessment by rating agencies of the creditworthiness of a bond issuer, indicating the risk level of default.

**Application:** Credit ratings guide investors in assessing the risk associated with different bonds. For example, Canadian investors may compare the credit ratings of provincial bonds to evaluate their relative safety.

### Sinking Fund
**Definition:** Funds set aside by an issuer to repay bonds gradually before they mature, reducing default risk.

**Application:** Sinking funds enhance bondholder security by ensuring gradual debt repayment. Investors may prefer bonds with sinking funds for their lower default risk.

### Callable Bond
**Definition:** A bond that allows the issuer to redeem it before its maturity date at a specified price.

**Application:** Callable bonds offer issuers flexibility to refinance debt at lower rates. Investors must consider the call risk, as bonds may be redeemed when interest rates decline, limiting potential returns.

### Trade Ticket
**Definition:** An electronic confirmation of a bond trade that includes details such as issuer, coupon rate, maturity, price, and yield.

**Application:** Trade tickets provide essential documentation for bond transactions, ensuring accuracy and compliance. They are vital for record-keeping and audit purposes in financial institutions.

### Yield Fluctuation
**Definition:** Changes in a bond’s yield due to shifts in interest rates or market conditions.

**Application:** Yield fluctuations impact bond valuations and investment strategies. Investors must monitor yield changes to adjust their portfolios and manage interest rate risk effectively.

### Interest Rate Risk
**Definition:** The risk that changes in interest rates will affect the price of a bond.

**Application:** Interest rate risk is a primary concern for bond investors. Strategies such as laddering bond maturities can help mitigate this risk by diversifying exposure across different interest rate environments.

### Reinvestment Risk
**Definition:** The risk that future coupon payments will have to be reinvested at lower interest rates than the bond's original yield.

**Application:** Reinvestment risk affects income-focused investors, particularly during declining interest rate periods. Strategies like investing in bonds with staggered maturities can help manage this risk.

### Liquidity
**Definition:** The ease with which a bond can be bought or sold in the market without significantly affecting its price.

**Application:** Liquidity is crucial for active bond traders and portfolio managers. Highly liquid bonds, such as those issued by the Canadian government, offer flexibility for timely trading and portfolio adjustments.

### Price Sensitivity
**Definition:** The degree to which a bond’s price responds to changes in interest rates.

**Application:** Price sensitivity, often measured by duration, informs investors about potential price volatility. Understanding this concept helps in constructing resilient bond portfolios.

### Bond Fund
**Definition:** An investment fund that pools money to invest in a diversified portfolio of bonds.

**Application:** Bond funds offer diversification and professional management, appealing to investors seeking exposure to fixed-income markets without selecting individual bonds. They are commonly used in retirement accounts for steady income generation.

### Fixed-Income Portfolio
**Definition:** A collection of fixed-income securities held by an investor to achieve specific financial goals.

**Application:** Fixed-income portfolios are tailored to meet various objectives, such as income generation, capital preservation, or risk management. Canadian investors often include a mix of government, corporate, and municipal bonds to achieve their desired outcomes.

By mastering these glossary terms, you will enhance your understanding of bond pricing and trading, enabling you to make informed investment decisions and effectively communicate complex financial concepts. Incorporate these terms into your financial vocabulary to improve your analysis and reporting skills, and apply them in real-world contexts to deepen your comprehension of fixed-income securities.
