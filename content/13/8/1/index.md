## 8.1 Introduction

Equity securities, encompassing both common and preferred shares, form the backbone of investment portfolios worldwide. They represent ownership in a company and offer investors the potential for capital appreciation and income. This section delves into the importance of equity securities, the role of stock market indexes, and the rights and characteristics of common and preferred shares. Understanding these concepts is crucial for anyone preparing for the Canadian Securities Course (CSC®) exam and for those looking to make informed investment decisions.

### Importance of Equity Securities

Equity securities, particularly common shares, are vital for investors seeking long-term growth and protection against inflation. Historically, equities have outperformed other asset classes, such as bonds, over extended periods. This superior performance is attributed to the potential for capital gains and dividends, which can compound over time.

#### Historical Returns: Stocks vs. Bonds

Historically, stocks have provided higher returns than bonds, albeit with greater volatility. For instance, over the past century, the average annual return for equities has been approximately 7-9%, compared to 3-5% for bonds. This difference underscores the importance of equities in a diversified portfolio, especially for investors with a long-term horizon.

Consider the following example: An investor who allocated $10,000 to a diversified portfolio of Canadian equities in 1990 would have seen their investment grow significantly more than if they had invested the same amount in Canadian government bonds. This growth is due to both capital appreciation and reinvested dividends, highlighting the power of equities in wealth accumulation.

### Role of Stock Market Indexes

Stock market indexes are essential tools for measuring the performance of the overall market or specific sectors. They serve as benchmarks for investors and fund managers, helping them assess the performance of their portfolios relative to the market.

#### Influential Stock Indexes

In Canada, the S&P/TSX Composite Index is the primary benchmark for the Canadian equity market. It includes a broad range of companies across various sectors, providing a comprehensive view of the market's performance.

Globally, the S&P 500 in the United States and the MSCI World Index are widely recognized. The S&P 500 tracks the performance of 500 large-cap U.S. companies, while the MSCI World Index includes companies from 23 developed countries, offering a global perspective.

Understanding these indexes is crucial for investors, as they provide insights into market trends and economic conditions. For example, a rising index may indicate economic growth and investor confidence, while a declining index could signal economic challenges or market corrections.

### Ownership and Rights in Common Shares

Owning common shares grants investors certain rights and privileges, making them an attractive investment option. These rights include voting on corporate matters, receiving dividends, and sharing in the company's growth.

#### Rights and Advantages

Common shareholders have the right to vote on important corporate decisions, such as electing the board of directors and approving mergers or acquisitions. This voting power allows shareholders to influence the company's direction and governance.

Additionally, common shareholders may receive dividends, which are distributions of the company's profits. While dividends are not guaranteed, many companies strive to provide consistent payouts to attract and retain investors.

#### Disadvantages

Despite their advantages, common shares also come with risks. Share prices can be volatile, and dividends may be reduced or eliminated during economic downturns. Furthermore, in the event of a company's liquidation, common shareholders are last in line to receive any remaining assets, after creditors and preferred shareholders.

### Common vs. Preferred Shares

While both common and preferred shares represent ownership in a company, they differ in terms of rights and income streams.

#### Preferred Shares

Preferred shares offer investors preferential treatment in dividends and asset distribution. They typically provide fixed dividend payments, making them similar to fixed-income securities like bonds. However, unlike bonds, preferred shares do not have a maturity date, and their dividends are not guaranteed.

Preferred shareholders have a higher claim on assets than common shareholders in the event of liquidation. However, they usually do not have voting rights, limiting their influence over corporate decisions.

### Relationship with Other Securities

Preferred shares share characteristics with both equities and fixed-income securities. Like bonds, they offer fixed income through dividends, but they also provide the potential for capital appreciation, similar to common shares.

#### Comparison with Bonds

Preferred shares and bonds both provide regular income, but they differ in risk and return profiles. Bonds are generally considered safer, as they have a fixed maturity date and interest payments are contractual obligations. Preferred shares, on the other hand, offer higher potential returns but come with greater risk, as dividends can be suspended.

### Glossary

- **Equity Securities:** Financial instruments representing ownership in a company.
- **Common Shares:** Standard ownership units in a company with voting rights.
- **Preferred Shares:** Ownership units with preferential treatment in dividends and asset distribution.

### Conclusion

Understanding the intricacies of equity securities, including common and preferred shares, is essential for any investor. These instruments offer unique opportunities and risks, making them a critical component of a well-rounded investment strategy. By grasping the concepts outlined in this section, readers will be better equipped to navigate the complexities of the stock market and make informed investment decisions.
