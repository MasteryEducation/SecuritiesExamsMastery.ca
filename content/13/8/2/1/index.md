## 8.2.1 Features, Benefits, and Risks of Common Share Ownership

Common shares represent an essential component of the equity securities market, offering investors a stake in a company's ownership. Understanding the features, benefits, and risks associated with common share ownership is crucial for making informed investment decisions. This section delves into the various aspects of common shares, providing insights into their potential as investment vehicles within the Canadian financial landscape.

### Ownership Stake

Owning common shares means holding a fractional ownership interest in a company. This ownership stake entitles shareholders to a portion of the company's profits and assets, proportional to the number of shares they own. Common shareholders are considered residual claimants, meaning they have a claim on the company's assets and earnings after all obligations, such as debts and preferred dividends, have been satisfied.

**Example:** Consider an investor who owns 1,000 shares of a company with 1,000,000 shares outstanding. This investor holds a 0.1% ownership stake in the company. If the company earns $1,000,000 in profits, the investor's share of the profits would be $1,000, assuming no dividends are paid out.

### Dividend Potential

Dividends are a key feature of common shares, providing shareholders with a source of income. Companies may distribute a portion of their earnings to shareholders in the form of dividends. The decision to declare dividends is influenced by several factors, including the company's profitability, cash flow, and future investment opportunities.

**Factors Influencing Dividend Declarations:**
- **Profitability:** Companies with stable and growing profits are more likely to pay dividends.
- **Cash Flow:** Adequate cash flow ensures the company can meet its operational needs while paying dividends.
- **Growth Opportunities:** Companies with significant growth prospects may retain earnings to fund expansion rather than pay dividends.

**Case Study:** Canadian banks, such as the Royal Bank of Canada (RBC) and Toronto-Dominion Bank (TD), are known for their consistent dividend payments, reflecting their stable earnings and strong cash flow positions.

### Voting Rights

Common shareholders typically have voting rights, allowing them to participate in corporate governance. Voting rights enable shareholders to influence key decisions, such as electing the board of directors, approving mergers or acquisitions, and making changes to the company's charter.

**Significance of Voting Privileges:**
- **Corporate Governance:** Shareholders can hold management accountable and influence the company's strategic direction.
- **Proxy Voting:** Shareholders can vote in person or by proxy, allowing them to participate even if they cannot attend meetings.

**Example:** In a scenario where a company proposes a merger, common shareholders may vote on whether to approve the transaction, impacting the company's future trajectory.

### Tax Considerations

In Canada, common share ownership offers favorable tax treatment for dividends and capital gains. Dividends received from Canadian corporations are eligible for the dividend tax credit, reducing the effective tax rate. Additionally, capital gains realized from the sale of shares are taxed at a lower rate compared to ordinary income.

**Tax Benefits:**
- **Dividend Tax Credit:** Reduces the tax burden on dividend income, making it more attractive for investors.
- **Capital Gains Taxation:** Only 50% of capital gains are taxable, providing a tax-efficient way to grow wealth.

**Example:** An investor receiving $1,000 in eligible dividends from a Canadian corporation may pay significantly less tax compared to $1,000 in interest income, thanks to the dividend tax credit.

### Marketability

Common shares are generally highly marketable, meaning they can be easily bought or sold on stock exchanges. The liquidity of common shares allows investors to enter or exit positions with relative ease, providing flexibility in managing their portfolios.

**Factors Affecting Marketability:**
- **Trading Volume:** High trading volumes indicate greater liquidity, making it easier to buy or sell shares.
- **Market Conditions:** Economic and market conditions can impact the ease of trading shares.

**Example:** Shares of large Canadian companies, such as BCE Inc. or Canadian National Railway, typically exhibit high liquidity due to their significant trading volumes on the Toronto Stock Exchange (TSX).

### Risk of Total Loss

While common shares offer potential rewards, they also carry the risk of total loss. In the event of a company's bankruptcy or liquidation, common shareholders are the last to be paid, after creditors and preferred shareholders. This means they may lose their entire investment if the company's assets are insufficient to cover its liabilities.

**Scenarios Leading to Total Loss:**
- **Bankruptcy:** If a company goes bankrupt, common shareholders may receive little to no compensation.
- **Poor Performance:** Companies with declining financial performance may see their share prices plummet, resulting in significant losses for shareholders.

**Example:** The collapse of Nortel Networks in the early 2000s serves as a reminder of the risks associated with common share ownership. Shareholders of Nortel lost their investments as the company filed for bankruptcy.

### Glossary

- **Limited Liability:** Protection against personal loss beyond the initial investment in a company. Shareholders are not personally liable for the company's debts.
- **Marketability:** The ease with which an asset can be bought or sold in the market. High marketability indicates greater liquidity and flexibility for investors.

### Conclusion

Common share ownership offers a blend of potential rewards and risks. Understanding the features, benefits, and risks associated with common shares is essential for making informed investment decisions. By considering factors such as ownership stake, dividend potential, voting rights, tax considerations, marketability, and the risk of total loss, investors can better navigate the complexities of the equity securities market.

For further exploration, consider reviewing resources from the Canadian Investment Regulatory Organization (CIRO) and the Toronto Stock Exchange (TSX). Additionally, books such as "The Intelligent Investor" by Benjamin Graham provide deeper insights into investment strategies and principles.
