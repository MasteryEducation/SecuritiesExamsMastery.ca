## 8.4.5 International Market Indexes and Averages

In today's interconnected global economy, understanding international market indexes and averages is crucial for investors seeking to diversify their portfolios and manage risk effectively. This section delves into the significance of these indexes, highlighting major international indexes, emerging market indexes, and the impact of currency fluctuations on Canadian investors.

### Global Index Overview

International market indexes serve as vital tools for investors by providing a snapshot of market performance across different regions. They offer insights into economic trends, investor sentiment, and the overall health of global markets. By tracking these indexes, Canadian investors can gain a broader perspective on international economic conditions, helping them make informed investment decisions.

### Major International Indexes

#### Nikkei Stock Average

The Nikkei Stock Average, commonly referred to as the Nikkei 225, is Japan's leading stock index. It represents 225 of the largest companies listed on the Tokyo Stock Exchange. The Nikkei is a price-weighted index, meaning that stocks with higher prices have a greater impact on the index's movement. This index is a key indicator of the Japanese economy and is closely watched by investors worldwide.

**Example:** Consider a Canadian investor interested in Japanese technology companies. By monitoring the Nikkei 225, they can assess the performance of major tech firms like Sony and Panasonic, which are included in the index.

#### FTSE 100 Index

The FTSE 100 Index is the UK's premier stock index, comprising the 100 largest companies listed on the London Stock Exchange by market capitalization. It is a market-capitalization-weighted index, reflecting the performance of major British companies. The FTSE 100 is a barometer of the UK economy and is widely used by investors to gauge market trends.

**Example:** A Canadian pension fund looking to diversify its holdings might consider investing in the FTSE 100 to gain exposure to sectors like finance and energy, represented by companies such as HSBC and BP.

#### DAX Performance

Germany's primary stock index, the DAX, includes 30 major blue-chip companies trading on the Frankfurt Stock Exchange. It is a performance index, meaning it accounts for dividends reinvested, providing a comprehensive view of total returns. The DAX is a critical measure of the German economy, Europe's largest.

**Example:** A Canadian investor interested in the automotive industry might track the DAX to follow companies like Volkswagen and BMW, which are significant components of the index.

#### CAC 40 Index

The CAC 40 Index is France's key stock index, consisting of 40 of the largest companies listed on the Euronext Paris. It is calculated based on market capitalization and serves as a benchmark for the French economy. The CAC 40 is essential for investors seeking exposure to French industries such as luxury goods and pharmaceuticals.

**Example:** Canadian investors looking to invest in luxury brands might consider the CAC 40, which includes companies like LVMH and L'Oréal.

#### Swiss Market Index (SMI)

The Swiss Market Index (SMI) comprises the 20 largest and most liquid Swiss stocks. It is a free-float market-capitalization-weighted index, reflecting the performance of Switzerland's largest companies. The SMI is crucial for investors interested in the Swiss economy and sectors like pharmaceuticals and banking.

**Example:** A Canadian investor focusing on healthcare might track the SMI to follow companies like Novartis and Roche, which are significant players in the index.

### Emerging Market Indexes

Emerging market indexes represent rapidly growing economies such as China, India, and Mexico. These indexes are gaining prominence as investors seek opportunities in markets with high growth potential. Emerging markets offer diversification benefits and the potential for higher returns, albeit with increased risk.

**Example:** The MSCI Emerging Markets Index includes companies from countries like China and India. Canadian investors can use this index to gain exposure to emerging market growth while managing risk through diversification.

### Impact of Currency Fluctuations

Currency fluctuations can significantly impact the performance and returns of international indexes for Canadian investors. Exchange rate movements affect the value of foreign investments when converted back to Canadian dollars. Understanding currency risk is essential for managing international investments effectively.

**Example:** A Canadian investor holding European stocks might experience gains in the local currency, but if the euro weakens against the Canadian dollar, the returns could be diminished when converted back to CAD.

### Investment Diversification

International indexes play a crucial role in achieving portfolio diversification. By investing in a mix of domestic and international assets, Canadian investors can spread risk and potentially enhance returns. Diversification helps mitigate the impact of regional economic downturns and provides exposure to global growth opportunities.

**Example:** A well-diversified portfolio might include Canadian equities, US stocks, and international indexes like the FTSE 100 and Nikkei 225, balancing risk and return across different markets.

### Glossary

- **Emerging Markets:** Nations with social or business activity in the process of rapid growth and industrialization.
- **Currency Risk:** The potential for investment losses due to changes in exchange rates.

### Conclusion

Understanding international market indexes and averages is essential for Canadian investors seeking to diversify their portfolios and manage global risk. By tracking major international indexes and considering emerging market opportunities, investors can gain valuable insights into global economic trends and make informed investment decisions. Additionally, being aware of currency fluctuations and their impact on returns is crucial for managing international investments effectively.
