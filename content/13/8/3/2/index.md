## 8.3.2 Types of Preferred Shares

Preferred shares are a unique class of equity securities that offer distinct advantages and features compared to common shares. They are often used by companies to raise capital while providing investors with a fixed income stream. In this section, we will explore the various types of preferred shares available in the Canadian market, each with its own characteristics and benefits.

### Cumulative vs. Non-Cumulative Preferred Shares

**Cumulative Preferred Shares** are a type of preferred stock where any unpaid dividends accumulate and must be paid out to shareholders before any dividends can be distributed to common shareholders. This feature provides a safety net for investors, ensuring that they receive their due dividends even if the company faces financial difficulties.

**Non-Cumulative Preferred Shares**, on the other hand, do not offer this benefit. If the company decides not to pay dividends in a given year, shareholders of non-cumulative preferred shares do not have the right to claim those unpaid dividends in the future. This makes non-cumulative preferreds riskier compared to their cumulative counterparts.

#### Example:
Consider a Canadian company that issues cumulative preferred shares with a dividend rate of 5%. If the company skips dividend payments for two years due to financial constraints, it must pay all accumulated dividends before any dividends are paid to common shareholders once it resumes payments.

### Callable vs. Non-Callable Preferred Shares

**Callable Preferred Shares** give the issuing company the right to redeem the shares at a predetermined price after a specified date. This feature allows companies to manage their capital structure by repurchasing shares when interest rates decline or when they have excess cash. However, this can be disadvantageous for investors if the shares are called away during favorable market conditions.

**Non-Callable Preferred Shares** do not have this feature, providing investors with more certainty regarding the longevity of their investment.

#### Example:
A Canadian bank issues callable preferred shares with a call price of $25 per share. If interest rates decrease, the bank might choose to call the shares and reissue new ones at a lower dividend rate, thus reducing its cost of capital.

### Convertible Preferred Shares

**Convertible Preferred Shares** offer investors the option to convert their preferred shares into a predetermined number of common shares. This feature provides the potential for capital appreciation if the company's common stock performs well. Convertible preferreds are attractive to investors who seek both income and the opportunity for equity growth.

#### Example:
A Canadian technology firm issues convertible preferred shares that can be converted into common shares at a ratio of 1:5. If the company's stock price rises significantly, investors may choose to convert their preferred shares to capitalize on the capital gains.

### Retractable Preferred Shares

**Retractable Preferred Shares** provide shareholders with the right to sell their shares back to the issuing company at a specified price and date. This feature offers a degree of liquidity and protection against market volatility, as investors can exit their investment under predetermined conditions.

#### Example:
A Canadian utility company issues retractable preferred shares with a retraction price of $26 per share, exercisable in five years. Investors can choose to retract their shares at that time, providing a safety net against market downturns.

### Floating-Rate Preferred Shares

**Floating-Rate Preferred Shares** have dividends that fluctuate based on changes in interest rates. This type of preferred share is beneficial in a rising interest rate environment, as the dividend payments increase with the rates, providing a hedge against inflation.

#### Example:
A Canadian financial institution issues floating-rate preferred shares with dividends tied to the Bank of Canada's prime rate plus 2%. As interest rates rise, so do the dividends, offering investors protection against inflation.

### Foreign-Pay Preferred Shares

**Foreign-Pay Preferred Shares** pay dividends in a foreign currency, exposing investors to currency risk. These shares can be attractive to investors seeking diversification and exposure to foreign markets, but they must consider the potential impact of exchange rate fluctuations on their returns.

#### Example:
A Canadian multinational corporation issues foreign-pay preferred shares with dividends paid in U.S. dollars. Canadian investors holding these shares must consider the impact of CAD/USD exchange rate movements on their dividend income.

### Participating Preferred Shares

**Participating Preferred Shares** offer shareholders the opportunity to receive additional dividends beyond the fixed rate if the company performs well. This feature allows investors to benefit from the company's success while still enjoying the stability of fixed dividend payments.

#### Example:
A Canadian manufacturing company issues participating preferred shares with a fixed dividend of 4% and an additional dividend contingent on profits exceeding a certain threshold. If the company achieves record profits, participating shareholders receive a bonus dividend.

### Deferred Preferred Shares

**Deferred Preferred Shares** defer dividend payments until a specified future date. This type of preferred share can be advantageous for companies looking to conserve cash in the short term while still providing investors with a return on their investment in the future.

#### Example:
A Canadian startup issues deferred preferred shares with dividends payable starting five years from the issue date. This allows the company to reinvest its earnings into growth initiatives while ensuring investors receive dividends in the future.

### Glossary

- **Floating-Rate Dividend:** A dividend that varies with changes in interest rates, providing a hedge against inflation.
- **Participating Dividend:** An additional dividend beyond the fixed payment, contingent on company performance.

### Conclusion

Understanding the various types of preferred shares is crucial for investors looking to diversify their portfolios and manage risk. Each type offers unique features and benefits, making them suitable for different investment strategies and market conditions. By carefully considering the characteristics of each type, investors can make informed decisions that align with their financial goals and risk tolerance.
