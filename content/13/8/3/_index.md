## 8.3 Preferred Shares

Preferred shares are a unique class of equity securities that provide investors with a fixed-income stream, combining elements of both stocks and bonds. They occupy a middle ground in the capital structure of a company, offering distinct features and benefits that appeal to specific types of investors. In this section, we will delve into the characteristics of preferred shares, compare them with common shares, explore various types of preferred shares, and discuss their role in investment strategies.

### Features and Benefits of Preferred Shares

Preferred shares are equity securities that grant holders a higher claim on dividends and assets than common shares. They are often considered a hybrid between stocks and bonds due to their fixed dividend payments and potential for capital appreciation.

#### Fixed-Income Stream

One of the primary features of preferred shares is their ability to provide a fixed-income stream. Unlike common shares, which offer variable dividends based on the company's profitability, preferred shares typically pay a fixed dividend. This makes them attractive to income-oriented investors seeking stable returns.

#### Comparison with Common Shares

Preferred shares differ from common shares in several key aspects:

- **Dividend Obligations:** Preferred shareholders receive dividends before common shareholders. This priority makes preferred shares a more reliable source of income.
- **Asset Claims:** In the event of liquidation, preferred shareholders have a higher claim on the company's assets than common shareholders, although they rank below debt holders.

### Types of Preferred Shares

Preferred shares come in various forms, each with unique features that cater to different investor needs. Understanding these types is crucial for making informed investment decisions.

#### Cumulative Preferred Shares

Cumulative preferred shares accumulate unpaid dividends. If a company is unable to pay dividends in a given year, these unpaid dividends are carried forward and must be paid before any dividends can be distributed to common shareholders. This feature provides an added layer of security for investors.

#### Non-Cumulative Preferred Shares

Non-cumulative preferred shares do not accumulate unpaid dividends. If a company skips a dividend payment, investors do not have the right to claim these missed payments in the future. This type of preferred share is riskier compared to cumulative shares.

#### Callable Preferred Shares

Callable preferred shares can be redeemed by the issuer at a predetermined date and price. This feature allows companies to repurchase shares when it is financially advantageous, such as when interest rates decline. Investors should be aware of the call risk associated with these shares.

#### Convertible Preferred Shares

Convertible preferred shares offer the option to convert into a specified number of common shares. This feature provides investors with the potential for capital appreciation if the company's common stock performs well.

#### Participating Preferred Shares

Participating preferred shares allow investors to receive additional dividends based on certain conditions, such as the company's profitability. This feature enables investors to benefit from the company's success beyond the fixed dividend.

#### Retractable Preferred Shares

Retractable preferred shares give investors the right to sell the shares back to the issuer at a predetermined price on a specified date. This feature provides an exit strategy for investors, offering liquidity and capital protection.

### Claim to Assets

In the event of a company's liquidation, preferred shareholders have a higher claim on the company's assets than common shareholders. This priority makes preferred shares a safer investment compared to common shares, although they still rank below debt holders. Understanding the hierarchy of claims is essential for assessing the risk associated with preferred shares.

### Dividend Preferences

Preferred dividends are prioritized over common dividends, providing a more stable income stream for investors. In the case of cumulative preferred shares, any unpaid dividends must be settled before common shareholders receive any payments. This preference ensures that preferred shareholders are compensated even during challenging financial periods.

### Issuance Reasons

Companies may choose to issue preferred shares for several reasons:

- **Capital Structure Optimization:** Preferred shares allow companies to raise capital without increasing debt levels, maintaining a balanced capital structure.
- **Tax Advantages:** In Canada, dividends from preferred shares are often taxed at a lower rate than interest income, making them an attractive option for both issuers and investors.
- **Flexibility:** Issuing preferred shares provides companies with financial flexibility, as they can structure the terms to suit their needs, such as including call or conversion features.

### Investor Perspectives

Preferred shares are particularly appealing to income-oriented investors and institutions due to their income stability and tax advantages. Canadian investors benefit from the dividend tax credit, which reduces the effective tax rate on dividend income compared to interest income from bonds.

#### Case Study: Canadian Pension Funds

Canadian pension funds often include preferred shares in their portfolios to achieve a balance between income generation and capital preservation. For example, a pension fund might invest in a mix of cumulative and convertible preferred shares to secure stable income while retaining the potential for capital gains.

### Glossary

- **Cumulative Preferred Shares:** Preferred shares that accumulate unpaid dividends, which must be paid before any dividends to common shareholders.
- **Callable Preferred Shares:** Preferred shares that the issuer can redeem at a predetermined date and price.
- **Convertible Preferred Shares:** Preferred shares that can be converted into a specified number of common shares.

### Practical Example

Consider a Canadian investor holding a portfolio of preferred shares from major banks like RBC and TD. By analyzing the dividend yield, call features, and conversion options, the investor can optimize their portfolio for income stability and potential growth. This hands-on approach allows investors to tailor their strategies to their financial goals and risk tolerance.

### Conclusion

Preferred shares offer a unique blend of income stability and potential for capital appreciation, making them a valuable component of a diversified investment portfolio. By understanding the features, types, and benefits of preferred shares, investors can make informed decisions that align with their financial objectives. As you explore the Canadian financial landscape, consider how preferred shares can enhance your investment strategy.
