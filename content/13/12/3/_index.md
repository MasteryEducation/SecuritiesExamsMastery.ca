## 12.3 Bringing Securities to the Market

Bringing securities to the market is a critical process that involves several key steps and regulatory requirements. This section will delve into the prospectus system, after-market stabilization techniques, and scenarios where prospectus requirements can be waived. Understanding these elements is essential for anyone involved in the issuance of securities in Canada.

### Prospectus System

The prospectus system is a fundamental component of the securities issuance process. It ensures that investors have access to essential information about the securities being offered, thereby promoting transparency and investor protection.

#### Preliminary and Final Prospectuses

A prospectus is a legal document that provides details about a securities offering to the public. In Canada, the process typically involves two stages:

1. **Preliminary Prospectus (Red Herring):** This document is filed with the securities regulatory authorities and provides initial information about the offering. It is used to gauge investor interest and does not include the final price or number of shares to be issued. The preliminary prospectus must include:
   - A description of the company's business and financial condition.
   - Information about the securities being offered.
   - Details about the management team and their compensation.
   - Any material risks associated with the investment.

2. **Final Prospectus:** After receiving feedback from regulators and potential investors, the issuer files a final prospectus. This document includes all the necessary information for investors to make an informed decision, including the final offering price and the number of shares. The final prospectus must be approved by the securities regulatory authorities before the securities can be sold to the public.

#### The Passport System

The Passport System is a regulatory framework designed to streamline the prospectus filing process across multiple Canadian jurisdictions. It allows issuers to file a single prospectus with their principal regulator, which then grants access to the capital markets in all participating jurisdictions. This system simplifies the process, reduces costs, and accelerates the time to market for issuers.

### After-Market Stabilization

After-market stabilization is crucial for maintaining the stability of security prices following an initial public offering (IPO). Several techniques are employed to achieve this:

#### Greenshoe Option

The Greenshoe option, also known as an over-allotment option, allows underwriters to purchase additional shares (up to 15% of the original offering size) at the offering price. This option helps stabilize the market price by providing additional shares to meet excess demand or by buying back shares to support the price if it falls below the offering price.

#### Penalty Bids

Penalty bids are used to discourage the flipping of shares by investors immediately after the IPO. Underwriters impose a penalty on brokers whose clients sell their shares within a short period after the offering. This helps maintain price stability by encouraging investors to hold onto their shares longer.

#### Stabilizing Bids

Stabilizing bids involve the underwriter placing bids to purchase shares at or below the offering price to prevent the market price from falling. This technique provides a temporary support level for the stock, ensuring a smoother transition to market trading.

### Prospectus Exemptions

In certain scenarios, securities can be issued without a prospectus. These exemptions are designed to facilitate capital raising while maintaining investor protection. Common exemptions include:

#### Private Placements

Private placements involve the sale of securities to a select group of investors, such as institutional investors or accredited individuals, without a public offering. This method is often used by companies seeking to raise capital quickly and with fewer regulatory requirements.

#### Small Offerings

Certain small offerings may qualify for prospectus exemptions if they meet specific criteria, such as a limited number of investors or a maximum aggregate investment amount. These exemptions reduce the regulatory burden on smaller issuers while still providing some level of investor protection.

### Glossary

- **Prospectus:** A legal document providing details about a securities offering to the public.
- **Greenshoe Option:** An option allowing underwriters to purchase additional shares to stabilize the market price after the initial offering.
- **Passport System:** A framework allowing issuers to file a single prospectus for multiple jurisdictions, simplifying the offering process.

### Practical Example: A Canadian IPO

Consider a Canadian technology company planning to go public. The company files a preliminary prospectus with the Ontario Securities Commission (OSC), outlining its business model, financials, and risks. After receiving feedback, the company files a final prospectus, setting the share price at $15 and offering 10 million shares.

To ensure price stability, the underwriters include a Greenshoe option, allowing them to purchase an additional 1.5 million shares if demand exceeds expectations. They also implement penalty bids to discourage early selling by investors.

By utilizing the Passport System, the company gains access to investors across Canada, reducing the complexity and cost of the IPO process.

### Best Practices and Challenges

- **Best Practices:** Ensure comprehensive and accurate disclosure in the prospectus to build investor trust. Utilize the Passport System to streamline the filing process and reduce costs.
- **Common Challenges:** Navigating regulatory requirements and maintaining price stability post-IPO can be challenging. Employing experienced underwriters and legal advisors can mitigate these risks.

### Conclusion

Bringing securities to the market involves a complex interplay of regulatory requirements, market dynamics, and strategic decisions. By understanding the prospectus system, after-market stabilization techniques, and prospectus exemptions, issuers can effectively navigate the Canadian securities landscape.
