## Glossary for Chapter 12: Financing and Listing Securities

In Chapter 12 of the CSC® Exam Prep Guide: Volume 1, we delve into the intricate processes of financing and listing securities. This glossary serves as a comprehensive reference tool, providing clear definitions and explanations of key terms introduced in the chapter. Use this glossary to reinforce your understanding and facilitate quick reference during practice and assessments.

### After-Market Stabilization

**Definition:** After-market stabilization refers to the actions taken by underwriters to maintain the price stability of a new securities issue in the secondary market. This process is crucial in preventing the price of a newly issued security from falling below the offering price shortly after the initial public offering (IPO).

**Example:** Consider a Canadian company that has just gone public. The underwriters may purchase shares in the open market to support the stock price if it begins to decline post-IPO. This practice helps build investor confidence and ensures a smooth transition from the primary to the secondary market.

### Best Efforts Underwriting

**Definition:** Best efforts underwriting is an agreement where the underwriter commits to selling as much of the offering as possible without guaranteeing the sale of the entire issue. Unlike firm commitment underwriting, the underwriter does not assume the full risk of the unsold portion.

**Example:** A small tech startup in Toronto may opt for a best efforts underwriting agreement, allowing the underwriter to sell shares to investors without the obligation to purchase any unsold shares themselves.

### Blue Skyed

**Definition:** "Blue Skyed" is a term used to confirm that a securities offering has met all regulatory requirements and is approved for public distribution. This term originates from "Blue Sky Laws," which are state securities laws in the United States designed to protect investors from fraud.

**Example:** Before a new mutual fund can be offered to investors in Canada, it must be "blue skyed," ensuring compliance with provincial securities regulations.

### Delisting

**Definition:** Delisting is the process by which a security is removed from trading on an exchange due to non-compliance with listing requirements or other factors. Delisting can occur voluntarily or involuntarily.

**Example:** A company listed on the Toronto Stock Exchange (TSX) may be delisted if it fails to meet the minimum market capitalization requirements, impacting its ability to raise capital through public markets.

### Firm Commitment Underwriting

**Definition:** Firm commitment underwriting is an agreement where the underwriter buys the entire offering and resells it to the public, assuming full risk. The underwriter guarantees the sale of all the offered shares, providing the issuer with immediate funds.

**Example:** A major Canadian bank like RBC may enter into a firm commitment underwriting agreement for a large corporation's bond issuance, ensuring the company receives the full proceeds from the sale.

### Greenshoe Option

**Definition:** The Greenshoe Option is an option for underwriters to buy additional shares from the issuer to stabilize the price after the offering. This option allows underwriters to cover over-allotments and manage demand fluctuations.

**Example:** During an IPO, if demand exceeds expectations, the underwriters may exercise the Greenshoe Option to purchase additional shares, ensuring price stability and meeting investor demand.

### Initial Public Offering (IPO)

**Definition:** An Initial Public Offering (IPO) is the first sale of a company’s shares to the public, resulting in the company’s listing on a stock exchange. This process transforms a private company into a publicly traded entity.

**Example:** Shopify's IPO on the New York Stock Exchange and TSX marked its transition from a private Canadian tech company to a publicly traded one, raising significant capital for expansion.

### Issuer

**Definition:** The issuer is the entity that offers securities for sale to investors. Issuers can be corporations, governments, or other organizations seeking to raise capital.

**Example:** The Government of Canada acts as an issuer when it sells government bonds to finance public projects and manage national debt.

### Listing Agreement

**Definition:** A listing agreement is a formal contract between a company and a stock exchange outlining the terms and conditions for listing securities. This agreement ensures compliance with exchange rules and regulations.

**Example:** A Canadian mining company entering into a listing agreement with the TSX must adhere to specific reporting and governance standards to maintain its listing status.

### Market Integrity

**Definition:** Market integrity refers to the fairness and efficiency of the financial markets, ensuring all participants have equal access to information and opportunities. It is a cornerstone of investor confidence and market stability.

**Example:** Regulatory bodies like the Canadian Securities Administrators (CSA) work to uphold market integrity by enforcing transparency and preventing insider trading.

### Passport System

**Definition:** The Passport System is a streamlined prospectus filing process allowing issuers to list securities across multiple jurisdictions with a single prospectus. This system simplifies regulatory compliance for cross-border offerings within Canada.

**Example:** A company based in Quebec can use the Passport System to file a single prospectus and offer securities in other provinces like Ontario and British Columbia, reducing administrative burdens.

### Private Placement

**Definition:** Private placement is the sale of securities to a select group of investors without a public offering, often bypassing the prospectus requirement. This method is typically used by companies seeking to raise capital quickly and discreetly.

**Example:** A Vancouver-based biotech firm may conduct a private placement to secure funding from institutional investors without the need for a public offering.

### Prospectus

**Definition:** A prospectus is a legal document providing detailed information about a securities offering to potential investors. It includes financial statements, risk factors, and details about the issuer's business operations.

**Example:** Before investing in a new mutual fund, Canadian investors can review the prospectus to understand the fund's objectives, fees, and risks.

### Secondary Offering

**Definition:** A secondary offering is the resale of securities by existing shareholders rather than the issuing corporation. This process does not raise new capital for the company but allows shareholders to liquidate their holdings.

**Example:** A major shareholder of a Canadian energy company may conduct a secondary offering to sell a portion of their shares to institutional investors.

### Syndicate

**Definition:** A syndicate is a group of investment dealers working together to underwrite and distribute a securities offering. Syndicates pool resources and share risks to manage large offerings effectively.

**Example:** For a large IPO, a syndicate of Canadian and international banks may collaborate to ensure the successful distribution of shares to a broad investor base.

### Tender Offer

**Definition:** A tender offer is a public offer for a large number of shares, typically controlling interest, at a premium to the market price. This strategy is often used in mergers and acquisitions.

**Example:** A Canadian conglomerate may launch a tender offer to acquire a controlling stake in a competitor, offering shareholders a premium over the current market price.

### Underwriter

**Definition:** An underwriter is an investment dealer responsible for managing the issuance and distribution of new securities. Underwriters assess risk, set offering prices, and facilitate the sale of securities to investors.

**Example:** TD Securities acts as an underwriter for various Canadian corporate bond issuances, ensuring efficient market entry and investor engagement.

### Voting Rights

**Definition:** Voting rights refer to the ability of shareholders to vote on corporate matters, such as electing the board of directors or approving mergers. These rights are typically proportional to the number of shares owned.

**Example:** Shareholders of a Canadian telecommunications company may exercise their voting rights at the annual general meeting to influence corporate governance decisions.

This glossary provides a foundational understanding of key terms related to financing and listing securities, essential for navigating the Canadian securities market. By familiarizing yourself with these concepts, you can enhance your comprehension and application of financial strategies within the Canadian context.
