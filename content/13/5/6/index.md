## Glossary for Chapter 5: Economic Policy

Understanding economic policy is crucial for anyone involved in the financial services industry, particularly in the Canadian context. This glossary provides definitions and explanations of key terms related to economic policy, helping you to grasp the concepts discussed in Chapter 5 of the CSC® Exam Prep Guide: Volume 1. By familiarizing yourself with these terms, you can enhance your comprehension of fiscal and monetary strategies and their implications for the economy.

### Balanced Budget

A **Balanced Budget** occurs when a government's total expenditures are equal to its total revenues. This concept is vital for maintaining fiscal discipline and ensuring that government spending does not exceed its income, which can lead to increased national debt. In Canada, achieving a balanced budget is often a goal for both federal and provincial governments, as it reflects sound financial management and economic stability.

**Example:** In 2019, the Government of Canada aimed to balance its budget by reducing unnecessary expenditures and increasing revenue through strategic taxation policies.

### Monetary Policy

**Monetary Policy** refers to the actions undertaken by a central bank, such as the Bank of Canada, to manage the money supply and interest rates to achieve macroeconomic objectives like controlling inflation, consumption, growth, and liquidity. The central bank uses various tools, including open market operations, the bank rate, and reserve requirements, to influence economic activity.

**Example:** During the COVID-19 pandemic, the Bank of Canada implemented an accommodative monetary policy by lowering interest rates to stimulate economic activity.

### Bank Rate

The **Bank Rate** is the interest rate at which the Bank of Canada lends money to chartered banks. This rate influences the overall interest rates in the economy, affecting borrowing costs for consumers and businesses. Changes in the bank rate can signal the central bank's monetary policy stance.

**Example:** If the Bank of Canada increases the bank rate, it typically leads to higher interest rates for loans and mortgages, which can slow down borrowing and spending.

### National Debt

**National Debt** is the total amount of money that a government owes to its creditors. It accumulates over time when a government runs budget deficits, borrowing funds to cover the shortfall. Managing national debt is crucial for maintaining economic stability and investor confidence.

**Example:** Canada's national debt increased significantly during the pandemic as the government borrowed to fund emergency relief programs.

### Basis Points

**Basis Points** are a unit of measure equal to 1/100th of a percentage point. They are commonly used to express changes in interest rates or other financial percentages. Understanding basis points is essential for interpreting interest rate movements and their implications for financial markets.

**Example:** If the Bank of Canada raises the interest rate by 25 basis points, it means the rate has increased by 0.25%.

### Overnight Repo

An **Overnight Repo** (repurchase agreement) is a short-term loan where securities are sold with an agreement to repurchase them the next day. It is a tool used by central banks to manage liquidity in the banking system.

**Example:** The Bank of Canada might use overnight repos to inject liquidity into the financial system, ensuring that banks have sufficient funds to meet their short-term obligations.

### Overnight Reverse Repo

An **Overnight Reverse Repo** is the opposite of an overnight repo. It involves buying securities with an agreement to sell them back the next day. This tool is used to withdraw excess liquidity from the banking system.

**Example:** To curb inflationary pressures, the Bank of Canada might conduct overnight reverse repos to reduce the money supply.

### Drawdown

**Drawdown** refers to the Bank of Canada's method of removing funds from the banking system to increase interest rates. This action is part of monetary policy operations to control inflation and stabilize the economy.

**Example:** During periods of economic overheating, the Bank of Canada may implement drawdowns to cool down excessive spending and borrowing.

### Redeposit

**Redeposit** is the process by which the Bank of Canada adds funds to the banking system to decrease interest rates. This action is used to stimulate economic activity by making borrowing cheaper.

**Example:** In a recession, the Bank of Canada might redeposit funds to encourage lending and investment.

### Fiscal Agent

A **Fiscal Agent** is an institution appointed to manage financial matters on behalf of another entity, such as the government. In Canada, the Bank of Canada often acts as a fiscal agent for the federal government, handling debt issuance and management.

**Example:** The Bank of Canada, as a fiscal agent, manages the issuance of government bonds to finance public spending.

### Separation of Powers

**Separation of Powers** is the division of responsibilities among different branches of government to prevent the concentration of power. This principle ensures checks and balances within the government, promoting accountability and transparency.

**Example:** In Canada, the separation of powers is evident in the distinct roles of the executive, legislative, and judicial branches.

### Policy Lag

**Policy Lag** refers to the time delay between recognizing an economic problem and seeing the effects of policy interventions. It includes recognition lag, decision lag, and implementation lag, affecting the effectiveness of economic policies.

**Example:** During a recession, there may be a policy lag before fiscal stimulus measures lead to increased economic activity.

### Economy-Wide

**Economy-Wide** refers to policies or effects that impact the entire economy rather than specific sectors or regions. Understanding economy-wide implications is crucial for assessing the overall effectiveness of economic policies.

**Example:** A change in the national interest rate has economy-wide effects, influencing borrowing costs across all sectors.

### Inflation-Control Targets

**Inflation-Control Targets** are specific goals set by the central bank to maintain inflation within a desired range. In Canada, the Bank of Canada aims to keep inflation around 2%, providing a stable environment for economic growth.

**Example:** The Bank of Canada's inflation-control target guides its monetary policy decisions, ensuring price stability.
