## Glossary for Chapter 6: Fixed-Income Securities

Understanding fixed-income securities is crucial for any finance professional, especially those preparing for the CSC® exam. This glossary provides clear definitions and explanations of key terms related to fixed-income securities, helping you to grasp the concepts discussed in Chapter 6. By familiarizing yourself with these terms, you can enhance your comprehension, improve your financial vocabulary, and apply these concepts in real-world scenarios.

### Par Value (Face Value)
**Definition:** The principal amount of the bond to be repaid at maturity.  
**Context:** Par value is the amount that the bond issuer agrees to pay back to the bondholder at the bond's maturity date. For example, if you purchase a bond with a par value of $1,000, you will receive $1,000 when the bond matures.

### Coupon Rate
**Definition:** The fixed interest rate paid by the bond issuer on the bond’s par value.  
**Context:** The coupon rate determines the periodic interest payments made to bondholders. For instance, a bond with a par value of $1,000 and a coupon rate of 5% will pay $50 annually.

### Yield to Maturity (YTM)
**Definition:** The total return expected on a bond if held until it matures, factoring in both interest payments and capital gain or loss.  
**Context:** YTM is a comprehensive measure of a bond's return, considering the bond's current market price, par value, coupon interest rate, and time to maturity. It helps investors compare bonds with different prices and coupon rates.

### Bond Price
**Definition:** The current market price of a bond, expressed as a percentage of its par value.  
**Context:** Bond prices fluctuate based on interest rates, credit ratings, and market demand. For example, a bond trading at 95% of its par value is priced at $950 if its par value is $1,000.

### Liquidity
**Definition:** The ease with which a bond can be bought or sold in the market without significantly affecting its price.  
**Context:** Highly liquid bonds, such as government bonds, can be traded quickly and with minimal price impact, making them attractive to investors who may need to sell quickly.

### Conversion Rate
**Definition:** The number of shares a bondholder can receive upon converting a convertible bond into equity.  
**Context:** Convertible bonds offer the option to convert into a specified number of shares, providing potential upside if the issuer's stock price rises.

### Strip Bond
**Definition:** A zero-coupon bond created by separating coupons from the principal, sold at a discount and redeemed at par.  
**Context:** Strip bonds do not pay periodic interest but are sold at a discount, with the full face value paid at maturity. They are useful for investors seeking a lump sum at a future date.

### Margin Rate
**Definition:** The interest rate charged on borrowed funds.  
**Context:** Investors using leverage to purchase bonds may pay a margin rate on borrowed funds, impacting the overall return on investment.

### Credit Rating
**Definition:** An assessment of the creditworthiness of a bond, given by rating agencies like Moody’s, S&P, and DBRS.  
**Context:** Credit ratings help investors assess the risk of default. Higher-rated bonds (e.g., AAA) are considered safer but offer lower yields compared to lower-rated bonds (e.g., BB).

### Callable Bond
**Definition:** A bond that can be redeemed by the issuer before its maturity date at a specified price.  
**Context:** Callable bonds provide issuers with flexibility to refinance debt if interest rates decline, but they pose reinvestment risk to investors.

### Subordinated Debenture
**Definition:** An unsecured bond that ranks below other bonds in case of default, offering higher yields to compensate for additional risk.  
**Context:** Subordinated debentures are riskier than senior debt but can offer higher returns, appealing to investors willing to take on more risk.

### Commercial Paper
**Definition:** A short-term unsecured debt instrument issued by corporations to finance short-term liabilities.  
**Context:** Commercial paper is typically issued at a discount and matures within 270 days, providing corporations with quick access to capital.

### Guaranteed Investment Certificate (GIC)
**Definition:** A Canadian investment offering a guaranteed rate of return for a fixed term, typically offered by banks.  
**Context:** GICs are low-risk investments suitable for conservative investors seeking stable returns.

### Convertible Bond
**Definition:** A bond that can be converted into a predetermined number of the issuing company’s equity shares.  
**Context:** Convertible bonds offer the potential for capital appreciation if the issuer's stock performs well, while providing fixed-income characteristics.

### Overnight Rate
**Definition:** The interest rate at which major financial institutions borrow and lend short-term funds to one another.  
**Context:** The overnight rate influences other interest rates in the economy and is a key tool for central banks in monetary policy.

### Yield Curve
**Definition:** A graph showing the relationship between bond yields and their maturities.  
**Context:** The yield curve provides insights into market expectations for interest rates and economic activity. An upward-sloping curve suggests economic growth, while an inverted curve may signal a recession.

### Bond Rating Agency
**Definition:** Organizations like Moody’s, S&P, and DBRS that assess the credit quality of bond issuers.  
**Context:** Bond rating agencies provide independent evaluations of credit risk, helping investors make informed decisions.

### Sinking Fund
**Definition:** Funds set aside annually by an issuer to repay debt securities through purchases in the market or calling them.  
**Context:** Sinking funds reduce default risk by ensuring that funds are available to retire debt, providing reassurance to bondholders.

### Redemption Price
**Definition:** The price at which a callable bond can be redeemed before maturity.  
**Context:** The redemption price is typically higher than the par value, compensating investors for the early termination of the bond.

### Maturity
**Definition:** The set date on which a bond or debenture must be repaid.  
**Context:** Maturity dates range from short-term (less than one year) to long-term (over ten years), affecting the bond's interest rate risk and yield.

### Interest-Rate Risk
**Definition:** The risk that changes in market interest rates will affect the price of a bond.  
**Context:** Rising interest rates cause bond prices to fall, impacting the market value of fixed-income investments.

### Term to Maturity
**Definition:** The remaining time before a bond’s principal is due to be repaid.  
**Context:** The term to maturity influences a bond's sensitivity to interest rate changes and its yield.

### Conversion Price
**Definition:** The price at which a convertible bond can be exchanged for equity shares.  
**Context:** The conversion price determines the value of the conversion option, impacting the bond's attractiveness to investors.

### Flip
**Definition:** The act of converting a convertible bond into its underlying equity shares.  
**Context:** Flipping allows bondholders to participate in equity gains, potentially increasing their investment returns.

### Coupon Payment
**Definition:** The periodic interest payment made by the bond issuer to the bondholder.  
**Context:** Coupon payments provide regular income to bondholders, contributing to the bond's total return.

### Interest Rate Premium
**Definition:** The additional yield that investors demand to compensate for the risk of lending or investing.  
**Context:** Interest rate premiums reflect the risk associated with different issuers and bond types, influencing investment decisions.

### Risk Premium
**Definition:** The return in excess of the risk-free rate of return that an investor requires for taking on additional risk.  
**Context:** Risk premiums vary based on market conditions and the perceived risk of the investment, affecting the bond's yield.

Understanding these terms is essential for analyzing fixed-income securities and making informed investment decisions. By incorporating these definitions into your financial vocabulary, you can enhance your ability to communicate and analyze fixed-income concepts effectively.
