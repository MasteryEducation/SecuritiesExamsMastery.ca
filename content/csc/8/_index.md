## Chapter 8: Equity Securities: Common and Preferred Shares

Equity securities represent an ownership interest in a company, granting shareholders potential rewards through dividends and capital appreciation. In Canada, equities play a vital role in both individual investment portfolios and large institutional strategies. This chapter explores the features of common and preferred shares, their respective benefits and drawbacks, and how investors use stock indexes and averages to track market performance. You will also learn how these securities are issued, traded, and regulated, and how they fit into broader investment strategies within the Canadian market.

--------------------------------------------------------------------------------
## 1. Introduction to Equity Securities

Equity securities are financial instruments that confer partial ownership of a corporation. Shareholders benefit from the growth and profitability of the corporation through:

• Dividend income  
• Capital gains when share prices appreciate  

Equity securities are categorized primarily into two broad classes: common shares and preferred shares. Understanding each category’s distinct attributes and associated risks is critical to making informed investment decisions. In Canada, these shares are traded on stock exchanges such as the Toronto Stock Exchange (TSX). 

### Why Invest in Equities?
• Potential for Higher Returns: Equity investments can generate significant capital gains in a growing economy.  
• Dividend Income: Many corporations pay regular dividends, offering ongoing income.  
• Ownership Rights: Common shareholders typically have voting rights, which allow them to influence certain corporate decisions.  

As with all investments, equities come with market risk, where share prices can fluctuate. Balancing potential returns against risk tolerance is a key focus of portfolio construction.

--------------------------------------------------------------------------------
## 2. Common Shares

### 2.1 Defining Common Shares
Common shares represent the most prevalent form of equity ownership in a public company. Each share allows the holder to participate in corporate decisions and, subject to the board’s discretion, share in the company’s profits through dividends. 

#### Key Features of Common Shares
1. Voting Rights: Common shareholders typically have the right to vote on important corporate matters, such as the election of the board of directors and approval of major transactions.  
2. Dividends: Although dividends are not guaranteed, many established companies—particularly in sectors like banking and utilities—distribute dividends at regular intervals.  
3. Capital Appreciation: Common shares can appreciate in market value if the company performs well, though prices may also decline if the company underperforms or if market conditions deteriorate.  
4. Limited Liability: Shareholders’ losses are limited to their invested capital.  

### 2.2 Corporate Governance and Shareholder Rights
Common shareholders can exercise their voting rights during annual general meetings (AGMs) or special shareholder meetings. Key governance items may include:

• Electing board members  
• Approving mergers or acquisitions  
• Endorsing management compensation policies  

#### Shareholder Derivative Actions
Under certain circumstances, if directors or officers are suspected of wrongdoing, shareholders may initiate a derivative lawsuit—subject to legal guidelines and approval by regulators such as the Ontario Securities Commission (OSC) or provincial courts—to protect the corporation’s best interest.

--------------------------------------------------------------------------------
## 3. Preferred Shares

### 3.1 Defining Preferred Shares
Preferred shares occupy a middle ground between common shares and bonds. They offer a fixed or floating dividend rate, typically paid before any dividends on common shares. Unlike common shares, preferred shares generally do not have voting rights unless dividends go into arrears or certain extraordinary corporate events arise.

#### Key Benefits and Drawbacks
• Priority in Dividends: Preferred shareholders enjoy dividend payments before common shareholders.  
• Potential for Lower Volatility: Preferred shares tend to experience less price volatility than common shares.  
• Limited Voting Rights: Except in special circumstances, preferred shareholders usually cannot vote on corporate matters.  
• Sensitivity to Interest Rates: Because of their fixed-income–like features, preferred share prices can be sensitive to interest rate fluctuations.  

### 3.2 Types of Preferred Shares
Preferred shares in Canada often come in various structures, reflecting companies’ attempts to tailor financing to market demand. Common types include:

1. **Cumulative Preferred Shares**  
   – Unpaid dividends accumulate and must be paid before common share dividends.  
2. **Non-cumulative Preferred Shares**  
   – Unpaid dividends do not accumulate if the board decides to skip or reduce a dividend.  
3. **Convertible Preferred Shares**  
   – Can be converted into a specified number of common shares, typically at predetermined dates and conversion ratios.  
4. **Retractable or Redeemable Preferred Shares**  
   – The issuing company may buy back these shares at set dates or upon certain conditions, often at a specific redemption price.  
5. **Rate-Reset Preferred Shares**  
   – The dividend is reset periodically based on a benchmark rate, such as the five-year Government of Canada bond yield, plus a spread.

### 3.3 Example: Rate-Reset Preferred Shares
A major Canadian bank—like RBC or TD—may issue a rate-reset preferred share that pays a fixed dividend for five years. After that period, the dividend rate “resets” based on the then-current Government of Canada 5-year bond yield plus a specified percentage. If interest rates rise, the dividend on these shares increases, offering partial protection against rate risk.

## 4. Comparing Common and Preferred Shares

Below is a summary of some fundamental differences between common shares and preferred shares:

| Aspect                     | Common Shares                                           | Preferred Shares                                            |
|----------------------------|---------------------------------------------------------|-------------------------------------------------------------|
| Voting Rights             | Typically have voting rights                            | Generally no voting rights                                  |
| Dividend Priority         | Dividend is paid after preferred shareholders           | Fixed dividend with priority over common shares             |
| Dividend Growth Potential | Dividends can grow over time if the company is healthy  | Dividend amounts are typically fixed or tied to a formula   |
| Price Volatility          | Generally higher price fluctuation                      | Typically more stable than common shares                    |
| Claims on Assets          | Junior claim on corporate assets in liquidation         | Higher claim than common stock but lower than bondholders   |

## 5. Stock Indexes and Averages

Stock indexes and averages help investors track the performance of a segment of the equity market:

• **S&P/TSX Composite Index** – Canada’s primary stock market index, representing approximately 95% of Canadian market capitalization.  
• **Dow Jones Industrial Average (DJIA)** – Tracks 30, large, blue-chip U.S. companies (used as a reference for the U.S. market, though it’s price-weighted).  
• **S&P 500** – Represents 500 of the largest U.S. companies by market cap.  

Investors often benchmark their portfolio performance against these indexes to gauge relative returns. In Canada, pension funds frequently use the S&P/TSX Composite Index to track their equity performance because it is a broad measure of the Canadian market.

### 5.1 Index Weighting Methods
• **Price-Weighted** (e.g., Dow Jones Industrial Average) – Weighted according to stock price.  
• **Market-Cap–Weighted** (e.g., S&P/TSX Composite Index, S&P 500) – Weighted according to market capitalization (price per share × outstanding shares).  
• **Equal-Weighted** – All constituent companies have the same weight, regardless of their market capitalization.

## 6. Issuance and Trading of Equities in Canada

### 6.1 Primary and Secondary Markets  
• **Primary Market:** Companies initially sell shares to the public (Initial Public Offerings, or IPOs) to raise capital. Investment dealers underwrite and distribute these new shares to institutional and retail investors.  
• **Secondary Market:** Shares are traded among investors on stock exchanges, such as the TSX or TSX Venture Exchange (TSXV).

### 6.2 Trading Mechanics
Securities trading in Canada is regulated by the Investment Industry Regulatory Organization of Canada (IIROC) and provincial securities commissions. Trades typically settle in two business days (T+2). When you buy or sell shares, your broker sends the order to the marketplace, where it is matched with a compatible order at the current market price or a specified limit price.

## 7. Practical Example: Investing through a TFSA

Imagine you decide to invest in common shares of a major Canadian bank (e.g., RBC) through your Tax-Free Savings Account (TFSA). You buy 100 shares at $100/share for a total of $10,000. Here is how the potential outcomes might unfold:

• If RBC performs well and the share price rises to $110, your position is now worth $11,000. You also receive dividends—let’s say $4.00 per share annually—amounting to $400 in total. Because this investment is within a TFSA, any capital gains or dividends you earn are typically tax-free.*  
• If RBC’s share price declines and the bank reduces dividends, your investment’s market value drops and dividend income decreases. However, your losses remain limited to your original $10,000 contribution since TFSAs only hold your after-tax contributions.

(*Always verify the most current Canada Revenue Agency (CRA) regulations for TFSA contribution limits and rules.)

## 8. Regulation of Equity Markets

### 8.1 Main Regulatory Bodies
1. **Provincial Securities Commissions** (e.g., OSC in Ontario, AMF in Quebec) – Oversee securities laws within each province.  
2. **Canadian Securities Administrators (CSA)** – Coordinates securities regulation across provinces and territories.  
3. **Investment Industry Regulatory Organization of Canada (IIROC)** – Oversees investment dealers and trading activity.  
4. **Office of the Superintendent of Financial Institutions (OSFI)** – Regulates federally registered banks and insurance companies, ensuring capital adequacy.

### 8.2 Investor Protection
The Canadian Investor Protection Fund (CIPF) offers coverage for investors in the event an IIROC-regulated firm becomes insolvent. The aim is to safeguard investor assets held by the failing firm, up to specific coverage limits.

## 9. Case Study: Preferred Shares for Stable Income

Retiree Evelyn wants to generate more stable income than common dividends. She invests in a major telecommunications provider’s cumulative preferred shares, paying a quarterly dividend at a fixed rate of 5%. Even if the company encounters financial headwinds and skips a quarter’s dividend, those payments accumulate and must be cleared before any dividends can be paid on common shares. This structure helps Evelyn plan for consistent income, though she gives up the voting rights that common shareholders might have.

## 11. Best Practices and Common Pitfalls

• **Diversify:** Owning a mix of common and preferred shares across sectors can help mitigate risk.  
• **Stay Informed:** Monitor dividend announcements, corporate earnings, and regulatory changes using resources like SEDAR (System for Electronic Document Analysis and Retrieval).  
• **Understand the Reset Terms (Preferred Shares):** Rate-reset preferred shares offer flexibility in changing interest rate environments, but understand how the rate is determined.  
• **Avoid Chasing Yield:** A high dividend yield can sometimes indicate underlying corporate weaknesses. Investigate the sustainability of dividend payouts.  
• **Manage Liquidity Risk:** More thinly traded preferred shares may be harder to sell without impacting the share price.  

--------------------------------------------------------------------------------
## 12. Summary and Actionable Insights

• **Common Shares:** Provide growth opportunities, voting rights, and dividends (though not guaranteed).  
• **Preferred Shares:** Offer more predictable income and precedence over common shares in dividends and liquidation.  
• **Market Indicators:** Stock indexes like the S&P/TSX Composite help track overall market performance and act as benchmarks.  
• **Regulatory Environment:** Multiple bodies (IIROC, OSC, CSA) enforce compliance, protect investors, and promote fair and efficient markets.  

Going forward, consider how equity securities fit within your broader portfolio. Evaluate how preferred shares might provide stable income while common shares offer growth potential. Review your risk tolerance and consult professional resources to ensure your selections align with your long-term financial goals.
