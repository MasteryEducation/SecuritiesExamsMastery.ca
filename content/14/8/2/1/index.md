## 20.2.1 Alternative Strategy Funds

Alternative strategy funds represent a dynamic and versatile segment of the investment universe, offering investors opportunities beyond traditional mutual funds. These funds are designed to employ a wide range of investment strategies, often with the goal of achieving returns that are uncorrelated with the broader market. In this section, we will delve into the defining characteristics of alternative strategy funds, explore various strategies such as relative value, event-driven, and directional strategies, and provide practical insights into their application within the Canadian financial landscape.

### Overview of Alternative Strategy Funds

Alternative strategy funds are investment vehicles that utilize non-traditional strategies to achieve their investment objectives. Unlike conventional mutual funds, which typically invest in a diversified portfolio of stocks and bonds, alternative strategy funds have the flexibility to employ a variety of investment techniques, including leverage, short selling, and derivatives. This flexibility allows them to pursue absolute returns, hedge against market volatility, and exploit market inefficiencies.

#### Distinguishing Features

1. **Diverse Strategies**: Alternative strategy funds can implement a wide array of strategies, ranging from market-neutral approaches to aggressive directional bets.
2. **Risk Management**: These funds often employ sophisticated risk management techniques to mitigate potential losses and enhance returns.
3. **Regulatory Environment**: In Canada, alternative strategy funds are subject to specific regulatory requirements, ensuring transparency and investor protection.

### Relative Value Strategies

Relative value strategies aim to capitalize on pricing inefficiencies between related securities. These strategies involve identifying mispricings and executing trades to profit from the convergence of prices. 

#### Examples of Relative Value Strategies

- **Convertible Arbitrage**: This strategy involves purchasing convertible securities (such as convertible bonds) and simultaneously shorting the underlying stock. The goal is to profit from discrepancies between the convertible's price and the stock's price.
- **Fixed-Income Arbitrage**: Investors exploit pricing anomalies in fixed-income securities, such as bonds, by taking long and short positions in related instruments.

#### Case Study: Canadian Convertible Arbitrage

Consider a Canadian hedge fund that identifies a mispricing in a convertible bond issued by a major Canadian bank. By purchasing the bond and shorting the bank's stock, the fund aims to profit as the bond's price aligns with the stock's value. This strategy requires careful analysis of interest rates, credit spreads, and market conditions.

### Event-Driven Strategies

Event-driven strategies focus on corporate actions such as mergers, acquisitions, restructurings, and bankruptcies. These strategies seek to profit from the anticipated impact of these events on the prices of the involved securities.

#### Benefits and Risks

- **Benefits**: Event-driven strategies can offer significant returns if the anticipated corporate event occurs as expected. They provide opportunities to profit from market inefficiencies and investor sentiment.
- **Risks**: These strategies are subject to event-specific risks, such as regulatory hurdles, deal cancellations, or changes in market conditions that can affect the outcome of the event.

#### Example: Merger Arbitrage in Canada

A Canadian investment fund specializing in merger arbitrage might invest in the stock of a target company in a pending acquisition, while shorting the acquiring company's stock. The fund profits if the merger is completed at the expected terms. However, if the deal falls through, the fund may incur losses.

### Directional Strategies

Directional strategies aim to capitalize on anticipated movements in market prices. These strategies involve taking long or short positions based on the investor's outlook on market trends.

#### Techniques and Market Conditions

- **Long/Short Equity**: Investors take long positions in stocks expected to rise and short positions in stocks expected to fall. This strategy benefits from both upward and downward market movements.
- **Global Macro**: This approach involves making investment decisions based on macroeconomic trends, such as interest rate changes, currency fluctuations, and geopolitical events.

#### Canadian Context: Long/Short Equity

In the Canadian market, a long/short equity fund might take a long position in a rising sector, such as technology, while shorting a declining sector, such as energy. This strategy allows the fund to benefit from sector-specific trends while hedging against broader market volatility.

### Glossary

- **Hedge Fund**: A type of alternative strategy fund that employs various aggressive strategies, including leverage and short selling, to achieve high returns.
- **Arbitrage**: The simultaneous purchase and sale of an asset to profit from a difference in the price.
- **Short Selling**: Selling borrowed securities with the intention of repurchasing them at a lower price.

### Conclusion

Alternative strategy funds offer a diverse range of investment opportunities, allowing investors to pursue unique strategies that can enhance portfolio diversification and potentially improve returns. By understanding the nuances of relative value, event-driven, and directional strategies, investors can better navigate the complexities of the Canadian financial landscape and make informed investment decisions.
