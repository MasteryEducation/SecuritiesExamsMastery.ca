## 20.1 Explain What an Alternative Investment Is

In the ever-evolving landscape of finance, alternative investments have emerged as a compelling option for investors seeking diversification beyond traditional asset classes such as equities, bonds, and cash. This section aims to provide a comprehensive understanding of what constitutes an alternative investment, its various categories, and the strategies employed within this dynamic segment of the investment universe.

### Definition of Alternative Investments

Alternative investments are asset classes that differ from traditional investments like stocks, bonds, and cash. Unlike conventional assets, alternative investments often involve complex structures, limited liquidity, and unique risk-return profiles. While there is no standardized definition, the general consensus is that alternative investments encompass a broad range of financial instruments and strategies that do not fit neatly into the traditional categories.

#### Distinguishing Features

1. **Complexity and Structure:** Alternative investments often involve intricate structures and strategies that require specialized knowledge to manage effectively.
2. **Liquidity Constraints:** Many alternative investments are less liquid than traditional assets, meaning they cannot be easily bought or sold in the market.
3. **Diversification Benefits:** These investments often have low correlation with traditional asset classes, providing diversification benefits to a portfolio.

### Categorization of Alternative Investments

Alternative investments can be broadly categorized into three main groups: alternative strategy funds, alternative assets, and private equity. Each category plays a significant role in the investment landscape, offering unique opportunities and challenges.

#### 1. Alternative Strategy Funds

Alternative strategy funds employ various investment strategies that differ from traditional long-only equity or fixed-income approaches. These strategies are designed to exploit market inefficiencies and generate returns that are uncorrelated with traditional markets.

- **Relative Value Strategies:** These strategies aim to profit from pricing discrepancies between related securities. For example, a fund might simultaneously buy and sell different securities to capitalize on perceived mispricings.
- **Event-Driven Strategies:** Focused on corporate events such as mergers, acquisitions, and restructurings, these strategies seek to profit from the anticipated impact of these events on security prices.
- **Directional Strategies:** These strategies take positions based on anticipated movements in market prices, often using leverage and derivatives to enhance returns.

#### 2. Alternative Assets

Alternative assets encompass a wide range of investments that do not fall within the traditional categories of stocks, bonds, or cash. These assets include:

- **Real Estate:** Investments in physical properties or real estate investment trusts (REITs).
- **Commodities:** Investments in physical goods such as gold, oil, or agricultural products.
- **Infrastructure:** Investments in public works projects like roads, bridges, and utilities.

#### 3. Private Equity

Private equity involves investments in private companies or public companies with the intent to take them private. This category includes:

- **Venture Capital:** Investments in early-stage companies with high growth potential.
- **Buyouts:** Acquiring a controlling interest in a company, often with the aim of improving its operations and increasing its value.
- **Growth Capital:** Investments in more mature companies looking to expand or restructure operations.

### Sub-Categories within Alternative Strategy Funds

Within the realm of alternative strategy funds, several sub-categories exist, each with specific objectives and methodologies:

- **Relative Value Strategies:** These strategies focus on identifying and exploiting price discrepancies between related securities. For instance, a fund might engage in convertible arbitrage, where it simultaneously buys convertible bonds and sells the underlying stock to profit from pricing inefficiencies.
  
- **Event-Driven Strategies:** These strategies capitalize on corporate events such as mergers, acquisitions, and bankruptcies. A merger arbitrage strategy, for example, involves buying the stock of a target company and shorting the stock of the acquiring company to profit from the spread between the current market price and the acquisition price.

- **Directional Strategies:** These strategies take positions based on anticipated market movements. A global macro strategy, for instance, might involve taking long or short positions in various asset classes based on macroeconomic trends and geopolitical events.

### Overview of Alternative Assets and Private Equity

Alternative assets and private equity are key components of the alternative investment universe, offering unique opportunities for investors:

- **Alternative Assets:** These include tangible assets like real estate and commodities, as well as intangible assets like intellectual property and patents. These assets often provide a hedge against inflation and can enhance portfolio diversification.

- **Private Equity:** This involves investing in private companies or public companies with the intent to take them private. Private equity investments are typically long-term in nature and involve active management to improve the company's operations and increase its value.

### Glossary

- **Alternative Investment:** Asset classes that differ from traditional investments like stocks, bonds, and cash.
- **Relative Value Strategy:** A strategy aiming to profit from pricing discrepancies between related securities.
- **Event-Driven Strategy:** A strategy focusing on corporate events such as mergers, acquisitions, and restructurings.
- **Directional Strategy:** A strategy that seeks to profit from anticipated movements in market prices.
- **Private Equity:** Investments in private companies or in public companies with the intent to take them private.

### Practical Examples and Case Studies

To illustrate the concepts discussed, consider the following examples:

- **Canadian Pension Funds:** Many Canadian pension funds, such as the Canada Pension Plan Investment Board (CPPIB), allocate a portion of their portfolios to alternative investments to enhance returns and diversify risk. These funds often invest in private equity, infrastructure, and real estate.

- **RBC's Alternative Investment Strategies:** RBC Global Asset Management offers a range of alternative investment strategies, including hedge funds and private equity, to institutional and high-net-worth clients. These strategies are designed to provide uncorrelated returns and enhance portfolio diversification.

### Best Practices and Challenges

When considering alternative investments, investors should be aware of the following best practices and challenges:

- **Due Diligence:** Conduct thorough due diligence to understand the risks and potential returns associated with alternative investments.
- **Liquidity Considerations:** Be mindful of the liquidity constraints associated with many alternative investments and ensure that your portfolio can accommodate these constraints.
- **Regulatory Compliance:** Ensure compliance with Canadian financial regulations, such as those set by the Canadian Investment Regulatory Organization (CIRO), when investing in alternative assets.

### Conclusion

Alternative investments offer a diverse range of opportunities for investors seeking to enhance returns and diversify risk. By understanding the various categories and strategies within this dynamic segment, investors can make informed decisions and effectively incorporate alternative investments into their portfolios.
