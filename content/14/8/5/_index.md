## 20.5 Describe the Main Structural Features of Hedge Funds, Alternative Mutual Funds, Fund of Hedge Funds, and ETFs

In the realm of alternative investments, understanding the structural features of hedge funds, alternative mutual funds (often referred to as liquid alts), fund of hedge funds (FoHF), and exchange-traded funds (ETFs) is crucial for both seasoned investors and those new to the field. This section delves into the intricacies of these investment vehicles, highlighting their unique characteristics, regulatory environments, and strategic advantages.

### Overview of Alternative Investment Structures

Alternative investments offer diversification beyond traditional asset classes like stocks and bonds. They often employ complex strategies to achieve returns, manage risk, and provide exposure to different market dynamics. Let's explore the structural features of some key alternative investment vehicles.

### Hedge Funds

**Definition and Structural Characteristics**

Hedge funds are private investment partnerships that employ a wide range of strategies to generate returns for their investors. Unlike traditional mutual funds, hedge funds are less regulated, allowing them greater flexibility in their investment approaches. This flexibility enables hedge funds to pursue strategies such as long/short equity, market neutral, and global macro, among others.

**Fee Structure**

Hedge funds typically charge a management fee, often around 2% of assets under management, and a performance-based incentive fee, commonly 20% of profits. This "2 and 20" structure aligns the interests of the fund managers with those of the investors but can also lead to higher costs compared to other investment vehicles.

**Investor Base**

Hedge funds primarily cater to accredited and institutional investors, such as pension funds and endowments, due to their complex strategies and higher risk profiles. These investors are assumed to have the financial acumen and resources to understand and bear the risks involved.

**Investment Strategies**

Hedge funds often utilize leverage, derivatives, and short selling to enhance returns and manage risk. Leverage involves borrowing capital to increase investment exposure, while derivatives and short selling allow hedge funds to profit from market movements and hedge against potential losses.

### Alternative Mutual Funds (Liquid Alts)

**Definition and Structural Characteristics**

Alternative mutual funds, or liquid alts, incorporate alternative investment strategies within a mutual fund framework. These funds offer retail investors access to strategies traditionally reserved for hedge funds, such as long/short equity and global macro, while maintaining the liquidity and transparency of conventional mutual funds.

**Regulatory Changes**

Recent regulatory changes in Canada have facilitated the creation of liquid alts, expanding retail investor access to alternative strategies. These changes allow mutual funds to use derivatives, leverage, and short selling within specified limits, providing a balance between innovation and investor protection.

**Structural Similarities and Differences**

Liquid alts share structural similarities with conventional mutual funds, including daily liquidity and transparency. However, they differ in their use of alternative strategies and the regulatory limitations imposed on leverage and derivatives usage.

### Fund of Hedge Funds (FoHF)

**Definition and Structural Characteristics**

A fund of hedge funds (FoHF) is an investment vehicle that pools capital to invest in a diversified portfolio of hedge funds. This structure allows investors to access a broader range of hedge fund strategies and managers, providing diversification and risk management benefits.

**Benefits and Costs**

FoHFs offer several advantages, including diversification, professional due diligence, and access to hedge funds that may be otherwise unavailable to individual investors. However, these benefits come at a cost, as FoHFs typically charge additional fees on top of the underlying hedge fund fees, leading to higher overall costs.

**Risks and Disadvantages**

Investors in FoHFs face potential dilution of returns due to the multiple layers of fees and the risk of relying on the FoHF manager's expertise. Additionally, the performance of a FoHF is dependent on the selection and management of the underlying hedge funds.

### ETFs

**Definition and Structural Characteristics**

Exchange-traded funds (ETFs) are investment funds that trade on stock exchanges, holding a basket of securities that typically track an index. ETFs offer investors a flexible and cost-effective way to gain exposure to various asset classes and investment strategies.

**Flexibility and Benefits**

ETFs can incorporate alternative strategies, such as leveraged and inverse ETFs, which aim to amplify or inverse the performance of an underlying index. The benefits of ETFs include intraday trading, transparency, and lower fees compared to hedge funds and FoHFs.

**Regulatory Framework**

ETFs are subject to a robust regulatory framework that ensures transparency and investor protection. This framework facilitates the inclusion of alternative strategies while maintaining the liquidity and accessibility that ETFs are known for.

### Glossary

- **Leveraged ETF:** An ETF that uses financial derivatives and debt to amplify the returns of an underlying index.
- **Inverse ETF:** An ETF designed to deliver the opposite of the performance of a specific index.
- **Fund of Hedge Funds (FoHF):** A portfolio of multiple hedge funds managed collectively to diversify risk and access various strategies.
- **Liquid Alts:** Alternative mutual funds that offer alternative investment strategies with mutual fund-like liquidity and transparency.
- **Intraday Trading:** The buying and selling of securities within the same trading day.

### Conclusion

Understanding the structural features of hedge funds, alternative mutual funds, fund of hedge funds, and ETFs is essential for navigating the complex landscape of alternative investments. Each vehicle offers unique benefits and challenges, making them suitable for different types of investors and investment goals. By leveraging these insights, Canadian investors can make informed decisions and effectively incorporate alternative investments into their portfolios.
