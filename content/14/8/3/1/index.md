## 20.3.1 Diversification

Diversification is a fundamental principle in portfolio management, aimed at reducing risk by allocating investments across various financial instruments, industries, and other categories. This section delves into the role of diversification, particularly through alternative investments, in enhancing portfolio stability and reducing risk.

### Role of Diversification

Diversification reduces portfolio risk by spreading investments across unrelated asset classes. The core idea is that by holding a variety of investments, the poor performance of some can be offset by the better performance of others. This is particularly important in the context of alternative investments, which often behave differently from traditional stocks and bonds.

#### How Diversification Works

Imagine a portfolio consisting solely of Canadian equities. If the Canadian stock market experiences a downturn, the entire portfolio would likely suffer. However, by diversifying into other asset classes such as bonds, commodities, or real estate, the impact of a downturn in one market can be mitigated by stability or gains in another.

### Low Correlation Benefits

One of the key benefits of diversification is the inclusion of assets with low or negative correlations to each other. Correlation is a statistical measure that describes the extent to which two assets move in relation to each other. Assets with low or negative correlations do not move in tandem, which can stabilize a portfolio's net asset value (NAV).

#### Benefits of Low Correlation

- **Risk Reduction:** Holding assets that do not move together reduces the overall risk of the portfolio. If one asset class underperforms, others may perform well, balancing the overall portfolio performance.
- **Stable NAV:** A consistently maintained net asset value contributes to lower portfolio volatility, providing investors with a smoother investment experience.

### Impact on Portfolio Volatility

Diversification through alternative investments can significantly lower overall portfolio volatility. Alternative investments, such as hedge funds, private equity, commodities, and real estate, often have different risk-return profiles compared to traditional investments.

#### Empirical Evidence

Studies have shown that portfolios including alternative investments tend to exhibit lower volatility. For example, a study by the Canadian Investment Review highlighted that Canadian pension funds incorporating real estate and infrastructure investments experienced reduced volatility and improved risk-adjusted returns.

### Examples of Diversifying Alternatives

Several alternative investments can enhance diversification within a portfolio:

- **Commodities:** Investing in commodities like gold, oil, or agricultural products can provide a hedge against inflation and currency fluctuations, offering a buffer during economic downturns.
- **Real Estate:** Real estate investments, including REITs (Real Estate Investment Trusts), offer income generation and capital appreciation potential, often with low correlation to stock markets.
- **Hedge Funds:** These funds employ diverse strategies, including long/short equity, market neutral, and global macro, which can provide returns independent of market movements.
- **Private Equity:** Investing in private companies can yield high returns and is less correlated with public equity markets.

### Glossary

- **Correlation:** A statistical measure that describes the extent to which two assets move in relation to each other.
- **Stable NAV:** A consistently maintained net asset value, contributing to lower portfolio volatility.

### Conclusion

Diversification is a powerful tool in risk management, particularly when incorporating alternative investments. By spreading investments across various asset classes with low correlations, investors can achieve a more stable and resilient portfolio. Understanding and applying these principles can lead to improved investment outcomes and a smoother ride through market fluctuations.
