## 20.3.2 Adding Alpha

In the realm of investment management, the quest for alpha is a central theme. Alpha represents the excess return on an investment relative to the return of a benchmark index. It is a measure of the value that an investment manager adds to a portfolio through active management. In this section, we delve into the concept of alpha, explore how alternative investments can be a source of alpha, and highlight the critical role of skilled managers in this process.

### Definition of Alpha

Alpha is a key performance metric in the investment world. It quantifies the ability of an investment strategy to outperform a market index or benchmark. In essence, alpha is the return on an investment that is not attributable to general market movements. A positive alpha indicates that the investment has outperformed the benchmark, while a negative alpha suggests underperformance.

Alpha is crucial for investors seeking to maximize returns while managing risk. It provides insight into the effectiveness of an investment strategy and the skill of the portfolio manager. In the context of alternative investments, alpha is particularly significant as these strategies often aim to deliver returns that are uncorrelated with traditional asset classes.

### Generating Alpha with Alternatives

Alternative investments encompass a wide range of asset classes and strategies, including hedge funds, private equity, real estate, and commodities. These investments often employ specialized approaches to capture alpha, leveraging market inefficiencies and unique opportunities.

#### Arbitrage Strategies

Arbitrage strategies seek to exploit price discrepancies between related securities. For example, a manager might identify a mispricing between a stock and its corresponding options or between two similar stocks in different markets. By simultaneously buying and selling these securities, the manager can lock in a risk-free profit, thus generating alpha.

#### Event-Driven Strategies

Event-driven strategies focus on corporate events such as mergers, acquisitions, bankruptcies, or restructurings. These events can create temporary market inefficiencies, which skilled managers can exploit to generate alpha. For instance, during a merger, the target company's stock might trade below the acquisition price due to uncertainty. An event-driven manager might buy the target's stock, betting that the deal will go through and the stock price will rise to the acquisition price.

### Role of Skilled Managers

The ability to generate alpha through alternative investments heavily relies on the expertise and experience of skilled managers. These professionals possess the knowledge and intuition to identify opportunities that others might overlook. They employ sophisticated analytical tools and models to assess risk and potential returns.

Skilled managers are adept at navigating complex markets and executing strategies with precision. Their ability to adapt to changing market conditions and their deep understanding of specific asset classes are crucial in capturing alpha. In Canada, investment firms like RBC Global Asset Management and TD Asset Management employ teams of experienced managers to implement alternative strategies effectively.

### Examples of Alpha-Generating Strategies

Let's explore some specific alternative strategies that aim to produce alpha:

#### Relative Value Strategies

Relative value strategies involve identifying price discrepancies between related securities and capitalizing on these differences. For example, a manager might find that two bonds with similar credit ratings and maturities are priced differently. By buying the undervalued bond and shorting the overvalued one, the manager can profit from the convergence of their prices.

#### Distressed Securities

Investing in distressed securities involves buying the debt or equity of companies in financial distress. These investments can offer substantial returns if the company successfully restructures or emerges from bankruptcy. Skilled managers assess the likelihood of recovery and the potential upside, aiming to generate alpha by taking calculated risks.

### Glossary

- **Alpha:** A measure of the active return on an investment, gauging the performance relative to a market index.

### Practical Example: Canadian Pension Funds

Canadian pension funds, such as the Canada Pension Plan Investment Board (CPPIB), often employ alternative strategies to enhance returns. By diversifying into private equity, infrastructure, and real estate, these funds aim to generate alpha and meet their long-term obligations. Their success in capturing alpha underscores the importance of skilled management and strategic asset allocation.

### Conclusion

Adding alpha to a portfolio through alternative investments requires a deep understanding of markets, a keen eye for opportunities, and the expertise of skilled managers. By employing strategies like arbitrage and event-driven approaches, investors can enhance returns and achieve performance beyond benchmark indices. As the Canadian financial landscape evolves, the pursuit of alpha remains a vital component of successful investment management.
