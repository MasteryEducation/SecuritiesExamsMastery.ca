## 20.4 Describe the Risks of Investing in Alternatives

Investing in alternative assets can offer significant benefits, such as diversification and potential for higher returns. However, these investments also come with unique risks that investors must understand and manage. In this section, we will explore the various risks associated with alternative investments, categorize them into first-order, second-order, and operational risks, and discuss strategies to mitigate these risks effectively.

### Introduction to Risks

Alternative investments encompass a wide range of asset classes, including hedge funds, private equity, real estate, commodities, and more. Each of these asset classes carries its own set of risks, which can be broadly categorized into three main types:

1. **First-Order Risks**: These are the primary risks associated with market movements and are systematic in nature.
2. **Second-Order Risks**: These involve more specific risks related to the structure and characteristics of the investment.
3. **Operational Risks**: These pertain to the internal processes and systems of the investment management firm.

Understanding these risks is crucial for investors seeking to incorporate alternative investments into their portfolios.

### First-Order Risks

First-order risks are those that arise from exposure to market directions affecting equities, fixed income, currencies, and commodities. These risks are systematic, meaning they are inherent to the entire market and cannot be diversified away. 

#### Systematic Risk

Systematic risk, also known as market risk, affects all investments within a market segment. For example, a downturn in the global equity market will impact all stocks, regardless of their individual characteristics. This type of risk is unavoidable and can significantly affect the performance of directional strategies employed by alternative investments.

#### Impact on Directional Strategies

Directional strategies, such as those used by hedge funds, rely on predicting market movements to generate returns. However, systematic risks can lead to significant losses if market movements are unfavorable. For instance, a hedge fund betting on rising oil prices may suffer losses if geopolitical tensions ease and prices fall unexpectedly.

### Second-Order Risks

Second-order risks are more specific and arise from the unique characteristics of alternative investments. These include:

#### Liquidity Risk

Liquidity risk refers to the difficulty of buying or selling an investment quickly without affecting its price. Alternative investments, such as private equity or real estate, often have low liquidity, making it challenging to exit positions swiftly.

#### Leverage Risk

Leverage risk involves the use of borrowed capital to amplify returns. While leverage can enhance gains, it also magnifies losses. For example, a real estate investment trust (REIT) using high leverage may face significant losses if property values decline.

#### Deal Breakage Risk

Deal breakage risk occurs when a planned transaction fails to close, leading to potential losses. This is common in private equity deals where regulatory or financial hurdles may prevent completion.

#### Default and Counterparty Risk

Default risk is the possibility that a borrower will fail to meet its obligations. Counterparty risk arises when the other party in a financial contract defaults. These risks are prevalent in derivatives and structured products.

#### Trading and Concentration Risk

Trading risk involves losses due to adverse market movements during trading. Concentration risk arises from a lack of diversification, where a portfolio is heavily weighted in a single asset or sector.

#### Pricing and Trading Model Risk

Pricing model risk occurs when the models used to value investments are inaccurate. Trading model risk involves errors in the algorithms used for trading decisions, leading to potential losses.

### Operational Risks

Operational risks are related to the internal processes and systems of the investment management firm. These include:

#### System Failures

System failures can occur due to technological issues, leading to disruptions in trading or reporting. For example, a hedge fund may suffer losses if its trading platform crashes during a critical market event.

#### Reporting Inaccuracies

Inaccurate reporting can lead to misinformed investment decisions. This risk is heightened in alternative investments due to their complex structures and valuation methods.

### Risk Mitigation Strategies

Mitigating the risks associated with alternative investments requires a comprehensive approach. Here are some strategies:

#### Due Diligence

Conduct thorough due diligence before investing. This includes evaluating the investment manager's track record, understanding the investment strategy, and assessing the underlying assets.

#### Diversification

Diversify across different asset classes and strategies to reduce exposure to any single risk. For example, combining real estate, commodities, and hedge funds can help balance the portfolio.

#### Robust Risk Management

Implement robust risk management practices, such as setting stop-loss limits, using hedging strategies, and regularly reviewing the portfolio's risk profile.

#### Regular Monitoring

Continuously monitor investments and market conditions to identify potential risks early. This allows for timely adjustments to the investment strategy.

#### Strong Operational Controls

Ensure strong operational controls are in place to prevent system failures and reporting inaccuracies. Regular audits and compliance checks can help maintain integrity.

### Glossary

- **Systematic Risk**: The inherent risk that affects the entire market or market segment, undiversifiable.
- **Liquidity Risk**: The risk that an investment cannot be bought or sold quickly enough to prevent or minimize a loss.
- **Leverage Risk**: The risk of magnified losses due to the use of borrowed capital.
- **Counterparty Risk**: The risk that the other party in a financial contract will default on their obligations.
- **Operational Risk**: The risk of loss resulting from inadequate or failed internal processes, people, and systems.

By understanding and effectively managing these risks, investors can better navigate the complexities of alternative investments and enhance their potential for success.
