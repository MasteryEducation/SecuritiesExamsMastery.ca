## 20.4.1 Risk Related to Tracking Error

In the realm of alternative investments, understanding the concept of tracking error is crucial for both fund managers and investors. Tracking error represents the divergence between the performance of an investment and its benchmark index. This section delves into the definition, causes, impact, and strategies to minimize tracking error, providing a comprehensive understanding of its role in investment performance evaluation.

### Definition and Measurement

**Tracking Error** is defined as the standard deviation of the difference between the returns of an investment and its benchmark index. It is a critical metric used to assess how closely an investment follows its benchmark. The formula for calculating tracking error is:

{{< katex >}} \text{Tracking Error} = \sqrt{\frac{\sum_{i=1}^{n} (R_i - B_i)^2}{n}} {{< /katex >}}

Where:
- \\( R_i \\) is the return of the investment in period \\( i \\).
- \\( B_i \\) is the return of the benchmark in period \\( i \\).
- \\( n \\) is the number of periods.

Tracking error is significant because it helps investors understand the consistency of a fund's performance relative to its benchmark. A lower tracking error indicates that the fund closely follows its benchmark, while a higher tracking error suggests greater deviation.

### Causes of Tracking Error

Several factors contribute to tracking error, including:

1. **Management Fees:** These are costs associated with managing the fund, which can reduce returns and cause deviations from the benchmark.
   
2. **Trading Expenses:** Costs incurred during the buying and selling of securities can impact performance, leading to tracking error.

3. **Sampling Methods:** Some funds use sampling techniques instead of full replication of the benchmark, which can introduce discrepancies.

4. **Cash Drag:** Holding excess cash instead of being fully invested can negatively impact performance, causing tracking error.

5. **Portfolio Turnover Rates:** High turnover rates can lead to increased transaction costs and tax implications, affecting returns and increasing tracking error.

### Impact on Investors

Tracking error can significantly affect investor returns. In index-based funds, minimizing tracking error is crucial to ensure that the fund's performance aligns closely with the benchmark. In niche or illiquid markets, tracking error can be more pronounced due to factors like limited liquidity and higher transaction costs.

Investors should be aware of the potential for tracking error when evaluating fund performance. A fund with a high tracking error may not deliver the expected returns, impacting the investor's overall portfolio performance.

### Minimizing Tracking Error

Fund managers employ various strategies to reduce tracking error, including:

- **Efficient Sampling Techniques:** By carefully selecting a representative sample of the benchmark, managers can minimize discrepancies.

- **Cost Management:** Reducing management fees and trading expenses can help align fund performance with the benchmark.

- **Arbitrage:** This involves exploiting price differences between the fund's holdings and the benchmark to align prices with the Net Asset Value (NAV), minimizing discrepancies.

### Examples of Tracking Error

Consider a Canadian equity fund benchmarked against the S&P/TSX Composite Index. If the fund returns 8% over a year while the index returns 10%, the tracking error can be calculated using the formula provided earlier. This example illustrates how tracking error can impact fund performance and investor returns.

### Glossary

- **Tracking Error:** The deviation of an investment's returns from its benchmark index's returns.
- **Net Asset Value (NAV):** The value per share of a mutual fund or ETF on a specific date or time.
- **Cash Drag:** The negative impact on performance when a fund holds excess cash instead of being fully invested.

Understanding and managing tracking error is essential for optimizing investment performance and aligning with investor expectations. By employing effective strategies, fund managers can minimize tracking error, enhancing the fund's appeal to investors seeking consistent returns.
