## 23.6 Disadvantages of Structured Products

Structured products are sophisticated financial instruments that combine derivatives with traditional securities like bonds or equities. While they offer potential benefits such as customized risk-return profiles and access to otherwise inaccessible markets, they also come with significant disadvantages. This section delves into the complexities, liquidity issues, cost structures, and fees associated with structured products, providing a comprehensive understanding for Canadian investors.

### Complexity and Risk Assessment

One of the primary disadvantages of structured products is their inherent complexity. These products often involve intricate combinations of financial instruments, making it challenging for investors to fully understand the underlying risks. The complexity arises from the use of derivatives, which can include options, futures, or swaps, each with its own risk profile.

#### Difficulty in Assessing Risks

The complexity of structured products can obscure the true risk exposure. For instance, a structured product might promise capital protection with a potential upside linked to a stock index. However, the capital protection is often contingent on the creditworthiness of the issuer, introducing credit risk. Additionally, the derivative component can expose investors to market risks that are not immediately apparent.

**Example:** Consider a structured note issued by a major Canadian bank, such as RBC. The note offers a return linked to the S&P/TSX Composite Index but includes a cap on the maximum return. While the product might seem attractive, the cap limits the upside potential, and the investor bears the risk of the bank's creditworthiness.

### Liquidity and Secondary Market Challenges

Structured products often lack liquidity, meaning they cannot be easily bought or sold in the secondary market. This illiquidity can pose significant challenges for investors who need to access their funds before the product's maturity.

#### Limited Secondary Market

Unlike stocks or bonds, structured products are not typically traded on exchanges. Instead, they are sold over-the-counter (OTC), which can result in a lack of transparency and limited price discovery. This makes it difficult for investors to determine the fair market value of their investment at any given time.

**Case Study:** A Canadian pension fund invests in a structured product with a 5-year maturity. Midway through the term, the fund needs to liquidate its position due to changing investment strategies. However, the lack of a robust secondary market means the fund must sell at a significant discount, impacting its overall returns.

### Impact of Built-in Cost Structures on Returns

Structured products often come with complex cost structures that can erode potential returns. These costs are embedded in the product's design and can include management fees, performance fees, and other hidden charges.

#### Understanding Built-in Costs

The built-in costs of structured products can significantly impact the net returns to investors. These costs are often not transparent, making it difficult for investors to assess the true cost of their investment.

**Example:** A structured product might advertise a 10% potential return, but after accounting for management fees, performance fees, and other costs, the net return to the investor could be significantly lower. This discrepancy highlights the importance of understanding the total cost structure before investing.

### Fees Associated with Structured Products

Structured products typically involve various fees that can diminish the overall investment returns. These fees can include management fees, performance fees, and other administrative charges.

#### Management and Performance Fees

Management fees are charged for the ongoing management of the structured product, while performance fees are based on the product's performance relative to a benchmark. These fees can be substantial and are often deducted from the returns, reducing the overall profitability of the investment.

**Example:** A structured product offered by TD Bank includes a 2% management fee and a 20% performance fee on returns above a specified benchmark. These fees can significantly reduce the investor's net returns, especially if the product underperforms.

### Best Practices and Considerations

When considering structured products, investors should:

- **Thoroughly Understand the Product:** Take the time to understand the product's structure, underlying assets, and associated risks.
- **Evaluate Liquidity Needs:** Consider the potential need for liquidity and the implications of limited secondary market options.
- **Assess Cost Structures:** Carefully review all fees and costs associated with the product to understand their impact on potential returns.
- **Consult Financial Advisors:** Seek advice from financial professionals who can provide insights into the suitability of structured products within a broader investment strategy.

### Conclusion

Structured products can offer unique investment opportunities, but they also come with significant disadvantages. Their complexity, liquidity challenges, and cost structures can pose risks to investors. By understanding these disadvantages and considering best practices, Canadian investors can make informed decisions about incorporating structured products into their portfolios.
