## 23.7 Risks Involved with Structured Products

Structured products are complex financial instruments that combine derivatives with traditional securities like bonds or equities. While they offer unique benefits, such as tailored risk-return profiles and access to otherwise inaccessible markets, they also come with a set of inherent risks. Understanding these risks is crucial for investors, particularly in the Canadian context, where regulatory frameworks and market dynamics can influence investment outcomes.

### General Investment Risks

#### Default Risk

Default risk, also known as credit risk, refers to the possibility that the issuer of a structured product will be unable to meet its financial obligations. This risk is particularly relevant for products like collateralized debt obligations (CDOs) or mortgage-backed securities (MBS), where the underlying assets may include loans or mortgages. If borrowers default, the structured product may suffer losses, impacting the investor's returns.

**Example:** Consider a Canadian bank issuing a structured note linked to a basket of corporate bonds. If one of the corporations defaults, the note's value may decline, affecting the investor's return.

#### Inflation and Interest Rate Risk

Inflation risk is the danger that the purchasing power of returns will be eroded by rising prices. Interest rate risk involves the impact of fluctuating interest rates on the value of structured products. For instance, rising interest rates can decrease the value of fixed-income components within structured products, such as bonds.

**Example:** A structured product with a fixed coupon rate may become less attractive if Canadian interest rates rise, as newer issues offer higher returns, leading to a decline in the product's market value.

#### Currency Risk

Currency risk arises when structured products are denominated in a foreign currency. Fluctuations in exchange rates can affect the returns when converted back to Canadian dollars. This risk is particularly pertinent for Canadian investors holding structured products with international exposure.

**Example:** An investor in Canada holding a structured product tied to the U.S. dollar may see returns diminish if the Canadian dollar strengthens against the U.S. dollar.

#### Manager Risk

Manager risk involves the potential for poor performance due to the decisions made by the product's manager. This risk is significant in actively managed structured products, where the manager's expertise and strategy play a crucial role in achieving desired outcomes.

**Example:** A structured product managed by a Canadian investment firm may underperform if the manager's asset allocation decisions do not align with market conditions.

### Specific Risks in Structured Products

#### Prepayment Risk

Prepayment risk is specific to structured products like mortgage-backed securities (MBS). It occurs when borrowers repay their loans earlier than expected, leading to a return of principal to investors sooner than anticipated. This can affect the cash flow and overall yield of the investment.

**Example:** In a low-interest-rate environment, Canadian homeowners may refinance their mortgages, leading to prepayments in MBS. Investors may then need to reinvest the returned principal at lower prevailing rates, reducing potential returns.

### Impact on Overall Return on Investment

The aforementioned risks can significantly affect the overall return on investment for structured products. For instance, default risk can lead to a loss of principal, while interest rate and inflation risks can erode the real value of returns. Currency fluctuations can further impact the net returns for Canadian investors with international exposure. Manager risk can lead to suboptimal performance, affecting the expected risk-return profile.

### Strategies for Mitigating Risks

Investors can employ several strategies to mitigate the risks associated with structured products:

1. **Diversification:** By diversifying across different asset classes, sectors, and geographic regions, investors can reduce exposure to specific risks, such as default or currency risk.

2. **Hedging:** Using financial instruments like options or futures can help manage interest rate and currency risks. For example, Canadian investors can use currency hedges to protect against adverse exchange rate movements.

3. **Due Diligence:** Conducting thorough research on the issuer's creditworthiness and the manager's track record can help mitigate default and manager risks. Investors should also understand the underlying assets and structure of the product.

4. **Interest Rate Management:** Investors can choose structured products with features that adjust to interest rate changes, such as floating-rate notes, to mitigate interest rate risk.

5. **Professional Advice:** Consulting with financial advisors who understand the complexities of structured products and the Canadian market can provide valuable insights and tailored strategies.

### Conclusion

Structured products offer unique investment opportunities but come with a range of risks that can impact returns. By understanding these risks and employing effective mitigation strategies, investors can better navigate the complexities of structured products within the Canadian financial landscape. Continuous learning and adaptation to market changes are essential for optimizing investment outcomes.
