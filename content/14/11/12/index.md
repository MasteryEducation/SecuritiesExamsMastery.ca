## Glossary for Chapter 23: Structured Products

Structured products are complex financial instruments designed to meet specific investment needs that cannot be met from standard financial instruments available in the markets. They are typically created by investment banks and are often linked to the performance of an underlying asset, index, or portfolio. This glossary provides detailed explanations of key terms related to structured products, helping you understand their characteristics, benefits, and risks.

### Asset-Backed Securities (ABS)

**Definition:** Asset-Backed Securities are financial instruments backed by a pool of assets such as loans, leases, and receivables. These assets generate cash flows that are used to pay interest and principal to investors.

**Example:** A Canadian bank might issue an ABS backed by a pool of car loans. Investors in this ABS receive payments derived from the car loan repayments.

**Key Considerations:**
- **Credit Risk:** The risk that borrowers will default on their loans.
- **Prepayment Risk:** The risk that loans will be paid off early, affecting cash flows.

### Principal-Protected Notes (PPN)

**Definition:** Principal-Protected Notes are debt instruments that guarantee the return of principal at maturity, with potential interest based on the performance of an underlying asset.

**Example:** A PPN might be linked to the S&P/TSX Composite Index, offering investors the security of their initial investment with the potential for additional returns if the index performs well.

**Key Considerations:**
- **Participation Rate:** Determines how much of the asset's performance is captured.
- **Performance Cap:** Limits the maximum return.

### Participation Rate

**Definition:** The percentage of performance from an underlying asset that a structured product will capture.

**Example:** If a structured product has a participation rate of 80% and the underlying asset appreciates by 10%, the investor will receive an 8% return.

**Key Considerations:**
- **Impact on Returns:** Higher participation rates generally lead to higher potential returns.

### Performance Cap

**Definition:** The maximum return that a structured product will pay, regardless of how well the underlying asset performs.

**Example:** A structured product with a performance cap of 15% will not pay more than 15% even if the underlying asset appreciates by 20%.

**Key Considerations:**
- **Limitation on Gains:** Caps can limit the upside potential of an investment.

### Split Shares

**Definition:** Securities that separate the investment attributes of an underlying portfolio into preferred and capital shares.

**Example:** A split share corporation might issue preferred shares that receive fixed dividends and capital shares that benefit from capital appreciation.

**Key Considerations:**
- **Dividend Cuts:** Can impact returns on preferred shares.
- **Market Volatility:** Affects capital shares more significantly.

### Mortgage-Backed Securities (MBS)

**Definition:** Bonds that claim ownership of a portion of the cash flows from a pool of mortgages.

**Example:** The Canada Mortgage and Housing Corporation (CMHC) issues MBS backed by Canadian residential mortgages.

**Key Considerations:**
- **Prepayment Risk:** Early repayment of mortgages can affect returns.
- **Credit Risk:** Risk of borrower default.

### Zero-Coupon Bond

**Definition:** A bond that does not pay interest during its life and is issued at a discount to its face value.

**Example:** A zero-coupon bond might be issued for $800 and mature at $1,000, with the difference representing the investor's return.

**Key Considerations:**
- **Interest Rate Sensitivity:** More sensitive to interest rate changes than regular bonds.

### Special Purpose Vehicle (SPV)

**Definition:** A subsidiary created by a parent company to isolate financial risk.

**Example:** An investment bank might create an SPV to issue ABS, ensuring that the bank's financial risk is limited to the SPV.

**Key Considerations:**
- **Legal and Regulatory Compliance:** SPVs must adhere to specific regulations.

### Tranches

**Definition:** Different layers of securities within an ABS, each with varying levels of risk and return.

**Example:** An ABS might have senior, mezzanine, and equity tranches, with senior tranches having the lowest risk and return, and equity tranches having the highest.

**Key Considerations:**
- **Risk and Return Trade-off:** Investors choose tranches based on their risk tolerance.

### Roll-Over Risk

**Definition:** The risk that an issuer will not be able to refinance an investment at maturity.

**Example:** A company issuing a short-term bond may face roll-over risk if it cannot issue a new bond to repay the maturing one.

**Key Considerations:**
- **Market Conditions:** Adverse conditions can increase roll-over risk.

### Leverage

**Definition:** The use of borrowed capital to increase the potential return of an investment.

**Example:** An investor might use leverage to purchase more shares than they could with their own capital, amplifying both potential gains and losses.

**Key Considerations:**
- **Risk Amplification:** Leverage increases both potential returns and risks.

### Credit Risk

**Definition:** The risk that an issuer will default on their financial obligations.

**Example:** An investor in corporate bonds faces credit risk if the issuing company becomes insolvent.

**Key Considerations:**
- **Credit Ratings:** Help assess the creditworthiness of issuers.

### Liquidity Risk

**Definition:** The risk that an investment cannot be easily sold or exchanged for cash without a loss in value.

**Example:** An investor holding a large position in a thinly traded stock may face liquidity risk when trying to sell.

**Key Considerations:**
- **Market Depth:** Affects the ease of buying and selling securities.

### Prepayment Risk

**Definition:** The risk that a borrower will repay a loan before its scheduled maturity, affecting the returns on MBS.

**Example:** If interest rates fall, homeowners may refinance their mortgages, leading to prepayment risk for MBS investors.

**Key Considerations:**
- **Interest Rate Movements:** Influence prepayment rates.

### Dividend Cuts

**Definition:** Reductions in dividend payments, which can impact the returns on preferred shares.

**Example:** A company facing financial difficulties might reduce its dividend payments, affecting preferred shareholders.

**Key Considerations:**
- **Company Financial Health:** Affects the likelihood of dividend cuts.

### Yield-to-Call

**Definition:** The rate of return on a bond or similar security if it is held until the call date.

**Example:** A callable bond with a yield-to-call of 5% will provide that return if the issuer calls the bond before maturity.

**Key Considerations:**
- **Call Provisions:** Affect the bond's return profile.

### National Housing Act (NHA)

**Definition:** Canadian legislation that governs the mortgage industry, including MBS structures.

**Example:** The NHA provides the legal framework for CMHC to issue MBS.

**Key Considerations:**
- **Regulatory Compliance:** Ensures the stability of the mortgage market.

### Canada Mortgage and Housing Corporation (CMHC)

**Definition:** A government-owned corporation that provides insurance for Canadian mortgages.

**Example:** CMHC insures mortgages, reducing the risk for lenders and facilitating the issuance of MBS.

**Key Considerations:**
- **Government Backing:** Provides stability to the mortgage market.
