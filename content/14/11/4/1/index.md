## 23.4.1 Types of Structured Products

Structured products are financial instruments designed to facilitate highly customized risk-return objectives. They are typically created by combining traditional securities such as bonds and equities with derivatives. In this section, we will explore the various types of structured products commonly available in the Canadian market, including Principal-Protected Notes (PPNs), Market-Linked Guaranteed Investment Certificates (GICs), Split Shares, Mortgage-Backed Securities (MBS), and Asset-Backed Securities (ABS). Each of these products offers unique characteristics and investment objectives, catering to different investor needs.

### Principal-Protected Notes (PPNs)

**Characteristics and Investment Objectives:**

Principal-Protected Notes are structured products that guarantee the return of the principal amount invested, regardless of the performance of the underlying assets. They are typically linked to the performance of an index, a basket of stocks, or other financial instruments. The primary objective of PPNs is to provide investors with exposure to potential upside gains while protecting their initial investment from downside risk.

**Underlying Assets:**

PPNs can be linked to a variety of underlying assets, including equity indices, commodities, currencies, or interest rates. The performance of these underlying assets determines the return on the PPN at maturity.

**Example:**

Consider a PPN linked to the S&P/TSX Composite Index. If the index performs well over the investment period, the investor receives a return based on the index's performance. However, if the index performs poorly, the investor still receives the full principal amount at maturity.

### Market-Linked Guaranteed Investment Certificates (GICs)

**Characteristics and Investment Objectives:**

Market-Linked GICs are similar to traditional GICs but offer returns linked to the performance of a specific market index or a basket of assets. They provide the security of a guaranteed principal with the potential for higher returns than traditional fixed-rate GICs. The investment objective is to offer a balance between security and growth potential.

**Underlying Assets:**

These GICs are typically linked to equity indices, such as the S&P/TSX 60 or the S&P 500. The return is calculated based on the performance of these indices over the term of the GIC.

**Example:**

A Market-Linked GIC might be tied to the performance of the S&P/TSX 60 Index. If the index appreciates over the term, the investor receives a return based on that appreciation, up to a predetermined cap. If the index declines, the investor still receives the full principal amount.

### Split Shares

**Characteristics and Investment Objectives:**

Split Shares are structured products that divide the investment returns of a single share or a portfolio of shares into two separate components: capital shares and preferred shares. Capital shares provide leveraged exposure to the price appreciation of the underlying shares, while preferred shares offer fixed dividend payments. The investment objective is to cater to different investor preferences for income or capital gains.

**Underlying Assets:**

Split Shares are typically based on a portfolio of blue-chip stocks. The underlying assets determine the returns for both capital and preferred shares.

**Example:**

Consider a Split Share Corporation holding shares of major Canadian banks. Investors in capital shares benefit from any increase in the share prices of these banks, while investors in preferred shares receive regular dividend payments.

### Mortgage-Backed Securities (MBS)

**Characteristics and Investment Objectives:**

Mortgage-Backed Securities are structured products that pool together a collection of mortgages and sell them as a single security. Investors receive periodic payments derived from the principal and interest payments made by the borrowers of the underlying mortgages. The investment objective is to provide a steady income stream with relatively low risk.

**Underlying Assets:**

The underlying assets of MBS are residential or commercial mortgages. The performance of these securities depends on the credit quality of the borrowers and the interest rate environment.

**Example:**

A Canadian MBS might consist of a pool of residential mortgages issued by a major bank like RBC. Investors receive monthly payments based on the mortgage payments made by homeowners.

### Asset-Backed Securities (ABS)

**Characteristics and Investment Objectives:**

Asset-Backed Securities are similar to MBS but are backed by a pool of other types of loans, such as auto loans, credit card receivables, or student loans. The investment objective is to offer diversification and a steady income stream.

**Underlying Assets:**

The underlying assets for ABS can include a wide range of loan types, providing investors with exposure to different sectors of the economy.

**Example:**

An ABS might be backed by a pool of auto loans issued by a Canadian financial institution. Investors receive payments based on the interest and principal payments made by borrowers of these auto loans.

### Comparison of Underlying Assets

The underlying assets of structured products play a crucial role in determining their risk and return profiles. Here's a comparison of the underlying assets associated with each type of structured product:

- **PPNs and Market-Linked GICs:** Typically linked to equity indices or baskets of stocks, providing exposure to market performance.
- **Split Shares:** Based on a portfolio of shares, often blue-chip stocks, offering both income and capital appreciation potential.
- **MBS:** Comprised of residential or commercial mortgages, offering income from mortgage payments.
- **ABS:** Backed by various types of loans, providing diversification across different sectors.

### Conclusion

Structured products offer a diverse range of investment opportunities, each with unique characteristics and objectives. By understanding the different types of structured products and their underlying assets, investors can make informed decisions that align with their financial goals. Whether seeking principal protection, income, or growth, there is a structured product to meet the needs of Canadian investors.
