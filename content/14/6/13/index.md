## Glossary for Chapter 18: Mutual Funds—Types and Features

Understanding mutual funds and their various components is crucial for making informed investment decisions. This glossary provides clear definitions and explanations of key terms introduced in Chapter 18, organized alphabetically for easy reference. Use this as a tool to clarify complex mutual fund terminology and concepts, enhancing your ability to select, manage, and evaluate mutual funds effectively.

### Adjusted Cost Base (ACB)
The Adjusted Cost Base (ACB) is the original cost of an investment, adjusted for additional investments, reinvested distributions, and withdrawals. It is crucial for calculating capital gains or losses when selling mutual fund shares. For example, if you initially purchase shares for $1,000 and reinvest $200 in distributions, your ACB becomes $1,200. Understanding ACB helps investors accurately report taxable gains or losses.

### Alpha
Alpha measures a mutual fund’s performance relative to a benchmark, indicating the excess returns achieved above the expected return based on its beta. A positive alpha suggests that the fund manager has added value through active management. For instance, if a fund has an alpha of 2, it means the fund outperformed its benchmark by 2%. Alpha is essential for evaluating a fund manager's skill in generating returns beyond market movements.

### Anonymous Participant
An Anonymous Participant is an authorized individual or entity that can create and redeem ETF shares in the market. This role is crucial in maintaining the liquidity and pricing efficiency of ETFs. Anonymous Participants help ensure that ETF prices remain close to their net asset value (NAV) by arbitraging price discrepancies.

### Annual Information Form (AIF)
The Annual Information Form (AIF) is a comprehensive disclosure document providing detailed information about a mutual fund’s operations, holdings, and management. It includes data on the fund's investment strategies, risks, and financial performance. The AIF is essential for investors conducting due diligence before investing in a mutual fund.

### Asset Allocation
Asset Allocation refers to the distribution of investments across different asset classes, such as equities, bonds, and cash, to balance risk and return based on the investor’s goals. For example, a conservative investor might allocate 60% to bonds and 40% to equities. Effective asset allocation is a cornerstone of portfolio management, helping investors achieve their financial objectives while managing risk.

### Balanced Fund
A Balanced Fund is a mutual fund that invests in a mix of equities and fixed-income securities to provide both growth and income. This type of fund is suitable for investors seeking a moderate risk-return profile. For instance, a balanced fund might allocate 50% to stocks and 50% to bonds, offering diversification and stability.

### Benchmark Index
A Benchmark Index is an index used as a standard to compare the performance of a mutual fund. Common benchmarks include the S&P/TSX Composite Index for Canadian equities or the FTSE Canada Universe Bond Index for Canadian bonds. Benchmarking helps investors assess whether a fund is meeting its investment objectives.

### Capital Gain
A Capital Gain is the profit realized from selling mutual fund shares at a higher price than the purchase price. For example, if you buy shares for $1,000 and sell them for $1,200, you realize a capital gain of $200. Understanding capital gains is important for tax planning and evaluating investment performance.

### Capital Loss
A Capital Loss occurs when mutual fund shares are sold at a lower price than the purchase price. For instance, selling shares bought for $1,000 at $800 results in a capital loss of $200. Capital losses can be used to offset capital gains for tax purposes, reducing taxable income.

### Cash Equivalents
Cash Equivalents are short-term, highly liquid investments that are easily convertible to cash, such as Treasury bills or money market instruments. They provide safety and liquidity, making them suitable for conservative investors or as a component of a diversified portfolio.

### Custodian
A Custodian is an independent entity responsible for holding and safeguarding a mutual fund’s assets. The custodian ensures the security of the fund's investments and compliance with regulatory requirements. This role is critical for maintaining investor confidence and protecting assets from fraud or mismanagement.

### Dividend Tax Credit
The Dividend Tax Credit is a federal tax credit available to Canadian investors to offset taxes on dividends received from Canadian corporations. This credit reduces the effective tax rate on dividend income, making dividend-paying investments more attractive to Canadian investors.

### Exchange-Traded Fund (ETF)
An Exchange-Traded Fund (ETF) is a type of mutual fund traded on stock exchanges, holding a portfolio of assets like stocks, bonds, or commodities. ETFs offer diversification, liquidity, and typically lower fees compared to traditional mutual funds. They are suitable for investors seeking passive investment strategies.

### Front-End Load
A Front-End Load is a sales commission charged when purchasing mutual fund shares. This fee is deducted from the initial investment, reducing the amount invested in the fund. For example, a 5% front-end load on a $1,000 investment results in $950 being invested in the fund. Understanding load fees is important for evaluating the cost of investing in mutual funds.

### Fund Facts Document
The Fund Facts Document is a standardized two-page document providing key information about a mutual fund, including its investment objectives, risks, and costs. It helps investors make informed decisions by summarizing essential details in a concise format. The Fund Facts Document is a regulatory requirement in Canada, promoting transparency and investor protection.

### Modified Dietz Method
The Modified Dietz Method is a way to calculate the time-weighted rate of return by assigning weights to cash flows based on the duration they were held. This method provides a more accurate measure of investment performance, accounting for the timing of cash flows. It is useful for evaluating the performance of mutual funds with irregular cash flows.

### Money Market Fund
A Money Market Fund is a mutual fund investing in short-term, highly liquid securities to offer safety and liquidity. These funds are suitable for conservative investors seeking to preserve capital while earning a modest return. Money market funds typically invest in Treasury bills, commercial paper, and certificates of deposit.

### NAVPS (Net Asset Value per Share)
The Net Asset Value per Share (NAVPS) is the per-share value of a mutual fund’s assets minus its liabilities, used to price purchases and redemptions. For example, if a fund has $10 million in assets and $1 million in liabilities with 1 million shares outstanding, the NAVPS is $9. Understanding NAVPS is crucial for determining the value of mutual fund investments.

### No-Load Fund
A No-Load Fund is a mutual fund that does not charge any sales commissions or loads upon purchase or redemption of shares. These funds are attractive to cost-conscious investors, as they allow the entire investment amount to be allocated to the fund. No-load funds typically have lower expense ratios compared to load funds.

### Peer Group
A Peer Group is a collection of mutual funds with similar investment objectives and strategies used for comparative performance analysis. Comparing a fund to its peer group helps investors assess its relative performance and identify top-performing funds within a specific category.

### Ratio Withdrawal Plan
A Ratio Withdrawal Plan is a systematic withdrawal strategy where a fixed percentage of the portfolio is withdrawn each year. This approach provides a predictable income stream while preserving the remaining capital for future growth. Ratio withdrawal plans are popular among retirees seeking to manage their retirement income.

### Redemption Fee
A Redemption Fee is a charge applied when redeeming mutual fund shares before a specified holding period. This fee discourages short-term trading and helps protect long-term investors from the costs associated with frequent redemptions. Understanding redemption fees is important for planning investment horizons and minimizing costs.

### Sales Load
A Sales Load is a commission charged to investors when buying or selling mutual fund shares, typically in the form of front-end or back-end loads. These fees compensate financial advisors for their services but can significantly impact investment returns. Evaluating sales loads is essential for understanding the true cost of mutual fund investments.

### Sharpe Ratio
The Sharpe Ratio is a risk-adjusted performance measure that calculates the excess return per unit of volatility in an investment portfolio. A higher Sharpe ratio indicates better risk-adjusted performance. For example, a fund with a Sharpe ratio of 1.5 has generated 1.5 units of return for each unit of risk. The Sharpe ratio is a valuable tool for comparing the performance of mutual funds with different risk profiles.

### Standard Deviation
Standard Deviation is a statistical measure of the dispersion of returns for a given security or portfolio, indicating its volatility. A higher standard deviation suggests greater variability in returns, implying higher risk. Understanding standard deviation helps investors assess the risk associated with mutual fund investments.

### Target Date Fund
A Target Date Fund is a mutual fund that automatically adjusts its asset allocation based on a specified target retirement date. These funds gradually shift from higher-risk investments to more conservative ones as the target date approaches. Target date funds are suitable for investors seeking a hands-off approach to retirement planning.

### Time-Weighted Rate of Return (TWRR)
The Time-Weighted Rate of Return (TWRR) is a performance measurement that eliminates the impact of cash flows to assess the manager’s performance. TWRR provides a more accurate reflection of a fund manager's skill by isolating the effect of market movements from investor cash flows. It is widely used in the mutual fund industry for performance evaluation.

### Treasury Bills
Treasury Bills are short-term government securities with maturities of one year or less. They are considered low-risk investments, offering safety and liquidity. Treasury bills are commonly held in money market funds and are suitable for conservative investors seeking capital preservation.

### Trust Deed
The Trust Deed is the legal document outlining the mutual fund’s investment objectives, strategies, and restrictions. It serves as the governing document for the fund, ensuring compliance with regulatory requirements and protecting investor interests. Understanding the trust deed is important for evaluating a mutual fund's alignment with an investor's goals.

### Unit
A Unit is a share or portion of ownership in a mutual fund, representing a proportional claim on the fund’s assets. Units are bought and sold at the fund's NAVPS, allowing investors to participate in the fund's performance. Understanding units is essential for managing mutual fund investments and calculating returns.

By familiarizing yourself with these terms, you can enhance your understanding of mutual funds and make more informed investment decisions. Use this glossary as a reference tool to clarify complex concepts and improve your ability to select, manage, and evaluate mutual funds effectively.
