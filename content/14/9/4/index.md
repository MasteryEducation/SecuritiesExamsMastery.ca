## 21.4 Discuss Benchmarking of Alternative Investment Performance

In the realm of alternative investments, benchmarking serves as a critical tool for evaluating fund performance. Unlike traditional investments, alternative strategies often aim for absolute returns, making the selection of appropriate benchmarks both challenging and essential. This section delves into the nuances of benchmarking alternative investment performance, offering insights into the selection and application of benchmarks, and comparing them with conventional benchmarks used in traditional investment strategies.

### Understanding Benchmarks

**Benchmarking** in the context of alternative investments refers to the process of setting a standard against which the performance of an investment fund can be measured. This standard, or benchmark, is crucial for investors and fund managers alike, as it provides a reference point to assess whether the fund is meeting its investment objectives.

In traditional investments, benchmarks are often market indexes, such as the S&P/TSX Composite Index in Canada, which reflect the performance of a broad market or sector. However, alternative investments, which include hedge funds, private equity, and real estate, often pursue unique strategies that do not align with traditional market indexes. Therefore, selecting an appropriate benchmark is vital to accurately evaluate the performance of these funds.

### Performance Benchmark for Alternative Strategies

For many alternative strategy funds, the **zero rate of return** serves as the primary performance benchmark. This approach aligns with the objective of achieving absolute returns, where the goal is to generate positive returns regardless of market conditions. The rationale behind using a zero return benchmark is that it sets a clear, straightforward target: any positive return indicates success, while a negative return suggests underperformance.

This absolute return focus distinguishes alternative investments from traditional ones, where relative performance against a market index is often the primary concern. By using a zero return benchmark, alternative funds emphasize their ability to generate value independently of market movements.

### Selection of Appropriate Benchmarks

Selecting the right benchmark for an alternative investment fund involves understanding the fund's specific investment objectives and strategies. Here are some key considerations:

1. **Strategy Alignment:** The benchmark should reflect the fund's strategy. For example, a hedge fund employing a long/short equity strategy might use a zero return benchmark, while a real estate fund might benchmark against a real estate index.

2. **Risk Profile:** The benchmark should match the risk profile of the fund. A high-risk strategy may require a more aggressive benchmark, while a conservative strategy might align with a more stable benchmark.

3. **Market Conditions:** Consideration of current market conditions can influence benchmark selection. In volatile markets, a zero return benchmark might be more appropriate to emphasize capital preservation.

4. **Specific Indexes:** Some alternative funds may use specific indexes that align with their niche strategies. For instance, a commodity-focused fund might use a commodity index as a benchmark.

### Comparison with Conventional Benchmarks

The use of a zero return benchmark in alternative investments contrasts sharply with conventional benchmarks like market indexes used for mutual funds and hedge funds. Here are some key differences:

- **Objective:** Traditional benchmarks focus on relative performance, comparing a fund's returns to a market index. In contrast, alternative benchmarks often focus on absolute performance, aiming for positive returns regardless of market conditions.

- **Risk Management:** Alternative benchmarks emphasize risk-adjusted returns, often prioritizing downside protection and volatility management over outperforming a market index.

- **Flexibility:** Alternative benchmarks offer more flexibility, allowing funds to pursue diverse strategies without being tied to a specific market segment.

### Examples

Consider a Canadian hedge fund employing a market-neutral strategy. This fund might use a zero return benchmark to highlight its goal of generating positive returns in both rising and falling markets. By comparing its performance against this benchmark, the fund can demonstrate its ability to deliver value independently of market trends.

Another example is a private equity fund focused on Canadian technology startups. This fund might benchmark against a technology index to reflect its sector focus, while also considering a zero return benchmark to emphasize its absolute return objective.

The implications of benchmarking on fund performance analysis are significant. A well-chosen benchmark provides clarity and transparency, helping investors understand the fund's performance in the context of its objectives. It also aids in identifying areas for improvement and aligning fund strategies with investor expectations.

### Glossary

- **Benchmark:** A standard against which the performance of a security, mutual fund, or investment manager can be measured.
- **Zero Rate of Return:** The benchmark for alternative strategy funds, representing the performance objective of achieving positive returns.
- **Alpha:** The excess return of an investment relative to the return of a benchmark index.

### Conclusion

Benchmarking alternative investment performance is a nuanced process that requires careful consideration of the fund's objectives, strategies, and market conditions. By selecting appropriate benchmarks, fund managers can provide meaningful performance evaluations that align with investor expectations and highlight the unique value of alternative strategies. As the landscape of alternative investments continues to evolve, understanding and applying effective benchmarking practices will remain a critical component of successful fund management.
