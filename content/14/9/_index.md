## Chapter 21: Alternative Investments: Strategies and Performance

Alternative investments have become an integral part of modern portfolio management, offering diversification benefits and potential for enhanced returns. This chapter delves into the various strategies employed in alternative investments, how their performance is measured and benchmarked, the due diligence process, and their suitability for different investor groups. Understanding these elements is crucial for financial professionals navigating the Canadian investment landscape.

### Understanding Alternative Investment Strategies

Alternative investment strategies encompass a wide range of approaches that differ significantly from traditional investments like stocks and bonds. Here, we explore some of the most common strategies:

#### Relative Value Strategies

Relative value strategies aim to exploit price discrepancies between related financial instruments. These strategies often involve arbitrage opportunities, where investors take long and short positions to profit from price differentials. Examples include:

- **Convertible Arbitrage:** Involves buying convertible securities and hedging against the underlying stock.
- **Fixed-Income Arbitrage:** Focuses on exploiting pricing inefficiencies between fixed-income securities.

#### Event-Driven Strategies

Event-driven strategies capitalize on corporate events such as mergers, acquisitions, restructurings, or bankruptcies. These strategies include:

- **Merger Arbitrage:** Involves buying and selling stocks of companies involved in a merger or acquisition.
- **Distressed Securities:** Focuses on investing in companies undergoing financial distress or bankruptcy.

#### Directional Strategies

Directional strategies take advantage of market trends and movements. These strategies include:

- **Global Macro:** Involves taking positions based on macroeconomic trends across various markets.
- **Managed Futures:** Utilizes futures contracts to capitalize on expected price movements in commodity, currency, and interest rate markets.

### Performance Measurement

Evaluating the performance of alternative investments requires a nuanced approach, considering both risk and return. Key measures include:

#### Risk Measures

- **Standard Deviation:** Measures the volatility of returns, indicating the level of risk.
- **Value at Risk (VaR):** Estimates the potential loss in value of an investment over a defined period for a given confidence interval.

#### Risk-Adjusted Return Measures

- **Sharpe Ratio:** Assesses the return of an investment compared to its risk, calculated as the excess return per unit of risk.
- **Sortino Ratio:** Similar to the Sharpe Ratio but focuses only on downside risk, providing a more accurate measure for investments with asymmetric return distributions.

### Performance Benchmarking

Benchmarking alternative investments involves comparing their performance against relevant standards. This process is crucial for assessing the effectiveness of an investment strategy. Common benchmarks include:

- **Hedge Fund Indices:** Such as the HFRI Fund Weighted Composite Index, which tracks the performance of hedge funds globally.
- **Custom Benchmarks:** Tailored benchmarks that reflect the specific strategy and risk profile of the investment.

### Due Diligence Process

Conducting thorough due diligence is essential before investing in alternative strategies. The process involves:

1. **Manager Evaluation:** Assessing the track record, expertise, and investment philosophy of the fund manager.
2. **Operational Due Diligence:** Examining the fund's operational infrastructure, including compliance, risk management, and reporting practices.
3. **Legal and Regulatory Review:** Ensuring the fund complies with relevant Canadian regulations and legal requirements.

### Suitability for Investors

Alternative investments are not suitable for all investors. They are typically more appropriate for:

- **Institutional Investors:** Such as pension funds and endowments, which have the resources and risk tolerance to invest in complex strategies.
- **High-Net-Worth Individuals:** Who seek diversification and are willing to accept higher risk for potentially higher returns.

Investors should consider their investment objectives, risk tolerance, and liquidity needs before investing in alternative strategies.

### Glossary

- **Arbitrage:** The simultaneous purchase and sale of an asset to profit from a difference in the price.
- **Convertible Securities:** Bonds or preferred stocks that can be converted into a predetermined number of common stock shares.
- **Distressed Securities:** Financial instruments issued by a company that is near or currently going through bankruptcy.
- **Global Macro:** An investment strategy based on the analysis of macroeconomic trends.
- **Managed Futures:** An alternative investment strategy in which professional portfolio managers use futures contracts as part of their investment strategy.

### Conclusion

Alternative investments offer unique opportunities and challenges. By understanding the various strategies, performance measures, and due diligence processes, investors can make informed decisions that align with their financial goals. As the Canadian financial landscape continues to evolve, staying informed about alternative investments will be crucial for financial professionals and investors alike.
