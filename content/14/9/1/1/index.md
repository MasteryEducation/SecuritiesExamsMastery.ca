## 21.1.1 Relative Value Strategies

Relative value strategies are a sophisticated class of investment approaches that aim to exploit pricing inefficiencies between related securities. These strategies are particularly appealing to investors seeking to generate returns that are independent of broader market movements, focusing instead on the relative performance of securities. In the Canadian financial landscape, these strategies can be effectively applied to various asset classes, including equities, convertible bonds, and fixed-income securities.

### Equity Market-Neutral Strategies

#### Definition and Objective

Equity market-neutral strategies are designed to profit from the relative performance of selected equities while minimizing exposure to overall market movements. The primary goal is to achieve market neutrality, where the portfolio's returns are not correlated with the general market direction, thus isolating the alpha generated from stock selection.

#### Implementation Steps

1. **Identify Pairs of Related Stocks:**
   - Utilize fundamental valuation models to identify pairs of related stocks. For example, consider two Canadian banks like RBC and TD, which have similar business models and operate in the same economic environment.

2. **Establish Long and Short Positions:**
   - Take long positions in undervalued stocks and short positions in overvalued stocks. This involves buying shares of the bank perceived to be undervalued and short-selling shares of the bank perceived to be overvalued.

3. **Manage Net Exposure:**
   - Adjust the portfolio to maintain market neutrality. This involves balancing the long and short positions so that the net exposure to market movements is minimized.

#### Example

Consider a scenario where RBC is trading at a lower valuation multiple compared to TD, despite similar growth prospects. An investor might go long on RBC and short on TD, expecting RBC to outperform TD in the future.

#### Risk Management

To manage risk, diversification within pairs is crucial. This means selecting multiple pairs of stocks to reduce the impact of any single pair's performance. Additionally, controlling leverage is essential to prevent excessive exposure to any one position.

#### Performance Expectations

The expectation is to generate returns that are independent of market direction, relying on the alpha from stock selection. This strategy can be particularly effective in volatile markets where stock-specific factors drive performance.

### Convertible Arbitrage

#### Definition and Objective

Convertible arbitrage is a strategy that seeks to exploit pricing inefficiencies between convertible securities and their underlying stocks. Convertible bonds, which can be converted into a predetermined number of shares, often trade at prices that reflect both bond and equity characteristics.

#### Implementation Steps

1. **Purchase Convertible Bonds:**
   - Identify and purchase convertible bonds that are believed to be undervalued relative to their underlying stocks.

2. **Short Sell Underlying Stock:**
   - Short sell the equivalent amount of the underlying stock to hedge against equity risk. This involves selling shares of the stock that the bond can be converted into.

3. **Monitor Spread:**
   - Continuously monitor the spread between bond prices and stock prices to identify profit opportunities.

#### Risk Management

Maintaining balanced long and short positions is key to minimizing market risk. This involves regularly adjusting positions to ensure that the portfolio remains hedged against market movements.

#### Performance Expectations

Investors can earn returns from bond interest and potential capital appreciation while hedging against equity volatility. The strategy benefits from the bond's fixed income component and the potential for capital gains if the bond's price increases.

### Fixed-Income Arbitrage

#### Definition and Objective

Fixed-income arbitrage aims to profit from pricing inefficiencies in fixed-income securities. This strategy involves taking advantage of discrepancies in bond prices relative to their risk profiles.

#### Implementation Steps

1. **Identify Mispriced Bonds:**
   - Use sophisticated models to identify bonds that are mispriced relative to their risk profiles, such as interest rate or credit risk.

2. **Take Offsetting Positions:**
   - Establish offsetting long and short positions in related fixed-income securities or derivatives to exploit these inefficiencies.

3. **Hedge Risks:**
   - Hedge interest rate and credit risks to isolate arbitrage opportunities. This involves using derivatives or other financial instruments to mitigate exposure to these risks.

#### Risk Management

Managing exposure through sophisticated models is crucial. These models help in maintaining leverage within safe limits and ensuring that the portfolio is not overly exposed to any single risk factor.

#### Performance Expectations

The strategy aims to generate steady returns from adjustments in interest rates and credit spreads. By focusing on relative value rather than market direction, investors can achieve consistent performance.

### Glossary

- **Relative Value Strategy:** Aims to capitalize on price discrepancies between related securities.
- **Equity Market-Neutral Strategy:** Profits from relative performance without market exposure.
- **Convertible Arbitrage:** Exploits mispricing between convertible bonds and underlying stocks.
- **Fixed-Income Arbitrage:** Targets inefficiencies in the fixed-income market.

### Conclusion

Relative value strategies offer a compelling approach for investors seeking to exploit pricing inefficiencies while minimizing market exposure. By focusing on the relative performance of securities, these strategies can generate returns that are independent of broader market movements. In the Canadian context, these strategies can be effectively applied to various asset classes, providing investors with diverse opportunities to enhance their portfolios.
