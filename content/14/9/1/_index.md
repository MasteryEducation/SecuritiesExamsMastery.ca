## 21.1 Explain How the Various Types of Alternative Strategies Work

In the realm of finance, alternative investment strategies offer a diverse array of approaches to generating returns beyond traditional asset classes like stocks and bonds. These strategies are particularly valuable for their potential to enhance portfolio diversification, manage risk, and achieve superior returns. In this section, we will delve into the three primary categories of alternative strategies: relative value, event-driven, and directional. Each category presents unique opportunities and challenges, and understanding their mechanics is crucial for any finance professional or investor.

### Overview of Strategy Classifications

Alternative strategies can be broadly classified into three categories, each with distinct characteristics and risk-return profiles:

1. **Relative Value Strategies**: These strategies aim to exploit pricing inefficiencies between related securities. They are generally considered to have lower risk and return potential compared to other alternative strategies.

2. **Event-Driven Strategies**: These strategies focus on capitalizing on specific corporate events, such as mergers or bankruptcies. They offer moderate risk and return potential.

3. **Directional Strategies**: These strategies involve taking positions based on anticipated market movements. They typically carry higher risk and return potential.

Understanding these classifications helps investors align their strategies with their risk tolerance and investment objectives.

### Relative Value Strategies

Relative value strategies seek to profit from discrepancies in the pricing of related securities. These strategies are often market-neutral, meaning they aim to minimize exposure to broad market movements. Let's explore some common relative value strategies:

#### Equity Market-Neutral Strategy

An equity market-neutral strategy involves taking long and short positions in equities to exploit pricing inefficiencies while maintaining minimal exposure to overall market movements. For example, a Canadian hedge fund might go long on undervalued stocks of a Canadian bank like RBC while shorting overvalued stocks of another financial institution. The goal is to profit from the relative performance of these stocks, regardless of the market's direction.

#### Convertible Arbitrage

Convertible arbitrage involves exploiting mispricing between convertible securities (like convertible bonds) and their underlying stocks. Investors might buy a convertible bond and simultaneously short the underlying stock, aiming to profit from price discrepancies. This strategy requires careful analysis of the convertible's terms and the stock's volatility.

#### Fixed-Income Arbitrage

Fixed-income arbitrage targets inefficiencies in the pricing of fixed-income securities. For instance, an investor might exploit yield curve anomalies by taking offsetting positions in government bonds with different maturities. This strategy often involves complex mathematical models to identify and capitalize on pricing inefficiencies.

### Event-Driven Strategies

Event-driven strategies focus on profiting from specific corporate events. These strategies require a keen understanding of corporate finance and the ability to assess the likelihood and impact of various events.

#### Merger (Risk) Arbitrage Strategy

Merger arbitrage, also known as risk arbitrage, involves investing in companies involved in mergers or acquisitions. Typically, an investor buys the target company's stock and shorts the acquiring company's stock, betting on the successful completion of the deal. In Canada, this might involve analyzing mergers in the mining sector, a significant part of the Canadian economy.

#### Distressed Securities Strategy

This strategy involves investing in companies experiencing financial distress or bankruptcy. Investors buy distressed securities at a discount, hoping to profit from a turnaround or restructuring. For example, a Canadian investor might focus on distressed oil and gas companies, given the sector's volatility.

#### High-Yield Bond Strategy

High-yield bond strategies focus on investing in below-investment-grade debt securities, often issued by companies with higher default risk. These bonds offer higher yields to compensate for the increased risk. Canadian investors might consider high-yield bonds from domestic energy companies, balancing risk with potential returns.

### Directional Strategies

Directional strategies involve taking positions based on anticipated market movements. These strategies are generally more speculative and require a strong understanding of market trends and economic indicators.

#### Long/Short Equity Strategy

A long/short equity strategy involves taking long positions in stocks expected to rise and short positions in stocks expected to fall. This strategy allows investors to profit from both upward and downward market movements. For instance, a Canadian investor might go long on technology stocks while shorting traditional retail stocks, anticipating shifts in consumer behavior.

#### Global Macro Strategy

Global macro strategies are based on macroeconomic trends across global markets. Investors analyze economic indicators, geopolitical events, and monetary policies to make informed investment decisions. A Canadian investor might focus on global commodity prices, given Canada's significant resource exports.

#### Emerging Markets Strategy

Emerging markets strategies involve investing in securities from developing economies. These markets often offer higher growth potential but come with increased volatility and risk. Canadian investors might look at emerging markets in Asia or Latin America, considering factors like economic growth and political stability.

#### Dedicated Short Bias Strategy

A dedicated short bias strategy involves maintaining a net short exposure to the market. This strategy profits from declining markets and requires careful risk management. Canadian investors might use this strategy during economic downturns or market corrections.

#### Managed Futures Strategy

Managed futures strategies utilize futures contracts to capitalize on market trends. These strategies often involve systematic trading models and can be applied to various asset classes, including commodities, currencies, and interest rates. A Canadian investor might use managed futures to hedge against currency fluctuations or commodity price changes.

### Practical Examples and Case Studies

To illustrate these strategies, consider the following examples:

- **Case Study: Canadian Pension Fund**: A Canadian pension fund employs a relative value strategy by investing in convertible bonds of major Canadian corporations while shorting their stocks. This approach aims to generate steady returns with minimal market exposure.

- **Example: Merger Arbitrage in the Mining Sector**: A Canadian hedge fund identifies a merger opportunity between two mining companies. By analyzing the deal's terms and potential regulatory hurdles, the fund profits from the successful completion of the merger.

- **Scenario: Global Macro Strategy in Commodities**: A Canadian investor uses a global macro strategy to invest in futures contracts for oil and natural gas, anticipating price increases due to geopolitical tensions in the Middle East.

### Best Practices and Common Pitfalls

When implementing alternative strategies, consider the following best practices:

- **Thorough Research**: Conduct comprehensive research and analysis to identify opportunities and assess risks.

- **Risk Management**: Implement robust risk management techniques to protect against adverse market movements.

- **Diversification**: Diversify across strategies and asset classes to reduce overall portfolio risk.

- **Regulatory Compliance**: Ensure compliance with Canadian financial regulations and reporting requirements.

Common pitfalls to avoid include over-leveraging, inadequate due diligence, and failing to adapt to changing market conditions.

### Conclusion

Alternative investment strategies offer a wide range of opportunities for investors seeking to diversify their portfolios and enhance returns. By understanding the mechanics of relative value, event-driven, and directional strategies, investors can make informed decisions that align with their risk tolerance and investment goals. As the Canadian financial landscape continues to evolve, staying informed and adaptable is crucial for success.
