## 21.3 Discuss Risk Measures and Risk-Adjusted Return Measures to Alternative Strategy Fund Investments

In the realm of alternative strategy fund investments, understanding and managing risk is paramount. This section delves into the various risk measures and risk-adjusted return metrics that are crucial for evaluating these investments, particularly within the Canadian financial landscape. By comprehending these concepts, investors can make informed decisions that align with their risk tolerance and investment goals.

### Understanding Risk Measures

Risk measures are essential tools that help investors assess the potential variability in returns of an investment. For alternative strategy funds, these measures provide insights into the inherent risks and help in comparing different investment opportunities.

#### Absolute Risk

Absolute risk refers to the total variability or volatility of returns, encompassing all sources of risk without distinguishing between upside and downside. It provides a holistic view of the potential fluctuations in an investment's value.

#### Standard Deviation

**Definition:** Standard deviation is a statistical measure that quantifies the dispersion of returns around the mean. It is a widely used metric for assessing the risk of an investment.

**Calculation and Interpretation:** Standard deviation is calculated by taking the square root of the variance, which is the average of the squared differences from the mean. A higher standard deviation indicates greater variability in returns, suggesting higher risk.

**Application in Alternative Strategies:** While standard deviation is a useful measure, it has limitations when applied to alternative strategies, which often exhibit non-normally distributed returns. These strategies may have skewed or kurtotic return distributions, making standard deviation less effective in capturing the true risk profile.

### Return Distributions

Understanding the distribution of returns is crucial for interpreting risk measures accurately.

#### Normal Distribution

A normal distribution is characterized by a bell-shaped curve, where most returns cluster around the mean. It assumes symmetry and a predictable pattern of variability.

#### Non-Normal Distribution

Alternative strategy funds often exhibit non-normal distributions, characterized by skewness and kurtosis. These distributions can have significant implications for risk assessment.

- **Skew:** Measures the asymmetry of a return distribution. Positive skew indicates a tendency towards higher returns, while negative skew suggests a tendency towards lower returns.
- **Kurtosis:** Measures the "tailedness" of a return distribution. High kurtosis indicates more extreme returns (fat tails), while low kurtosis suggests fewer extreme returns.

**Impact on Risk Measures:** Skew and kurtosis affect the interpretation of standard deviation and other risk measures. For instance, a high kurtosis may imply that standard deviation underestimates the probability of extreme losses.

### Downside Risk

Downside risk focuses on the negative volatility or risk of loss, addressing the limitations of standard deviation, which treats upside and downside volatility equally.

#### Drawdown Measures

Drawdown measures are critical for assessing downside risk in alternative strategy funds.

- **Drawdown:** The peak-to-trough decline in fund value.
- **Maximum Drawdown:** The largest drawdown over a specific period.
- **Time to Recovery:** The time taken to recover from a drawdown to reach a new peak.

These metrics are vital for evaluating the risk profile of alternative strategy funds, as they highlight the potential for significant losses and the time required to recover.

### Risk-Adjusted Returns

Risk-adjusted return measures provide insights into the efficiency of an investment in generating returns relative to the risk taken.

#### Sharpe Ratio

**Definition:** The Sharpe ratio measures excess returns relative to the risk taken, using standard deviation as the risk metric.

**Calculation and Interpretation:** The Sharpe ratio is calculated by subtracting the risk-free rate from the portfolio's return and dividing the result by the standard deviation. A higher Sharpe ratio indicates better risk-adjusted performance.

**Application in Comparing Funds:** The Sharpe ratio facilitates comparison of risk-adjusted performance across different funds, allowing investors to identify those that offer the best returns for the level of risk assumed.

#### Other Measures

In addition to the Sharpe ratio, several other risk-adjusted return metrics are used to evaluate alternative strategy funds:

- **Jensen’s Alpha:** Measures a portfolio's performance relative to its expected return based on its beta.
- **Sortino Ratio:** Focuses only on downside risk, providing a more accurate assessment of risk-adjusted returns for investments with asymmetric return distributions.
- **Calmar Ratio:** Compares annualized returns to maximum drawdown, emphasizing the importance of managing downside risk.
- **Sterling Ratio:** Similar to the Calmar ratio but includes average drawdowns, offering a comprehensive view of risk-adjusted performance.

### Performance Benchmark

For alternative strategy funds, the performance benchmark is often defined as the zero rate of return. This benchmark aligns with the objective of generating absolute returns, emphasizing the importance of achieving positive performance regardless of market conditions.

### Glossary

- **Absolute Risk:** Total variability of returns without distinguishing between upside and downside.
- **Standard Deviation:** A measure of dispersion of returns around the mean.
- **Skew:** Asymmetry of a return distribution.
- **Kurtosis:** Tailedness of a return distribution.
- **Drawdown:** Peak-to-trough decline in fund value.
- **Maximum Drawdown:** The largest drawdown over a specific period.
- **Time to Recovery:** The time taken to recover from a drawdown to reach a new peak.
- **Sharpe Ratio:** A measure of risk-adjusted return comparing excess returns to standard deviation.
- **Jensen’s Alpha:** A measure of a portfolio's performance relative to its expected return based on its beta.
- **Sortino Ratio:** A risk-adjusted return measure focusing only on downside risk.
- **Calmar Ratio:** A ratio comparing annualized returns to maximum drawdown.
- **Sterling Ratio:** Similar to the Calmar ratio but includes average drawdowns.

### Practical Examples and Case Studies

To illustrate these concepts, consider a Canadian pension fund investing in alternative strategy funds. By analyzing the standard deviation, skew, and kurtosis of the fund's returns, the pension fund can assess the risk profile and determine the suitability of the investment for its portfolio. Additionally, by calculating the Sharpe ratio and other risk-adjusted return metrics, the fund can compare the performance of different alternative strategy funds and select those that align with its risk tolerance and return objectives.

### Conclusion

Understanding risk measures and risk-adjusted return metrics is crucial for evaluating alternative strategy fund investments. By comprehensively assessing these factors, investors can make informed decisions that align with their financial goals and risk tolerance. As the Canadian financial landscape continues to evolve, staying informed about these concepts will empower investors to navigate the complexities of alternative investments effectively.
