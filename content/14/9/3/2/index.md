## 21.3.2 Standard Deviation

In the realm of finance, understanding risk is crucial for making informed investment decisions. Standard deviation is a fundamental statistical measure used to quantify the risk associated with investment returns. In this section, we will delve into the definition, calculation, interpretation, and application of standard deviation, particularly in the context of alternative investment strategies. We will also discuss its limitations and provide practical examples to enhance your understanding.

### Definition and Calculation

**Standard Deviation** is a statistical measure that quantifies the dispersion of a set of data points around their mean. In finance, it is commonly used to measure the volatility of an investment's returns. A higher standard deviation indicates a wider range of returns, suggesting greater volatility and risk.

To calculate the standard deviation of an investment's historical returns, follow these steps:

1. **Calculate the Mean Return:** Sum all the historical returns and divide by the number of observations.
2. **Determine Deviations from the Mean:** Subtract the mean return from each individual return to find the deviation for each period.
3. **Square the Deviations:** Square each deviation to eliminate negative values.
4. **Calculate the Variance:** Sum all the squared deviations and divide by the number of observations minus one (for a sample).
5. **Compute the Standard Deviation:** Take the square root of the variance.

This formula can be expressed as:

{{< katex >}}
\sigma = \sqrt{\frac{\sum (R_i - \bar{R})^2}{N-1}}
{{< /katex >}}

Where:
- \\(\sigma\\) is the standard deviation.
- \\(R_i\\) is each individual return.
- \\(\bar{R}\\) is the mean return.
- \\(N\\) is the number of observations.

### Interpretation

The standard deviation provides insights into the risk profile of an investment. Here's how to interpret different levels of standard deviation:

- **Low Standard Deviation:** Indicates that the returns are closely clustered around the mean, suggesting lower volatility and risk.
- **High Standard Deviation:** Signifies that the returns are spread out over a wider range, indicating higher volatility and risk.

For example, consider two funds: Fund A with a standard deviation of 5% and Fund B with a standard deviation of 15%. Fund B is more volatile and riskier than Fund A, as its returns fluctuate more widely around the mean.

### Application in Alternative Strategies

Alternative investment strategies, such as hedge funds, private equity, and real estate, often exhibit non-normally distributed returns. Standard deviation remains a valuable tool for assessing risk in these contexts, but it requires careful interpretation.

- **Relevance:** Standard deviation helps investors understand the variability of returns in alternative strategies, which may not follow a normal distribution.
- **Annualization:** To compare standard deviations across different time frames, it is common to annualize the measure. This is done by multiplying the standard deviation by the square root of the number of periods in a year (e.g., \\(\sqrt{12}\\) for monthly returns).

### Limitations

While standard deviation is a widely used risk measure, it has limitations, particularly for alternative investments:

- **Symmetric Penalty:** Standard deviation penalizes both upside and downside volatility equally. This can be misleading for strategies that aim to capture upside volatility.
- **Non-Normal Distributions:** Many alternative strategies exhibit skewed or kurtotic return distributions, which standard deviation does not fully capture.

### Practical Example

Consider a Canadian pension fund investing in a hedge fund with the following monthly returns over a year: 2%, -1%, 3%, -2%, 5%, -3%, 4%, -1%, 2%, 0%, 3%, -2%.

1. **Calculate the Mean Return:** (2 - 1 + 3 - 2 + 5 - 3 + 4 - 1 + 2 + 0 + 3 - 2) / 12 = 1.08%
2. **Determine Deviations and Square Them:** For each return, subtract the mean and square the result.
3. **Calculate the Variance and Standard Deviation:** Follow the steps outlined earlier to find the standard deviation.

This process will reveal the fund's volatility, aiding the pension fund in assessing the risk associated with this investment.

### Glossary

- **Standard Deviation:** A measure of the dispersion of returns around the mean, indicating the investment’s volatility.

### Conclusion

Standard deviation is a crucial tool for assessing the risk of investment returns, particularly in alternative strategies. While it provides valuable insights into volatility, it is important to consider its limitations and complement it with other risk measures for a comprehensive analysis.
