## 21.7 Describe the Due Diligence Process That Should Be Conducted When Contemplating Investment in an Alternative Strategy Fund

Investing in alternative strategy funds can offer diversification and potentially higher returns, but it also involves unique risks and complexities. Conducting thorough due diligence is crucial to understanding these risks and ensuring that the investment aligns with your financial goals. This section outlines a comprehensive due diligence framework, focusing on eight primary areas of inquiry.

### Comprehensive Due Diligence Framework

Due diligence is a systematic process of evaluating an investment opportunity to mitigate risks and ensure its suitability for your portfolio. It involves a detailed examination of the fund's structure, management, operations, and performance. By following a structured approach, investors can make informed decisions and avoid potential pitfalls.

### Eight Primary Areas of Inquiry

#### 1. Structure of the Investment Management Organization

Understanding the organization behind the fund is essential. This involves assessing the background, experience, and governance of the firm and its key personnel. Key considerations include:

- **Background and Experience:** Investigate the history and track record of the management team. Have they successfully managed similar funds in the past?
- **Governance:** Evaluate the firm's governance structure. Are there clear lines of accountability and oversight?
- **Stability and Expertise:** Consider the stability of the management team. High turnover can be a red flag. Also, check if the managers have personal investments in the fund, which can indicate alignment of interests.

#### 2. Investment Management Information

The fund's investment strategy and the manager's experience are critical factors. Key points include:

- **Assets Under Management (AUM):** Review the size of the fund's AUM. Larger funds may have more resources but could also face challenges in deploying capital effectively.
- **Investment Strategy:** Examine the uniqueness of the fund’s investment approach. Is it innovative, and does it align with the fund's performance claims?
- **Manager's Experience:** Assess the manager's expertise in executing the strategy. Have they navigated similar market conditions successfully?

#### 3. Risk Analysis

Risk management is a cornerstone of successful investing. Evaluate the fund's approach to risk:

- **Risk Identification and Quantification:** How does the fund identify and measure risks? Are there robust systems in place?
- **Stress Testing:** Does the fund conduct regular stress tests to evaluate its resilience under adverse conditions?
- **Risk Management Processes:** Assess how the fund manages exposure to various risk factors, such as market, credit, and liquidity risks.

#### 4. Operations

Operational efficiency and reliability are crucial for fund success. Consider the following:

- **Service Providers:** Analyze the reputation and reliability of the fund's service providers, including prime brokers, custodians, administrators, auditors, and legal counsel.
- **Operational Infrastructure:** Evaluate the fund’s systems, record-keeping, and compliance practices. Are they robust and scalable?
- **Compliance:** Ensure that the fund adheres to regulatory requirements and best practices.

#### 5. Fund Structure

The fund's structure can impact its tax treatment and liquidity. Key considerations include:

- **Offering Documents:** Examine the form of the offering, such as a prospectus or offering memorandum. What are the tax implications?
- **Redemption Terms:** Assess the fund’s redemption terms, fee structures, and any restrictions on redemptions. Are they investor-friendly?

#### 6. Investment Performance

Analyzing past performance can provide insights into future potential. Consider:

- **Historical Performance:** Review the fund’s historical performance, including consistency, drawdowns, and risk-adjusted returns.
- **Benchmark Comparison:** Compare the fund’s performance against appropriate benchmarks and peer groups. Is it competitive?

#### 7. Account Structure and Composition

Understanding the fund's investor base is important for assessing stability:

- **Client Base:** Review the distribution of assets among different investor types. Is there a concentration of ownership?
- **Impact of Large Investors:** Evaluate how large investors might affect the fund's stability and performance. Could their actions lead to significant redemptions?

#### 8. Fees

Fees can significantly impact net returns. Scrutinize the fee structure:

- **Management and Performance Fees:** What are the management and performance fees? Are they justified by the fund's performance?
- **Alignment of Interests:** Assess how fees impact the alignment of interests between managers and investors. Are there any hidden costs?

### Detailed Evaluation Process

For each area, specific questions and points of analysis can guide the due diligence process. Here are some examples:

- **Managerial Claims:** Verify the accuracy of the manager's claims about their strategy and performance.
- **Strategy Specifics:** Understand the specifics of the investment strategy. How does it generate returns?
- **Operational Robustness:** Assess the robustness of the fund's operations. Are there any weaknesses that could lead to operational failures?

### Examples

Consider a hypothetical scenario where a Canadian pension fund is evaluating an alternative strategy fund. The pension fund's investment committee might ask:

- Does the fund manager have experience managing similar assets during economic downturns?
- How does the fund's performance compare to similar funds managed by major Canadian banks like RBC or TD?
- Are the fund's redemption terms aligned with the pension fund's liquidity needs?

### Glossary

- **Due Diligence:** A comprehensive appraisal of an investment undertaken by a prospective buyer to establish its assets, liabilities, and evaluate its potential.
- **Investment Committee:** A group responsible for making investment decisions and overseeing portfolio management.
- **Prime Broker:** A financial institution providing services like lending securities and facilitating trades for hedge funds.
- **Custodian:** A financial institution that holds and safeguards a fund’s assets.
- **Administrator:** An entity responsible for calculating and publishing the fund’s NAV and maintaining records.
- **Offering Memorandum:** A document providing detailed information about an investment fund’s operations for regulatory compliance.
- **Net Asset Value (NAV):** The value per share of a mutual fund or ETF on a specific date or time.

### Conclusion

Conducting thorough due diligence is essential when contemplating investment in an alternative strategy fund. By systematically evaluating the fund's structure, management, operations, and performance, investors can make informed decisions that align with their financial goals. Remember to verify managerial claims, understand strategy specifics, and assess operational robustness to mitigate risks effectively.
