## 21.6 Discuss Benchmarking of Alternative Investment Performance

In the dynamic world of alternative investments, benchmarking plays a crucial role in evaluating fund performance. Unlike traditional investments, alternative strategies often pursue absolute returns, making the selection of appropriate benchmarks both challenging and essential. This section delves into the nuances of benchmarking alternative investment performance, offering insights into the selection of benchmarks, the use of composite benchmarks, and the integration of performance evaluation metrics.

### Defining Appropriate Benchmarks

Selecting the right benchmark is vital for accurately assessing the performance of alternative strategy funds. A benchmark serves as a standard against which the performance of a fund can be measured. For alternative investments, which often employ diverse and complex strategies, the choice of benchmark must reflect the fund's objectives and strategies.

#### Importance of Selecting Appropriate Benchmarks

- **Alignment with Fund Objectives:** The benchmark should align with the fund's investment objectives, whether they aim for absolute returns, relative returns, or a combination of both.
- **Representation of Strategy Diversity:** Alternative funds often employ multiple strategies, such as long/short equity, global macro, or event-driven strategies. A single benchmark may not capture this diversity, necessitating the use of composite benchmarks.

#### Use of Composite Benchmarks

Composite benchmarks are constructed from multiple indexes to represent the diverse strategies within alternative funds. They provide a more comprehensive view of performance by combining various strategy-specific benchmarks.

- **Example:** A composite benchmark for a multi-strategy fund might include components from relative value, event-driven, and directional strategy benchmarks. This approach ensures that the benchmark reflects the fund's overall strategy mix.

### Zero Rate of Return Benchmark

The zero rate of return is often used as a primary benchmark for alternative strategy funds. This benchmark aligns with the objective of achieving absolute returns, which is a common goal for many alternative investments.

#### Comparison with Traditional Benchmarks

- **Zero Return Benchmark:** Focuses on achieving positive returns regardless of market conditions. It is particularly relevant for hedge funds and other alternative strategies that aim to generate returns independent of market movements.
- **Traditional Benchmarks:** Typically used by mutual funds, these benchmarks are often based on market indexes (e.g., S&P/TSX Composite Index) and focus on relative performance compared to the market.

### Composite Benchmarks

Composite benchmarks offer a nuanced approach to benchmarking by combining multiple indexes to reflect the multi-strategy nature of alternative funds.

#### Construction of Composite Benchmarks

- **Selection of Indexes:** Choose indexes that represent the various strategies employed by the fund. For instance, a fund using both equity and fixed income strategies might combine equity and bond indexes.
- **Weighting of Components:** Determine the appropriate weighting for each component based on the fund's strategy allocation. This ensures that the composite benchmark accurately reflects the fund's investment approach.

### Performance Evaluation Metrics

Benchmarking is most effective when used in conjunction with performance evaluation metrics, such as risk-adjusted return measures.

#### Risk-Adjusted Return Measures

- **Sharpe Ratio:** A widely used metric that compares excess returns to the standard deviation of returns. It provides insight into the risk-adjusted performance of a fund.
- **Role of Benchmarking:** By comparing a fund's performance against its benchmark, investors can identify areas of strength and weakness, informing investment decisions and portfolio adjustments.

### Examples of Benchmarking

Benchmarking provides valuable insights into the performance of alternative strategy funds, influencing investment decision-making and portfolio management.

#### Case Study: Canadian Pension Fund

Consider a Canadian pension fund employing a multi-strategy approach, including long/short equity and global macro strategies. By using a composite benchmark that combines relevant equity and macroeconomic indexes, the fund can assess its performance across different market conditions.

- **Insights Gained:** The fund can identify which strategies are contributing positively to performance and which may require adjustments.
- **Impact on Decision-Making:** Benchmarking informs decisions on strategy allocation, risk management, and potential rebalancing of the portfolio.

### Glossary

- **Benchmark:** A standard against which the performance of a security, mutual fund, or investment manager can be measured.
- **Zero Rate of Return:** The benchmark for alternative strategy funds, representing the performance objective of achieving positive returns.
- **Composite Benchmark:** A combined index representing multiple strategies or asset classes within a fund.
- **Sharpe Ratio:** A measure of risk-adjusted return comparing excess returns to standard deviation.

### Conclusion

Benchmarking alternative investment performance is a complex but essential task. By selecting appropriate benchmarks, utilizing composite benchmarks, and integrating performance evaluation metrics, investors can gain a comprehensive understanding of fund performance. This knowledge is crucial for making informed investment decisions and optimizing portfolio management.
