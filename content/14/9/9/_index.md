## 21.9 Describe the Due Diligence Process That Should Be Conducted When Contemplating Investment in an Alternative Strategy Fund

Investing in alternative strategy funds can offer significant opportunities for diversification and enhanced returns. However, these investments also come with unique risks and complexities that require thorough evaluation. Due diligence is a critical process that helps investors assess the potential risks and returns of these funds, ensuring informed investment decisions.

### Introduction to Due Diligence

Due diligence is the comprehensive appraisal of an investment, undertaken by a prospective investor to establish its assets, liabilities, and evaluate its potential. In the context of alternative strategy funds, due diligence is essential for understanding the intricacies of the fund's operations, strategies, and risk management practices. By conducting thorough due diligence, investors can identify potential red flags, verify the fund's claims, and ensure alignment with their investment objectives.

### Eight Primary Areas of Inquiry

To conduct effective due diligence, investors should focus on eight primary areas of inquiry:

#### 1. Structure of the Investment Management Organization

Understanding the structure of the investment management organization is crucial. This involves assessing the firm's origins, ownership structure, regulatory registration, and the background of key personnel. Key considerations include:

- **Firm Origins and Ownership:** Investigate the history and ownership structure of the firm. Is it privately held or publicly traded? What is the firm's reputation in the industry?
- **Regulatory Registration:** Ensure the firm is registered with relevant regulatory bodies, such as the Canadian Investment Regulatory Organization (CIRO).
- **Key Personnel:** Evaluate the experience, stability, and governance practices of the investment team. Consider the track record and expertise of the fund managers.

#### 2. Investment Management Information

Reviewing the fund's investment management information provides insights into its strategies and performance. Key points include:

- **Assets Under Management (AUM):** Examine the fund's AUM and growth trends. A growing AUM may indicate investor confidence, while a declining AUM could signal potential issues.
- **Investment Strategies:** Analyze the uniqueness of the investment approach and its alignment with performance claims. Does the strategy differentiate itself from competitors?

#### 3. Risk Analysis

Risk management is a critical component of due diligence. Investors should evaluate the fund's risk management frameworks, including:

- **Diversification and Leverage:** Assess how the fund diversifies its investments and uses leverage. Excessive leverage can amplify risks.
- **Derivatives Usage:** Understand the fund's use of derivatives and how they contribute to risk management.
- **Stress Testing:** Evaluate how the fund conducts stress tests and manages exposure to various risk factors.

#### 4. Operations

Operational infrastructure is vital for the smooth functioning of a fund. Key considerations include:

- **Service Providers:** Analyze the reputation and reliability of service providers, such as prime brokers, custodians, administrators, auditors, and legal counsel.
- **Operational Systems:** Evaluate systems for trading, compliance, and record-keeping. Robust systems are essential for minimizing operational risks.

#### 5. Fund Structure

The structure of the fund affects its liquidity and tax treatment. Important aspects include:

- **Offering Documents:** Examine the form of the offering, such as a prospectus or offering memorandum, and its implications for investors.
- **Redemption Terms:** Assess the fund’s redemption terms, fee structures, and any restrictions or conditions on redemptions.

#### 6. Investment Performance

Analyzing historical performance metrics helps investors gauge the fund's success. Consider:

- **Performance Metrics:** Review returns, volatility, and risk-adjusted ratios. Compare the fund’s performance against relevant benchmarks and peer groups.

#### 7. Account Structure and Composition

Understanding the fund's account structure and composition provides insights into its stability. Key points include:

- **Investor Distribution:** Review the distribution of assets among different investor types, such as individuals and institutions.
- **Asset Concentration:** Assess the concentration of assets among top investors and the implications for fund stability.

#### 8. Fees

Fees can significantly impact net returns. Investors should scrutinize:

- **Fee Structure:** Examine the management fee, performance fee, and any other costs associated with investing in the fund.
- **Alignment of Interests:** Evaluate how fees impact the net returns to investors and the alignment of interests between managers and investors.

### Detailed Evaluation Process

For each area of inquiry, investors should ask specific questions and conduct a detailed analysis. This involves verifying managerial claims, understanding strategy specifics, and assessing operational robustness. For example:

- **Managerial Claims:** Are the fund's performance claims supported by historical data?
- **Strategy Specifics:** How does the fund's strategy align with current market conditions?
- **Operational Robustness:** Are the fund's operational systems capable of handling increased trading volumes?

### Examples

Consider a hypothetical scenario where an investor is evaluating an alternative strategy fund managed by a well-known Canadian bank. The investor might:

- **Assess the Bank's Reputation:** Review the bank's history and reputation in managing alternative investments.
- **Evaluate Performance Metrics:** Compare the fund's historical returns against Canadian market benchmarks.
- **Analyze Fee Structures:** Determine how the fund's fees compare to industry standards and their impact on net returns.

### Glossary

- **Due Diligence:** A comprehensive appraisal of an investment undertaken by a prospective buyer to establish its assets, liabilities, and evaluate its potential.
- **Investment Committee:** A group responsible for making investment decisions and overseeing portfolio management.
- **Prime Broker:** A financial institution providing services like lending securities and facilitating trades for hedge funds.
- **Custodian:** A financial institution that holds and safeguards a fund’s assets.
- **Administrator:** An entity responsible for calculating and publishing the fund’s NAV and maintaining records.
- **Offering Memorandum:** A document providing detailed information about an investment fund’s operations for regulatory compliance.
- **Net Asset Value (NAV):** The value per share of a mutual fund or ETF on a specific date or time.
- **Performance Metrics:** Quantitative measures used to evaluate the performance of an investment fund.
- **Fee Structure:** The arrangement of fees charged by the fund, including management and performance fees.
- **Stress Testing:** Assessing how a fund’s portfolio would perform under extreme market conditions.

### Conclusion

Conducting thorough due diligence is essential for investors considering alternative strategy funds. By evaluating the fund's structure, strategies, risk management, operations, and performance, investors can make informed decisions that align with their financial goals. Understanding the intricacies of these funds and their potential risks and returns is crucial for successful investment outcomes.
