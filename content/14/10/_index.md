## Chapter 22: Other Managed Products

In the diverse landscape of investment opportunities, managed products offer a structured approach to investing that can cater to a wide range of financial goals and risk tolerances. This chapter delves into various managed products beyond the commonly known mutual funds and exchange-traded funds (ETFs), focusing on their unique structures, features, and roles within the Canadian financial market.

### Understanding Managed Products

Managed products are investment vehicles that pool funds from multiple investors to invest in a diversified portfolio of assets. These products are professionally managed, providing investors with access to expertise and a broad range of investment opportunities. While mutual funds and ETFs are well-known examples, other managed products offer distinct benefits and structures that can complement an investor's portfolio.

### Segregated Funds

Segregated funds, often referred to as "seg funds," are insurance-based investment products that combine the growth potential of mutual funds with the security of insurance. Offered by insurance companies, segregated funds provide unique protections, such as maturity and death benefit guarantees, which can be particularly appealing to risk-averse investors.

#### Key Features of Segregated Funds

- **Insurance Protection:** Segregated funds offer guarantees on the principal investment, typically ranging from 75% to 100%, upon maturity or death of the policyholder. This feature provides a safety net against market downturns.
- **Creditor Protection:** In certain circumstances, segregated funds may offer protection from creditors, making them an attractive option for business owners or professionals concerned about liability.
- **Estate Planning Benefits:** Segregated funds allow for direct beneficiary designations, bypassing the probate process and potentially reducing estate settlement costs and delays.

#### Differences from Mutual Funds

While segregated funds and mutual funds share similarities in terms of investment objectives and management, the key differences lie in their structure and benefits. Segregated funds are insurance contracts, which means they are subject to different regulatory oversight and offer additional protections not available with mutual funds.

### Labour-Sponsored Venture Capital Corporations (LSVCCs)

Labour-Sponsored Venture Capital Corporations (LSVCCs) are unique investment vehicles designed to support emerging businesses and stimulate economic growth. These funds are sponsored by labour unions or other organizations and provide venture capital to small and medium-sized enterprises (SMEs).

#### Advantages of LSVCCs

- **Tax Incentives:** Investors in LSVCCs may be eligible for federal and provincial tax credits, which can significantly enhance the after-tax return on investment.
- **Support for Innovation:** By investing in LSVCCs, investors contribute to the growth of innovative businesses, potentially leading to job creation and economic development.

#### Disadvantages of LSVCCs

- **Higher Risk:** LSVCCs typically invest in early-stage companies, which can be riskier and more volatile than established businesses.
- **Liquidity Constraints:** Investments in LSVCCs often come with lock-in periods, limiting investors' ability to access their funds.

### Closed-End Funds

Closed-end funds are investment funds with a fixed number of shares that are traded on stock exchanges. Unlike open-end funds, which continuously issue and redeem shares, closed-end funds raise capital through an initial public offering (IPO) and trade like stocks.

#### Structure and Benefits

- **Market Pricing:** Shares of closed-end funds are bought and sold at market prices, which can be above (premium) or below (discount) the net asset value (NAV) of the fund.
- **Leverage Opportunities:** Closed-end funds can use leverage to enhance returns, potentially increasing income and capital gains for investors.

#### Risks and Considerations

- **Price Volatility:** The market price of closed-end fund shares can be more volatile than the NAV, influenced by supply and demand dynamics.
- **Liquidity Risk:** While closed-end funds are traded on exchanges, their liquidity can vary, affecting the ease with which investors can buy or sell shares.

### Income Trusts and Private Equity

Income trusts and private equity represent alternative investment opportunities that can offer attractive returns and diversification benefits.

#### Income Trusts

Income trusts are investment vehicles that hold income-generating assets, such as real estate or natural resources, and distribute the income to unit holders. They are popular for their potential to provide steady cash flow.

- **Types of Income Trusts:** Common types include Real Estate Investment Trusts (REITs) and Business Trusts, each with distinct asset focuses and risk profiles.
- **Advantages:** Income trusts can offer high yields and tax-efficient distributions, appealing to income-focused investors.

#### Private Equity

Private equity involves investing in private companies or taking public companies private, with the goal of improving their value and eventually selling them for a profit.

- **Investment Process:** Private equity investments typically involve a longer time horizon and active management to enhance company performance.
- **Advantages and Disadvantages:** While private equity can offer substantial returns, it also involves higher risk, illiquidity, and a need for significant capital commitment.

### Glossary

- **Segregated Fund:** An insurance-based investment product offering guarantees on principal and potential creditor protection.
- **LSVCC:** A venture capital fund supporting SMEs, offering tax incentives but with higher risk and liquidity constraints.
- **Closed-End Fund:** An investment fund with a fixed number of shares traded on exchanges, offering leverage opportunities but with potential price volatility.
- **Income Trust:** A trust holding income-generating assets, providing steady cash flow and tax-efficient distributions.
- **Private Equity:** Investment in private companies with the aim of enhancing value and achieving high returns, involving higher risk and illiquidity.

### Conclusion

Understanding the diverse range of managed products available in the Canadian market can empower investors to make informed decisions that align with their financial goals and risk tolerance. By exploring options beyond traditional mutual funds and ETFs, investors can access unique benefits and opportunities for growth, income, and diversification.
