## 22.4 Differentiate Among the Types of Income Trusts

Income trusts are a unique investment vehicle that combines elements of both equity and fixed income, offering investors a way to gain exposure to a diverse range of income-generating assets. In this section, we will delve into the different types of income trusts, their advantages and disadvantages, and their taxation implications within the Canadian financial landscape.

### Definition and Overview

An **income trust** is a type of pooled investment fund that holds interests in the operating assets of companies. These trusts are traded on stock exchanges, similar to closed-end funds, and focus on generating income for their unitholders. Income trusts are structured to pass the majority of their income to investors, making them attractive for those seeking regular income streams.

Income trusts are akin to closed-end funds in that they pool capital from multiple investors to purchase a portfolio of assets. However, unlike mutual funds, which issue and redeem shares on demand, income trusts have a fixed number of units that trade on exchanges, allowing investors to buy and sell units like stocks.

### Types of Income Trusts

Income trusts can be broadly categorized into two main types: Real Estate Investment Trusts (REITs) and Business Trusts. Each type has distinct characteristics and investment focuses.

#### Real Estate Investment Trusts (REITs)

**Real Estate Investment Trusts (REITs)** are income trusts that invest in income-producing real estate properties. These properties can include commercial buildings, shopping centers, apartments, and industrial facilities. REITs generate income primarily through rental payments from tenants and are required to distribute a significant portion of this income to unitholders.

**Key Features of REITs:**

- **Income Generation:** REITs provide investors with exposure to the real estate market and a steady stream of rental income.
- **Diversification:** By investing in a portfolio of properties, REITs offer diversification across different real estate sectors and geographic locations.
- **Liquidity:** As publicly traded entities, REITs offer liquidity similar to stocks, allowing investors to buy and sell units on the stock exchange.

**Example:** Consider a Canadian REIT that owns a portfolio of office buildings in major cities. The REIT collects rental income from tenants and distributes a substantial portion of this income to its unitholders, providing them with a regular income stream.

#### Business Trusts

**Business Trusts** are income trusts that hold interests in operating companies across various industries. These trusts are structured to own and manage businesses, distributing the income generated from operations to unitholders.

**Key Features of Business Trusts:**

- **Industry Exposure:** Business trusts provide investors with exposure to specific industries, such as energy, utilities, or manufacturing.
- **Income Distribution:** Similar to REITs, business trusts distribute a significant portion of their income to unitholders, offering potential for high yields.
- **Operational Focus:** Business trusts are actively involved in the management and operation of the businesses they own.

**Example:** A Canadian business trust might own and operate a chain of retail stores. The trust generates income from retail sales and distributes profits to its unitholders, offering them a share in the business's success.

### Advantages of Income Trusts

Income trusts offer several advantages that make them appealing to investors seeking income and diversification:

1. **High Yield Potential:** Due to their income distribution requirements, income trusts often provide higher yields compared to traditional equities. This makes them attractive to income-focused investors.

2. **Steady Income Streams:** Income trusts are designed to provide regular income distributions, making them suitable for investors seeking consistent cash flow, such as retirees.

3. **Diversification:** By investing in a portfolio of assets or businesses, income trusts offer diversification benefits, reducing the risk associated with individual investments.

### Disadvantages and Risks

While income trusts offer attractive benefits, they also come with certain risks and disadvantages:

1. **Market and Economic Risks:** The performance of income trusts is tied to the underlying operating assets, making them susceptible to market and economic fluctuations. For example, a downturn in the real estate market could impact the income generated by a REIT.

2. **Price Divergence from NAV:** The trading price of income trust units can diverge from their Net Asset Value (NAV), leading to potential discrepancies between market price and underlying asset value. This can affect investor returns.

3. **Regulatory Changes:** Changes in tax laws or regulations can impact the structure and attractiveness of income trusts, affecting their ability to distribute income efficiently.

### Taxation of Income Trusts

Income trusts are taxed similarly to taxable Canadian corporations, with certain distinctions that investors should be aware of:

1. **Tax Treatment:** Income distributed by trusts is typically taxed in the hands of the unitholders. However, the trust itself may benefit from tax efficiencies by distributing income before corporate taxes are applied.

2. **Eligibility for Registered Accounts:** REITs, in particular, are eligible for inclusion in registered accounts such as Registered Retirement Savings Plans (RRSPs) and Registered Retirement Income Funds (RRIFs), allowing for tax-deferred growth.

### Glossary

- **Income Trust:** A pooled investment fund holding interests in operating assets of companies, traded on stock exchanges and focused on income generation.
- **Real Estate Investment Trust (REIT):** A trust that invests in income-producing real estate and distributes most of its income to unitholders.
- **Business Trust:** A trust that holds and operates businesses across various industries, distributing income to unitholders.
- **Net Asset Value (NAV):** The per-share value of a fund’s assets minus its liabilities.
- **Yield:** The income returned on an investment, typically expressed as an annual percentage.
- **Market Risk:** The risk of losses due to factors that affect the overall performance of the financial markets.
- **Economic Risk:** The risk of losses due to changes in economic conditions.

### Conclusion

Income trusts offer a compelling investment option for those seeking income and diversification within their portfolios. By understanding the different types of income trusts, their advantages, risks, and tax implications, investors can make informed decisions that align with their financial goals. As with any investment, it is crucial to consider individual risk tolerance and investment objectives when incorporating income trusts into a broader investment strategy.
