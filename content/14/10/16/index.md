## Glossary for Chapter 22: Understanding Managed Products in Canadian Finance

In Chapter 22, we delve into the world of other managed products, exploring a variety of investment vehicles that offer unique opportunities and challenges. This glossary provides comprehensive definitions and contextual understanding of key terms, helping you navigate the complexities of these financial instruments. By mastering these terms, you will enhance your ability to evaluate and utilize managed products effectively within your investment strategies.

### Segregated Fund
A segregated fund is an insurance-based investment vehicle similar to a mutual fund but with added benefits such as maturity guarantees and death benefits. These funds are offered by insurance companies and provide a level of protection against market downturns, making them attractive to risk-averse investors.

**Example:** Consider an investor who purchases a segregated fund with a 75% maturity guarantee. If the market value of the fund decreases, the investor is assured of receiving at least 75% of their initial investment at the end of the contract term.

### LSVCC (Labour-Sponsored Venture Capital Corporation)
LSVCCs are managed investment funds sponsored by labour organizations to invest in emerging and growing businesses. They offer tax credits to investors, making them appealing for those looking to support local economic development while receiving tax benefits.

**Example:** An investor in Ontario might invest in an LSVCC to support local startups and receive a provincial tax credit, reducing their overall tax liability.

### Closed-End Fund
A closed-end fund is a pooled investment fund with a fixed number of shares that trade on an exchange. Unlike open-end funds, closed-end funds are subject to market supply and demand, which can lead to trading at a premium or discount to their Net Asset Value (NAV).

**Example:** A closed-end fund focused on Canadian infrastructure projects might trade at a discount to its NAV, offering investors an opportunity to buy assets at a lower price than their intrinsic value.

### Income Trust
An income trust holds operating assets of companies and distributes income to investors. These trusts are traded on stock exchanges and are popular for their ability to provide steady income streams.

**Example:** A Canadian energy income trust might own pipelines and distribute the income generated from transportation fees to its investors.

### REIT (Real Estate Investment Trust)
REITs are trusts that invest in and manage income-producing real estate properties. They are required to distribute most of their income to unitholders, making them a popular choice for income-focused investors.

**Example:** A Canadian REIT might own a portfolio of shopping malls and distribute rental income to its investors, providing a stable income stream.

### Business Trust
A business trust holds and operates business assets, distributing income to investors similarly to closed-end funds. These trusts can offer exposure to specific sectors or industries.

**Example:** A business trust focused on Canadian telecommunications might own and operate cell towers, distributing the income generated from leasing space to telecom companies.

### Private Equity
Private equity involves investments in private companies or buyouts of public companies, aiming for high returns through strategic management and operational improvements. These investments are typically illiquid and require a long-term commitment.

**Example:** A private equity firm might acquire a struggling Canadian manufacturing company, implement operational improvements, and sell it at a profit after several years.

### Net Asset Value (NAV)
NAV is the per-share value of a fund’s assets minus its liabilities. It is a critical measure for evaluating the value of mutual funds and other investment vehicles.

**Example:** If a mutual fund has assets worth $100 million and liabilities of $10 million, and there are 10 million shares outstanding, the NAV per share would be $9.

### Liquidity
Liquidity refers to the ease with which an asset can be bought or sold in the market without significantly affecting its price. High liquidity is desirable as it allows investors to enter and exit positions easily.

**Example:** Canadian government bonds are highly liquid, allowing investors to buy and sell them quickly with minimal impact on price.

### Yield
Yield is the income returned on an investment, typically expressed as an annual percentage. It is a key metric for evaluating the income-generating potential of an investment.

**Example:** A Canadian corporate bond with a face value of $1,000 and an annual coupon payment of $50 has a yield of 5%.

### Diversification
Diversification is the practice of spreading investments across various assets to reduce risk. By diversifying, investors can mitigate the impact of poor performance in any single investment.

**Example:** A Canadian investor might diversify their portfolio by holding stocks, bonds, and real estate to reduce overall risk.

### Discount (in Closed-End Funds)
A discount occurs when a closed-end fund trades below its NAV. This can present a buying opportunity for investors who believe the fund's assets are undervalued.

**Example:** A closed-end fund with an NAV of $20 per share trading at $18 is said to be trading at a discount.

### Premium (in Closed-End Funds)
A premium occurs when a closed-end fund trades above its NAV. This can indicate strong demand for the fund's shares.

**Example:** A closed-end fund with an NAV of $20 per share trading at $22 is said to be trading at a premium.

### Management Expense Ratio (MER)
MER is the annual fee expressed as a percentage of a fund’s average net assets, covering management and operational costs. It is an important factor in evaluating the cost-effectiveness of a fund.

**Example:** A mutual fund with an MER of 1.5% charges $15 annually for every $1,000 invested.

### Assuris
Assuris is a not-for-profit organization providing protection to policyholders in the event of an insurance company's insolvency. It ensures that policyholders receive a portion of their benefits even if the insurer fails.

**Example:** If a Canadian insurance company becomes insolvent, Assuris may cover up to 85% of the promised benefits to policyholders.

### High-Yield Bond
A high-yield bond offers higher interest rates due to a higher risk of default. These bonds are also known as junk bonds and are attractive to investors seeking higher returns.

**Example:** A Canadian high-yield bond issued by a startup company might offer a 7% yield to compensate for the increased risk of default.

### Maturity Guarantee
A maturity guarantee is a contractual promise to return a specified percentage of the invested capital after a set period. It is a feature of segregated funds that provides a safety net for investors.

**Example:** An investor in a segregated fund with a 100% maturity guarantee is assured of receiving their full initial investment back at the end of the contract term, regardless of market performance.

### Death Benefit
A death benefit is a payout to beneficiaries upon the death of the annuitant, based on the contract terms. It is a feature of insurance products, including segregated funds.

**Example:** A segregated fund with a death benefit might pay out the greater of the NAV or the initial investment to the beneficiary upon the investor's death.

### Creditor Protection
Creditor protection is an insurance feature that shields segregated fund assets from creditors in the event of bankruptcy. This protection makes segregated funds appealing to individuals with significant liabilities.

**Example:** An entrepreneur facing potential business liabilities might invest in segregated funds to protect their personal assets from creditors.

### Leveraged Buyout (LBO)
An LBO is the acquisition of a company using a significant amount of borrowed money to meet the cost of acquisition. The assets of the acquired company often serve as collateral for the loans.

**Example:** A private equity firm might use an LBO to acquire a Canadian retail chain, using the chain's assets as collateral for the debt.

### Exit Strategy
An exit strategy is the method by which an investor intends to sell their stake in a private equity investment to realize returns. Common exit strategies include initial public offerings (IPOs) and sales to strategic buyers.

**Example:** A private equity firm might plan to exit its investment in a Canadian tech startup through an IPO once the company reaches a certain level of growth.

### Cash Drag
Cash drag is the negative impact on portfolio performance when a fund holds excess cash instead of being fully invested. It can reduce overall returns, especially in a rising market.

**Example:** A mutual fund holding 10% of its assets in cash during a bull market might experience cash drag, as the cash does not generate returns.

### Skew
Skew is a measure of the asymmetry of a return distribution, indicating the likelihood of extreme positive or negative returns. Positive skew suggests a higher probability of extreme positive returns, while negative skew indicates the opposite.

**Example:** A Canadian equity fund with a positive skew might have a higher chance of experiencing significant gains compared to losses.

### Kurtosis
Kurtosis is a measure of the "tailedness" of a return distribution, indicating the frequency of extreme returns. High kurtosis suggests a higher likelihood of extreme returns, both positive and negative.

**Example:** A bond fund with high kurtosis might experience more frequent extreme price movements compared to a fund with low kurtosis.

### Drawdown
Drawdown is a peak-to-trough decline in fund value, expressed as a percentage. It is a key risk metric for evaluating the volatility of an investment.

**Example:** A Canadian balanced fund experiencing a 15% drawdown during a market correction indicates a significant decline in value from its peak.

### Maximum Drawdown
Maximum drawdown is the largest observed drop from peak to trough over a specified period. It provides insight into the worst-case scenario for an investment.

**Example:** An investor evaluating a Canadian equity fund might consider its maximum drawdown during the 2008 financial crisis to assess its risk profile.

### Time to Recovery
Time to recovery is the period required to recover from a drawdown to reach a new peak. It is an important consideration for investors focused on long-term growth.

**Example:** A Canadian bond fund that took two years to recover from a drawdown during a recession might be considered less resilient than a fund that recovered in one year.

### Sharpe Ratio
The Sharpe Ratio is a measure of risk-adjusted return comparing excess returns to standard deviation. It helps investors understand the return generated per unit of risk.

**Example:** A Canadian mutual fund with a Sharpe Ratio of 1.5 is considered to have generated 1.5 units of return for each unit of risk taken.

### Jensen’s Alpha
Jensen’s Alpha measures a portfolio's performance relative to its expected return based on its beta. A positive alpha indicates outperformance, while a negative alpha suggests underperformance.

**Example:** A Canadian equity fund with a positive Jensen’s Alpha of 0.5% has outperformed its expected return based on market risk.

### Sortino Ratio
The Sortino Ratio is a risk-adjusted return measure focusing only on downside risk. It provides a more accurate assessment of an investment's performance by considering only negative volatility.

**Example:** A Canadian balanced fund with a Sortino Ratio of 2.0 is considered to have generated 2 units of return for each unit of downside risk taken.

### Calmar Ratio
The Calmar Ratio compares annual returns to maximum drawdown. It is used to evaluate risk-adjusted returns, particularly for funds with significant volatility.

**Example:** A Canadian hedge fund with a Calmar Ratio of 3.0 has generated 3 units of return for each unit of maximum drawdown experienced.

### Sterling Ratio
The Sterling Ratio is similar to the Calmar Ratio, used to evaluate risk-adjusted returns by comparing annual returns to drawdowns. It provides insight into a fund's ability to generate returns relative to risk.

**Example:** A Canadian REIT with a Sterling Ratio of 2.5 is considered to have generated 2.5 units of return for each unit of drawdown experienced.
