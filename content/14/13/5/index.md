## Glossary for Chapter 25: Fee-Based Accounts

In Chapter 25 of the CSC® Exam Prep Guide: Volume 2, we delve into the intricacies of fee-based accounts, a pivotal component of modern investment services. This glossary provides clear and concise definitions of key terms introduced in the chapter, offering insights into their relevance and application within the context of Canadian financial services. Each term is accompanied by practical examples or contexts to enhance understanding.

### Discretionary Accounts

**Definition:** Discretionary accounts are investment accounts where the financial advisor has the authority to make investment decisions on behalf of the client without obtaining prior consent for each trade. This arrangement is typically used when clients trust their advisor's expertise to manage their portfolio actively.

**Relevance:** Discretionary accounts are crucial for clients who prefer a hands-off approach to investing, allowing advisors to respond swiftly to market changes and opportunities.

**Example:** A Canadian investor with a discretionary account might have their portfolio adjusted by their advisor to increase exposure to technology stocks following a market analysis indicating potential growth in the sector.

### Mutual Fund Wraps

**Definition:** Mutual fund wraps are managed accounts that hold a selection of individual mutual funds. These accounts are coordinated by an overlay manager who optimizes asset allocation and investment strategy to align with the client's financial goals.

**Relevance:** Mutual fund wraps offer diversification and professional management, making them suitable for investors seeking a balanced approach to risk and return.

**Example:** A mutual fund wrap might include a mix of Canadian equity funds, international funds, and fixed-income funds, adjusted periodically by the overlay manager to maintain the desired asset allocation.

### Exchange-Traded Fund (ETF) Wraps

**Definition:** ETF wraps are managed accounts that utilize exchange-traded funds to build a diversified investment portfolio. These accounts can follow either a passive or active investment approach based on asset allocation strategies.

**Relevance:** ETF wraps provide cost-effective diversification and are often used by investors looking for a blend of active management and low-cost investment vehicles.

**Example:** An ETF wrap might consist of a combination of Canadian, U.S., and global ETFs, rebalanced quarterly to align with the client's risk tolerance and investment objectives.

### Overlay Manager

**Definition:** An overlay manager is a portfolio manager responsible for coordinating the investment strategies across multiple mandates within multi-mandate managed accounts. They ensure that the overall portfolio aligns with the client's investment policy and objectives.

**Relevance:** Overlay managers play a critical role in maintaining the coherence and efficiency of complex investment strategies, particularly in unified managed accounts.

**Example:** In a unified managed account, the overlay manager might adjust the allocation between equity and fixed-income mandates to optimize the portfolio's risk-return profile based on market conditions.

### Separately Managed Accounts (SMAs)

**Definition:** Separately managed accounts are individual investment accounts managed by external portfolio managers. These accounts allow for customized investment strategies and direct ownership of securities.

**Relevance:** SMAs offer personalized investment solutions, making them ideal for investors with specific financial goals or preferences for certain asset classes.

**Example:** A Canadian investor might choose an SMA focused on environmentally sustainable investments, with the portfolio manager selecting stocks and bonds that meet specific environmental criteria.

### Unified Managed Accounts (UMAs)

**Definition:** Unified managed accounts are multi-mandate managed accounts that consolidate various investment strategies under a single portfolio, managed by an overlay manager.

**Relevance:** UMAs provide a streamlined approach to managing diverse investment strategies, offering clients a comprehensive view of their investments.

**Example:** A UMA might include a mix of equity, fixed-income, and alternative investments, with the overlay manager adjusting allocations to optimize performance and manage risk.

### Household Accounts

**Definition:** Household accounts are fee-based accounts that coordinate investments across an entire family or household. These accounts optimize tax management and investment planning by considering the financial needs and goals of all family members.

**Relevance:** Household accounts are beneficial for families seeking to integrate their financial planning and investment strategies, maximizing tax efficiency and wealth accumulation.

**Example:** A household account might involve consolidating the investment portfolios of parents and children, allowing for strategic asset allocation and tax planning across the family unit.

### Private Family Office

**Definition:** A private family office provides comprehensive financial management services for ultra-high-net-worth families. These services include investment management, estate planning, and tax planning.

**Relevance:** Private family offices offer tailored financial solutions, addressing the complex needs of wealthy families and ensuring the preservation and growth of family wealth.

**Example:** A Canadian family with significant business interests might establish a private family office to manage their investments, coordinate philanthropic activities, and plan for intergenerational wealth transfer.

### Robo-Advisor

**Definition:** A robo-advisor is an automated, online investment management service that provides passive investment strategies using ETFs and other low-cost investment vehicles.

**Relevance:** Robo-advisors offer a cost-effective and accessible option for investors seeking a hands-off approach to portfolio management, leveraging technology to optimize investment strategies.

**Example:** A young Canadian professional might use a robo-advisor to invest in a diversified portfolio of ETFs, benefiting from automated rebalancing and tax-loss harvesting.

### Self-Directed Brokerage Accounts

**Definition:** Self-directed brokerage accounts are fee-based accounts offering online, technology-driven investment management with options for direct guidance or robo-advisory services.

**Relevance:** These accounts empower investors to take control of their investment decisions while providing access to professional tools and resources.

**Example:** An experienced Canadian investor might use a self-directed brokerage account to trade stocks and ETFs, utilizing research tools and market analysis provided by the brokerage platform.

### Full-Service Brokerage Accounts

**Definition:** Full-service brokerage accounts are fee-based accounts providing comprehensive financial planning and unlimited trading services, often bundled with various wealth management services.

**Relevance:** Full-service brokerage accounts cater to investors seeking personalized advice and a wide range of financial services, including estate planning and tax optimization.

**Example:** A Canadian retiree might choose a full-service brokerage account to receive ongoing financial advice, access to exclusive investment opportunities, and assistance with retirement planning.
