## Glossary for Chapter 15: Introduction to the Portfolio Approach

In Chapter 15, we delve into the intricacies of portfolio management, exploring various strategies and risk factors that influence investment decisions. This glossary serves as a comprehensive guide to the key terms and concepts discussed in the chapter, providing clarity and insight into the portfolio approach. Understanding these terms is crucial for effectively managing investments and optimizing portfolio performance within the Canadian financial landscape.

### Active Investment Strategy
An active investment strategy involves making specific investments with the goal of outperforming a market benchmark. This approach requires continuous analysis and decision-making, often involving market timing and security selection. For example, a Canadian fund manager might actively adjust a portfolio to capitalize on anticipated changes in the Bank of Canada's interest rate policies.

### Inflation Rate Risk
Inflation rate risk refers to the potential erosion of investment returns due to rising inflation. For instance, if a Canadian bond yields 3% but inflation rises to 4%, the real return becomes negative, reducing purchasing power.

### Alpha
Alpha measures an investment's performance relative to a benchmark, indicating the excess return achieved. A positive alpha suggests that a portfolio manager has added value beyond the expected return based on the portfolio's beta. For example, if a Canadian equity fund has an alpha of 2, it has outperformed its benchmark by 2%.

### Interest Rate Risk
Interest rate risk is the potential for investment value fluctuations due to changes in interest rates, particularly affecting bonds. For instance, if the Bank of Canada raises interest rates, existing bond prices typically fall, impacting their market value.

### Beta
Beta is a measure of a security's volatility relative to the overall market. A beta greater than 1 indicates higher risk and potential return. For example, a Canadian technology stock with a beta of 1.5 is expected to be 50% more volatile than the market.

### Liquidity Risk
Liquidity risk is the risk that an investment cannot be quickly bought or sold without affecting its price. For instance, a Canadian small-cap stock may have limited buyers, making it difficult to sell without a significant price concession.

### Bottom-Up Analysis
Bottom-up analysis focuses on evaluating individual securities based on their fundamental characteristics, such as financial health and growth prospects, rather than broader economic factors. A Canadian investor might analyze a company's balance sheet and earnings reports to determine its investment potential.

### Non-Systematic Risk
Non-systematic risk is specific to a company or industry and can be mitigated through diversification. For example, a Canadian investor holding stocks in both the energy and technology sectors reduces exposure to risks unique to each sector.

### Business Risk
Business risk involves the potential for a company's operations to fail in generating sufficient income to cover costs and obligations. For instance, a Canadian retail company facing declining sales due to changing consumer preferences may experience increased business risk.

### Passive Investment Strategy
A passive investment strategy aims to replicate the performance of a market index without attempting to outperform it. This approach often involves investing in index funds or ETFs that track Canadian indices like the S&P/TSX Composite Index.

### Buy and Hold
Buy and hold is a passive investment strategy where securities are held for a long period, regardless of market fluctuations. A Canadian investor might purchase shares of a stable, dividend-paying company and hold them for decades.

### Political Risk
Political risk refers to the potential impact of political actions or changes on an investment's value. For example, changes in Canadian trade policies or regulations can affect the profitability of companies operating in affected industries.

### Capital Gain
A capital gain is an increase in the value of an investment asset, resulting in a higher worth than the purchase price. For instance, if a Canadian investor buys a stock for $50 and sells it for $70, the capital gain is $20.

### Rate of Return
The rate of return is the gain or loss of an investment over a specified period, expressed as a percentage of the initial cost. For example, if a Canadian investor earns $500 on a $5,000 investment, the rate of return is 10%.

### Capital Loss
A capital loss occurs when the value of an investment asset decreases, resulting in a lower worth than the purchase price. For instance, if a Canadian investor buys a stock for $100 and sells it for $80, the capital loss is $20.

### Real Rate of Return
The real rate of return is the return on an investment after adjusting for inflation, representing the actual increase in purchasing power. For example, if a Canadian bond yields 5% and inflation is 2%, the real rate of return is 3%.

### Cash Flow
Cash flow is the net amount of cash being transferred into and out of a business or investment. Positive cash flow indicates that a Canadian company or investment is generating more cash than it is spending.

### Risk-Free Rate of Return
The risk-free rate of return is the theoretical return of an investment with zero risk, often represented by short-term government securities like Canadian Treasury bills.

### Correlation
Correlation is a statistical measure that describes how two securities move in relation to each other. A Canadian investor might analyze the correlation between different asset classes to optimize portfolio diversification.

### Sector Rotation
Sector rotation is an investment strategy that involves shifting investment funds among different industry sectors based on economic cycles and market conditions. A Canadian investor might move funds from the energy sector to the technology sector during periods of economic growth.

### Default Risk
Default risk is the risk that a borrower will be unable to make the required payments on their debt obligations. For example, a Canadian bondholder faces default risk if the issuing company experiences financial difficulties.

### Specific Risk
Specific risk is the risk inherent to a particular company or industry, which can be reduced through diversification. For instance, a Canadian investor holding a diversified portfolio of stocks across various sectors reduces exposure to specific risks.

### Diversification
Diversification is the strategy of spreading investments across various securities to reduce exposure to any single asset or risk. A Canadian investor might diversify by holding a mix of stocks, bonds, and real estate.

### Standard Deviation
Standard deviation is a statistical measure of the dispersion of returns for a given security or portfolio, indicating its volatility. A Canadian investor might use standard deviation to assess the risk associated with a particular investment.

### Ex-Ante
Ex-ante refers to measures or estimates made before an event occurs, such as expected returns. A Canadian investor might use ex-ante analysis to forecast the potential performance of a new investment.

### Systematic Risk
Systematic risk is the inherent risk that affects the entire market or a broad range of assets, often undiversifiable. For example, economic recessions or global financial crises impact the Canadian market as a whole.

### Ex-Post
Ex-post refers to measures or results observed after an event has occurred, such as historical returns. A Canadian investor might use ex-post analysis to evaluate the past performance of a portfolio.

### Top-Down Analysis
Top-down analysis is an investment approach that starts with macroeconomic factors and narrows down to industry and individual securities. A Canadian investor might begin by analyzing the national economy before selecting specific stocks.

### Foreign Exchange Rate Risk
Foreign exchange rate risk is the risk of losing money due to changes in exchange rates when investing in foreign securities. A Canadian investor holding U.S. stocks may face losses if the Canadian dollar strengthens against the U.S. dollar.

### Volatility
Volatility is the degree of variation of a trading price series over time, measured by the standard deviation of returns. A Canadian investor might assess the volatility of a stock to determine its risk level.

### Holding Period Return
Holding period return is the total return received from holding an asset or portfolio of assets over a period of time, expressed as a percentage. A Canadian investor might calculate the holding period return to evaluate the performance of a long-term investment.

### Yield
Yield is the income return on an investment, such as the interest or dividends received from holding a particular security. A Canadian investor might consider the yield of a bond or stock when making investment decisions.

### Indexing
Indexing is a passive investment strategy that aims to replicate the performance of a specific market index. A Canadian investor might invest in an ETF that tracks the S&P/TSX Composite Index to achieve market returns.

### Return
Return is the gain or loss derived from an investment over a particular period. A Canadian investor might calculate the return on an investment to assess its profitability.

By understanding these terms and concepts, investors can make informed decisions and effectively manage their portfolios within the Canadian financial market. Each term plays a crucial role in shaping investment strategies and assessing risks, ultimately contributing to the achievement of financial goals.
