## 17.1.1 Advantages and Disadvantages of Managed Products

Managed products, such as mutual funds, exchange-traded funds (ETFs), and hedge funds, offer investors a structured way to access a diversified portfolio of assets. These products are particularly popular in Canada due to their ability to cater to a wide range of investment goals and risk profiles. In this section, we will explore the advantages and disadvantages of managed products, providing insights into how they can fit into an investor's portfolio.

### Advantages of Managed Products

#### Professional Management

One of the primary benefits of managed products is access to professional management. Fund managers are experienced professionals who make informed decisions based on extensive research and market analysis. This expertise can be particularly valuable for investors who lack the time or knowledge to manage their own portfolios. For example, Canadian pension funds often employ professional managers to oversee their investments, ensuring that they meet their long-term financial obligations.

#### Economies of Scale

Managed products benefit from economies of scale, which can lead to lower costs for investors. By pooling funds from many investors, these products can reduce transaction costs and management fees. This makes it possible for small investors to access a diversified portfolio without incurring significant expenses. For instance, a mutual fund can spread its fixed costs over a large asset base, reducing the cost per investor.

#### Low-Cost Diversification

Diversification is a key principle of investing, and managed products offer an efficient way to achieve it. By investing in a mutual fund or ETF, investors can gain exposure to a wide range of securities, including stocks, bonds, and other assets, without needing a large amount of capital. This diversification helps mitigate risk by spreading investments across different asset classes and sectors.

#### Variety of Types

Managed products come in various types, each designed to meet specific investment goals and risk profiles. Whether an investor is seeking growth, income, or a balanced approach, there is likely a managed product that aligns with their objectives. For example, Canadian investors can choose from equity funds, bond funds, balanced funds, and sector-specific funds, among others.

#### Flexible Purchase and Redemption Options

Investors appreciate the flexibility offered by managed products in terms of purchase and redemption options. They can choose to invest lump sums or make regular contributions through systematic investment plans. Additionally, mutual fund shares can typically be redeemed at the net asset value per share (NAVPS), providing liquidity when needed.

#### Liquidity

Liquidity is a significant advantage of managed products, particularly mutual funds. Investors can generally sell their shares quickly at the current NAVPS, making it easy to access their money when needed. This liquidity is crucial for investors who may require funds for emergencies or other financial needs.

#### Ease of Estate Planning

Managed products can simplify estate planning by providing a straightforward way to transfer assets upon the investor's death. Beneficiaries can inherit mutual fund shares without the need for complex legal arrangements, making the process more efficient and less costly.

#### Loan Collateral and Margin Eligibility

Mutual fund shares can be used as collateral for loans, providing investors with additional financial flexibility. This feature can be particularly useful for investors who need to access capital without selling their investments. Additionally, some managed products are eligible for margin accounts, allowing investors to borrow against their holdings to increase their investment potential.

### Disadvantages of Managed Products

#### Lack of Transparency

One of the main drawbacks of managed products is the lack of transparency, particularly in hedge funds. Investors may have limited visibility into the specific holdings and strategies employed by the fund manager. This can make it challenging to assess the risk and performance of the investment accurately.

#### Liquidity Constraints

While mutual funds generally offer liquidity, some managed products may impose restrictions on redemptions or charge fees for early withdrawals. For example, certain hedge funds have lock-up periods during which investors cannot redeem their shares. These constraints can limit an investor's ability to access their funds when needed.

#### High Fees

Managed products often come with various fees, including front-end loads, back-end loads, management fees, and other charges. These fees can erode investment returns over time. For instance, a mutual fund with a high management expense ratio (MER) may underperform a similar fund with lower fees, impacting the investor's overall returns.

#### Systematic Risk

Managed products are subject to systematic risk, which refers to market-wide risks that cannot be diversified away. Factors such as economic downturns, interest rate changes, and geopolitical events can affect the performance of these products, regardless of the fund manager's skill.

#### Tax Complications

Frequent trading within managed products can lead to taxable events, reducing after-tax returns for investors. For example, mutual funds that engage in active trading may generate capital gains distributions, which are taxable to investors even if they do not sell their shares. This can complicate tax planning and reduce the overall tax efficiency of the investment.

### Guidelines for Investors

When considering managed products, investors should analyze how each advantage and disadvantage aligns with their investment profiles and strategies. Here are some guidelines to help make informed decisions:

- **Assess Professional Management:** Consider the track record and expertise of the fund manager. A skilled manager can add value, but it's essential to evaluate their performance relative to benchmarks and peers.
- **Evaluate Fees and Expenses:** Understand the fee structure of the managed product and its impact on returns. Look for funds with competitive fees that align with the level of service and performance provided.
- **Consider Liquidity Needs:** Ensure that the liquidity of the managed product matches your financial needs and time horizon. Be aware of any redemption restrictions or fees that may apply.
- **Understand Tax Implications:** Consider the tax efficiency of the managed product and how it fits into your overall tax strategy. Tax-advantaged accounts like RRSPs and TFSAs can help mitigate tax liabilities.
- **Align with Investment Goals:** Choose managed products that align with your investment objectives, risk tolerance, and time horizon. Diversify across different fund types to achieve a balanced portfolio.

### Glossary

- **Front-End Load:** A sales charge paid when purchasing mutual fund shares.
- **Back-End Load:** A sales charge paid when redeeming mutual fund shares.
- **NAVPS (Net Asset Value per Share):** The value of a mutual fund’s assets minus its liabilities, divided by the number of outstanding shares.

By understanding the advantages and disadvantages of managed products, investors can make informed decisions that align with their financial goals and risk tolerance. Managed products offer a range of benefits, but it's crucial to consider the potential drawbacks and how they may impact investment performance.
