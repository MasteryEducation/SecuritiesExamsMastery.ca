## Glossary for Chapter 17: Mutual Funds—Structure and Regulation

Understanding mutual funds requires familiarity with a variety of terms that describe their structure, regulation, and investment strategies. This glossary serves as a comprehensive reference tool, providing clear definitions and explanations of key terms introduced in Chapter 17. Organized alphabetically, it is designed to help you navigate the complex world of mutual funds with confidence.

### Active Management
**Definition:** A mutual fund strategy where managers actively select securities and make investment decisions aiming to outperform benchmarks.

**Importance:** Active management involves a hands-on approach where fund managers use research, forecasts, and their own judgment to make investment decisions. This strategy contrasts with passive management, where funds aim to replicate the performance of a specific index. Active management can potentially lead to higher returns but often comes with higher fees.

### Annual Managed Expense Ratio (MER)
**Definition:** The total percentage of fund assets used for management fees and operating expenses.

**Importance:** The MER is a critical factor for investors to consider, as it directly impacts the net return of a mutual fund. A higher MER can erode returns over time, making it essential for investors to weigh the cost against the potential benefits of active management.

### Back-End Load
**Definition:** A sales charge applied when redeeming mutual fund shares, often decreasing over time.

**Importance:** Back-end loads are designed to incentivize long-term investment in a fund. The fee typically decreases the longer an investor holds the fund, eventually disappearing after a certain period. Understanding the back-end load schedule is crucial for investors planning their exit strategy.

### Balanced Fund
**Definition:** A mutual fund that invests in a mix of equity and fixed-income securities to provide both growth and income.

**Importance:** Balanced funds offer diversification within a single investment vehicle, balancing the risk and return by spreading investments across different asset classes. They are suitable for investors seeking a moderate risk profile with potential for both capital appreciation and income.

### Back-End Load Schedule
**Definition:** The timeline showing how back-end loads decrease as the holding period increases.

**Importance:** This schedule is important for investors to understand the cost implications of redeeming their shares at different times. It helps in planning the timing of redemptions to minimize fees.

### Custodian
**Definition:** The financial institution responsible for safeguarding a mutual fund’s assets and ensuring proper transaction settlements.

**Importance:** The custodian plays a critical role in maintaining the integrity and security of a mutual fund's assets. By holding the securities and handling transactions, the custodian ensures that the fund operates smoothly and transparently.

### Deferred Sales Charge
**Definition:** Another term for back-end load, a fee charged upon redemption of mutual fund shares.

**Importance:** Deferred sales charges are a key consideration for investors when choosing a mutual fund, as they affect the net proceeds from selling shares. Understanding these charges helps investors make informed decisions about fund selection and timing of redemptions.

### Front-End Load
**Definition:** A sales commission charged when purchasing mutual fund shares.

**Importance:** Front-end loads are deducted from the initial investment, reducing the amount of money that is actually invested in the fund. Investors should consider these charges when evaluating the cost-effectiveness of a mutual fund.

### Fund Facts Document
**Definition:** A standardized two-page summary providing key information about a mutual fund to help investors make informed decisions.

**Importance:** The Fund Facts document is a crucial tool for investors, offering a concise overview of a fund's objectives, risks, past performance, and costs. It is designed to enhance transparency and help investors compare different funds easily.

### Hybrid Fund
**Definition:** Another term for balanced fund, investing in both equities and fixed-income securities.

**Importance:** Hybrid funds provide a diversified investment approach, combining the growth potential of equities with the stability of fixed-income securities. They are ideal for investors seeking a balanced risk-return profile.

### Net Asset Value per Share (NAVPS)
**Definition:** The value per share of a mutual fund’s assets minus its liabilities, used to price purchases and redemptions.

**Importance:** NAVPS is a fundamental metric for mutual funds, reflecting the per-share value of the fund's holdings. It is calculated daily and serves as the basis for buying and selling fund shares.

### Money Market Fund
**Definition:** A mutual fund investing in short-term, highly liquid securities, offering safety and liquidity.

**Importance:** Money market funds are considered low-risk investments, suitable for investors seeking capital preservation and liquidity. They invest in high-quality, short-term debt instruments, providing a stable return.

### No-Load Fund
**Definition:** A mutual fund that does not charge any sales commissions or loads on purchases or redemptions.

**Importance:** No-load funds are attractive to cost-conscious investors, as they eliminate sales charges, allowing the entire investment to work for the investor. This can lead to higher net returns over time.

### Open-End Trust
**Definition:** A common mutual fund structure where investors can buy and sell shares at NAVPS.

**Importance:** Open-end trusts offer flexibility and liquidity, allowing investors to enter and exit the fund at any time. This structure is prevalent in the mutual fund industry, providing ease of access to a wide range of investment opportunities.

### Principal-Protected Note
**Definition:** An investment product that guarantees the return of the initial investment amount at maturity.

**Importance:** Principal-protected notes appeal to risk-averse investors, offering the security of capital protection while providing exposure to potential market gains. They are often used in conjunction with mutual funds to enhance portfolio stability.

### Publisher
**Definition:** The entity responsible for creating and managing mutual fund documents and disclosures.

**Importance:** Publishers play a vital role in ensuring that investors have access to accurate and timely information about mutual funds. They are responsible for producing documents like the Fund Facts and Simplified Prospectus, which are essential for informed investment decisions.

### Put Option
**Definition:** A derivative used by mutual funds to hedge against potential declines in asset values.

**Importance:** Put options provide mutual funds with a tool to manage risk, offering protection against adverse market movements. By securing the right to sell assets at a predetermined price, funds can mitigate potential losses.

### Redemption Price
**Definition:** The price at which mutual fund shares are sold back to the fund, typically NAVPS minus any applicable fees.

**Importance:** Understanding the redemption price is crucial for investors, as it determines the amount received when selling fund shares. It is important to consider any fees that may reduce the net proceeds from redemptions.

### Risk-Adjusted Return
**Definition:** A measure of how much return is achieved per unit of risk taken, often assessed using the Sharpe Ratio.

**Importance:** Risk-adjusted return is a key metric for evaluating the performance of mutual funds, helping investors compare funds with different risk profiles. It provides insight into the efficiency of a fund's return relative to its risk.

### Simplified Prospectus
**Definition:** A detailed document providing comprehensive information about a mutual fund, available upon investor request.

**Importance:** The Simplified Prospectus is an essential resource for investors seeking in-depth information about a mutual fund's objectives, strategies, risks, and costs. It complements the Fund Facts document, offering a more detailed view of the fund.

### Target Asset Allocation
**Definition:** The desired distribution of investments across various asset classes to achieve specific investment objectives.

**Importance:** Target asset allocation is a fundamental aspect of mutual fund management, guiding the selection and weighting of assets to meet the fund's goals. It helps investors align their portfolios with their risk tolerance and investment horizon.

### Trust Deed
**Definition:** The legal document establishing a mutual fund trust, outlining its investment objectives, strategies, and restrictions.

**Importance:** The trust deed is a foundational document for mutual funds, setting the framework for their operation and governance. It ensures that the fund's activities align with its stated objectives and regulatory requirements.

### Unit
**Definition:** A share or portion of ownership in a mutual fund, representing a proportional claim on the fund’s assets.

**Importance:** Units are the basic building blocks of mutual fund ownership, allowing investors to participate in the fund's performance. Understanding the concept of units is essential for evaluating investment opportunities and managing fund holdings.
