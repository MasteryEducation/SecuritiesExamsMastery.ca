## Glossary for Chapter 14: Company Analysis

In Chapter 14, we delve into the critical components of company analysis, focusing on various financial ratios and metrics that provide insights into a company's financial health and performance. Understanding these terms is essential for anyone involved in financial analysis, investment decision-making, or corporate finance. This glossary serves as a comprehensive guide to the key concepts and ratios used in company analysis, particularly within the Canadian financial context.

### Asset Coverage Ratio

The **asset coverage ratio** is a measure of a company's ability to cover its debt obligations with its tangible assets after accounting for current liabilities. It is calculated as:

{{< katex >}} \text{Asset Coverage Ratio} = \frac{\text{Total Tangible Assets} - \text{Current Liabilities}}{\text{Total Debt}} {{< /katex >}}

This ratio is crucial for assessing the financial stability of a company, especially in scenarios where liquidation might be necessary. A higher ratio indicates a stronger ability to cover debts, which is particularly important for creditors and investors.

### Inventory Turnover Ratio

The **inventory turnover ratio** shows how many times a company's inventory is sold and replaced over a period. It is calculated as:

{{< katex >}} \text{Inventory Turnover Ratio} = \frac{\text{Cost of Goods Sold}}{\text{Average Inventory}} {{< /katex >}}

This ratio helps in understanding how efficiently a company manages its inventory. A higher turnover indicates efficient inventory management, which can lead to reduced holding costs and increased profitability.

### Capital Structure

**Capital structure** refers to the mix of debt and equity that a company uses to finance its operations. It is a critical aspect of financial strategy, influencing a company's risk profile and cost of capital. Companies must balance the benefits of debt, such as tax advantages, with the risks of financial distress.

### Liquidity Ratios

**Liquidity ratios** measure a company's ability to meet its short-term obligations. Key liquidity ratios include:

- **Current Ratio**: Measures the ability to pay short-term liabilities with short-term assets.
- **Quick Ratio (Acid Test)**: A more stringent measure that excludes inventories from current assets.

These ratios are vital for assessing a company's short-term financial health and operational efficiency.

### Cash Flow

**Cash flow** is the net amount of cash being transferred into and out of a business. It is a critical indicator of a company's financial health, affecting its ability to fund operations, pay debts, and invest in growth opportunities. Cash flow analysis is essential for understanding the sustainability of a company's financial practices.

### Net Current Assets

**Net current assets**, also known as working capital, are calculated as:

{{< katex >}} \text{Net Current Assets} = \text{Current Assets} - \text{Current Liabilities} {{< /katex >}}

This metric indicates the short-term financial health of a company, reflecting its ability to cover short-term obligations with its short-term assets.

### Cash Flow-to-Total Debt Outstanding Ratio

The **cash flow-to-total debt outstanding ratio** measures a company's ability to cover its total debt with its operating cash flow. It is calculated as:

{{< katex >}} \text{Cash Flow-to-Total Debt Ratio} = \frac{\text{Operating Cash Flow}}{\text{Total Debt}} {{< /katex >}}

A higher ratio suggests a stronger capacity to manage debt, which is crucial for long-term financial stability.

### Net Profit Margin Ratio

The **net profit margin ratio** is a profitability ratio that shows the percentage of revenue that remains as profit after all expenses. It is calculated as:

{{< katex >}} \text{Net Profit Margin} = \frac{\text{Net Income}}{\text{Revenue}} \times 100 {{< /katex >}}

This ratio provides insights into a company's efficiency in converting revenue into actual profit, which is vital for assessing overall profitability.

### Current Ratio

The **current ratio** is a liquidity ratio that measures a company's ability to pay short-term obligations with its short-term assets. It is calculated as:

{{< katex >}} \text{Current Ratio} = \frac{\text{Current Assets}}{\text{Current Liabilities}} {{< /katex >}}

A ratio above 1 indicates that the company has more current assets than current liabilities, suggesting good short-term financial health.

### Operating Performance Ratios

**Operating performance ratios** assess how well a company utilizes its resources to generate earnings. These ratios include:

- **Return on Assets (ROA)**
- **Return on Equity (ROE)**

These metrics are crucial for evaluating management efficiency and operational effectiveness.

### Debt-to-Equity Ratio

The **debt-to-equity ratio** compares a company's total debt to its shareholders' equity. It is calculated as:

{{< katex >}} \text{Debt-to-Equity Ratio} = \frac{\text{Total Debt}}{\text{Shareholders' Equity}} {{< /katex >}}

This ratio provides insights into the financial leverage of a company, indicating the proportion of debt used to finance assets relative to equity.

### Preferred Dividend Coverage Ratio

The **preferred dividend coverage ratio** indicates how well a company can cover its preferred dividends with its earnings. It is calculated as:

{{< katex >}} \text{Preferred Dividend Coverage Ratio} = \frac{\text{Net Income}}{\text{Preferred Dividends}} {{< /katex >}}

A higher ratio suggests a stronger ability to meet dividend obligations, which is important for preferred shareholders.

### Dividend Discount Model

The **dividend discount model** is a valuation model that estimates the price of a stock based on the present value of its expected future dividends. It is particularly useful for valuing companies with stable and predictable dividend payments.

### Price-to-Earnings Ratio (P/E Ratio)

The **price-to-earnings ratio (P/E ratio)** compares a company's current share price to its earnings per share. It is calculated as:

{{< katex >}} \text{P/E Ratio} = \frac{\text{Market Price per Share}}{\text{Earnings per Share}} {{< /katex >}}

This ratio is widely used to assess whether a stock is overvalued or undervalued relative to its earnings.

### Dividend Payout Ratio

The **dividend payout ratio** is the ratio of the total dividends paid out to shareholders relative to the company’s net income. It is calculated as:

{{< katex >}} \text{Dividend Payout Ratio} = \frac{\text{Dividends Paid}}{\text{Net Income}} {{< /katex >}}

This ratio provides insights into a company's dividend policy and its ability to sustain dividend payments.

### Quick Ratio (Acid Test)

The **quick ratio**, or acid test, is a more stringent liquidity ratio that excludes inventories from current assets. It is calculated as:

{{< katex >}} \text{Quick Ratio} = \frac{\text{Current Assets} - \text{Inventories}}{\text{Current Liabilities}} {{< /katex >}}

This ratio provides a more conservative view of a company's short-term liquidity position.

### Dividend Yield

The **dividend yield** is the ratio of a company's annual dividend compared to its share price. It is calculated as:

{{< katex >}} \text{Dividend Yield} = \frac{\text{Annual Dividends per Share}}{\text{Price per Share}} {{< /katex >}}

This ratio is important for income-focused investors, indicating the return on investment from dividends.

### Return on Common Equity Ratio (ROE)

The **return on common equity ratio (ROE)** measures a company's profitability relative to shareholders' equity. It is calculated as:

{{< katex >}} \text{ROE} = \frac{\text{Net Income}}{\text{Shareholders' Equity}} {{< /katex >}}

This ratio indicates how effectively a company uses equity to generate profits, which is crucial for assessing management performance.

### Earnings per Common Share (EPS)

**Earnings per common share (EPS)** is the portion of a company's profit allocated to each outstanding common share. It is calculated as:

{{< katex >}} \text{EPS} = \frac{\text{Net Income} - \text{Preferred Dividends}}{\text{Average Outstanding Shares}} {{< /katex >}}

EPS is a key indicator of a company's profitability and is often used in valuation models.

### Risk Analysis Ratios

**Risk analysis ratios** assess a company’s ability to manage its financial obligations and associated risks. These ratios include:

- **Interest Coverage Ratio**
- **Debt-to-Equity Ratio**

These metrics are essential for understanding a company's financial risk profile and its ability to withstand economic downturns.

### Equity Value per Common Share Ratio (Book Value per Share)

The **equity value per common share ratio**, or book value per share, measures a company's book value per common share. It is calculated as:

{{< katex >}} \text{Book Value per Share} = \frac{\text{Total Shareholders' Equity} - \text{Preferred Equity}}{\text{Outstanding Shares}} {{< /katex >}}

This ratio provides insights into the intrinsic value of a company's shares, which is important for value investors.

### Trend Ratios

**Trend ratios** show the progress of financial metrics over a period. They are used to identify patterns and trends in a company's financial performance, aiding in forecasting and strategic planning.

### Financial Ratios

**Financial ratios** are derived from company financial statements and are used to assess various aspects of a company's performance and financial health. They include liquidity, profitability, and valuation ratios, among others.

### Value Ratios

**Value ratios** compare stock price to fundamental financial metrics to assess valuation. These ratios help investors determine whether a stock is fairly valued, overvalued, or undervalued.

### Gross Profit Margin Ratio

The **gross profit margin ratio** is a profitability ratio that shows the percentage of revenue that exceeds the cost of goods sold. It is calculated as:

{{< katex >}} \text{Gross Profit Margin} = \frac{\text{Revenue} - \text{Cost of Goods Sold}}{\text{Revenue}} \times 100 {{< /katex >}}

This ratio provides insights into a company's production efficiency and pricing strategy.

### Working Capital

**Working capital** is the difference between a company’s current assets and current liabilities. It is a measure of a company's short-term financial health and operational efficiency.

### Interest Coverage Ratio

The **interest coverage ratio** measures a company's ability to pay interest on outstanding debt based on its earnings before interest and taxes (EBIT). It is calculated as:

{{< katex >}} \text{Interest Coverage Ratio} = \frac{\text{EBIT}}{\text{Interest Expense}} {{< /katex >}}

A higher ratio indicates a stronger ability to meet interest obligations, which is crucial for maintaining financial stability.
