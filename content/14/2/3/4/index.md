## 14.3.4 Value Ratios

In the realm of financial analysis, value ratios are indispensable tools that provide insights into a company's financial health and its attractiveness as an investment. These ratios help investors evaluate the potential return on their investments and compare the relative value of different companies. This section delves into key value ratios, offering detailed explanations, practical examples, and real-world applications within the Canadian financial landscape.

### Dividend Payout Ratio

The Dividend Payout Ratio is a crucial metric that indicates the proportion of a company's earnings distributed to shareholders in the form of dividends. It is calculated using the formula:

{{< katex >}} \text{Dividend Payout Ratio} = \left( \frac{\text{Common Share Dividends}}{\text{Profit}} \right) \times 100 {{< /katex >}}

**Example Calculation:**

Consider a Canadian company, Maple Leaf Industries, which reported a profit of $500,000 and distributed $160,400 in dividends. The dividend payout ratio would be:

{{< katex >}} \text{Dividend Payout Ratio} = \left( \frac{160,400}{500,000} \right) \times 100 = 32.08\% {{< /katex >}}

**Interpretation:**

A dividend payout ratio of 32.08% suggests that Maple Leaf Industries distributes approximately one-third of its earnings as dividends, retaining the remainder for reinvestment or other purposes. This ratio helps investors assess the sustainability of dividend payments and the company's reinvestment strategy.

### Earnings Per Common Share (EPS)

Earnings Per Share (EPS) is a fundamental measure of a company's profitability, calculated as:

{{< katex >}} \text{EPS} = \frac{\text{Profit}}{\text{Weighted Average Number of Common Shares Outstanding}} {{< /katex >}}

**Example Calculation:**

Suppose Maple Leaf Industries has a profit of $500,000 and 160,000 weighted average common shares outstanding. The EPS would be:

{{< katex >}} \text{EPS} = \frac{500,000}{160,000} = \$3.12 \text{ per share} {{< /katex >}}

**Interpretation:**

An EPS of $3.12 indicates the amount of profit attributable to each share of common stock. Investors use EPS to gauge a company's profitability and compare it with peers. It's important to consider the impact of dilution, which can occur if the company issues additional shares.

### Dividend Yield

Dividend Yield measures the annual dividend income relative to the current market price of a share, calculated as:

{{< katex >}} \text{Dividend Yield} = \left( \frac{\text{Annual Dividend per Share}}{\text{Current Market Price}} \right) \times 100 {{< /katex >}}

**Example Calculation:**

If Maple Leaf Industries pays an annual dividend of $1.50 per share and its current market price is $39.37, the dividend yield would be:

{{< katex >}} \text{Dividend Yield} = \left( \frac{1.50}{39.37} \right) \times 100 = 3.81\% {{< /katex >}}

**Interpretation:**

A dividend yield of 3.81% provides investors with a measure of the income generated from their investment relative to the share price. Comparing dividend yields across companies and industries can help identify attractive income-generating investments.

### Equity Value per Common Share (Book Value per Share)

Equity Value per Common Share, or Book Value per Share, represents the net asset value attributable to each share, calculated as:

{{< katex >}} \text{Equity Value per Share} = \frac{\text{Equity}}{\text{Number of Common Shares Outstanding}} {{< /katex >}}

**Example Calculation:**

If Maple Leaf Industries has equity of $5,323,200 and 160,000 common shares outstanding, the equity value per share would be:

{{< katex >}} \text{Equity Value per Share} = \frac{5,323,200}{160,000} = \$33.27 {{< /katex >}}

**Interpretation:**

An equity value per share of $33.27 indicates the book value of the company's assets per share. It's essential to differentiate between book value and market value, as market value reflects investor perceptions and market conditions.

### Price-to-Earnings (P/E) Ratio

The Price-to-Earnings (P/E) Ratio is a widely used valuation metric that compares a company's current market price to its earnings per share:

{{< katex >}} \text{P/E Ratio} = \frac{\text{Current Market Price}}{\text{EPS}} {{< /katex >}}

**Example Calculation:**

With a current market price of $26.25 and an EPS of $3.12, Maple Leaf Industries' P/E ratio would be:

{{< katex >}} \text{P/E Ratio} = \frac{26.25}{3.12} = 8.41:1 {{< /katex >}}

**Interpretation:**

A P/E ratio of 8.41:1 suggests that investors are willing to pay $8.41 for every dollar of earnings. This ratio helps compare valuations across companies and industries, with lower P/E ratios potentially indicating undervaluation.

### Dividend Discount Model (DDM)

The Dividend Discount Model (DDM) is a method for valuing a stock by discounting expected future dividends to their present value. The formula is:

{{< katex >}} \text{Price} = \frac{\text{Div}_1}{r - g} {{< /katex >}}

Where:
- \\(\text{Div}_1\\) is the expected dividend next year.
- \\(r\\) is the required rate of return.
- \\(g\\) is the growth rate of dividends.

**Example Calculation:**

Assume Maple Leaf Industries expects a dividend of $1.60 next year, with a required return of 8% and a growth rate of 3%. The intrinsic stock value would be:

{{< katex >}} \text{Price} = \frac{1.60}{0.08 - 0.03} = \$32.00 {{< /katex >}}

**Interpretation:**

The DDM suggests an intrinsic value of $32.00 per share. While useful, the model relies on assumptions about growth rates and required returns, which can limit its accuracy.

### Glossary

- **Dividend Payout Ratio:** The percentage of earnings paid to shareholders as dividends.
- **Earnings Per Share (EPS):** Profit allocated to each outstanding share of common stock.
- **Dividend Yield:** The annual dividend income relative to the share's market price.
- **Equity Value per Share:** The net asset value per share of common stock.
- **Price-to-Earnings (P/E) Ratio:** A valuation ratio comparing market price to earnings per share.
- **Dividend Discount Model (DDM):** A valuation method using expected future dividends.

### Conclusion

Understanding and applying value ratios is crucial for making informed investment decisions. By analyzing these metrics, investors can assess a company's financial health, compare it with peers, and determine its attractiveness as an investment. As you explore these ratios, consider the broader economic context and industry trends to enhance your analysis.
