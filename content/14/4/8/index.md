## Glossary for Chapter 16: The Portfolio Management Process

In Chapter 16 of the CSC® Exam Prep Guide: Volume 2, we delve into the intricacies of the portfolio management process. This glossary serves as a comprehensive reference for key terms and concepts essential for understanding and mastering the art of portfolio management within the Canadian financial landscape.

### Asset Allocation

**Definition:** Asset allocation is the process of dividing investments among different asset classes—such as cash, fixed income, and equities—to balance risk and return based on the client's objectives and constraints.

**Example:** Consider a Canadian investor with a moderate risk tolerance and a long-term investment horizon. Their portfolio might be allocated as 60% equities, 30% fixed income, and 10% cash. This mix aims to achieve growth while managing risk.

**Best Practices:** Regularly review and adjust asset allocation to align with changing market conditions and the investor's evolving goals.

### Investment Policy Statement (IPS)

**Definition:** An Investment Policy Statement (IPS) is a formal document that outlines the investment guidelines, objectives, and strategies agreed upon by the portfolio manager and the client.

**Example:** A Canadian retiree's IPS might specify a conservative investment strategy focusing on income generation and capital preservation, with a preference for dividend-paying stocks and government bonds.

**Importance:** The IPS serves as a roadmap for investment decisions, ensuring consistency and alignment with the client's financial goals.

### Strategic Asset Allocation

**Definition:** Strategic asset allocation is a long-term approach to asset allocation that aims to establish a baseline mix of asset classes based on the client's risk tolerance and investment goals.

**Example:** A young Canadian professional with high risk tolerance might have a strategic asset allocation of 80% equities and 20% fixed income, reflecting their focus on growth.

**Considerations:** Strategic asset allocation should be revisited periodically to ensure it remains aligned with the client's long-term objectives.

### Dynamic Asset Allocation

**Definition:** Dynamic asset allocation involves adjusting the portfolio’s asset mix in response to changing market conditions or economic factors to optimize risk-return trade-offs.

**Example:** During a market downturn, a portfolio manager might increase the allocation to fixed income to reduce risk, then shift back to equities as conditions improve.

**Strategy:** Dynamic asset allocation requires active monitoring and a willingness to adapt to market changes.

### Tactical Asset Allocation

**Definition:** Tactical asset allocation involves temporarily deviating from the strategic asset mix to capitalize on short-term market opportunities or to mitigate impending risks.

**Example:** A portfolio manager might overweight technology stocks in anticipation of strong earnings reports, then revert to the strategic allocation once the opportunity has passed.

**Risk:** Tactical asset allocation can enhance returns but also increases the risk of mistiming the market.

### Risk-Adjusted Return

**Definition:** Risk-adjusted return measures how much return an investment has made relative to the amount of risk taken to achieve that return, commonly assessed using metrics like the Sharpe Ratio.

**Example:** A Canadian mutual fund with a high Sharpe Ratio indicates that it has delivered strong returns relative to its risk, making it an attractive investment option.

**Application:** Use risk-adjusted return metrics to compare investments with different risk profiles.

### Sharpe Ratio

**Definition:** The Sharpe Ratio is a risk-adjusted performance measure that calculates the excess return per unit of volatility or total risk in an investment portfolio.

**Example:** A portfolio with a Sharpe Ratio of 1.5 has generated 1.5 units of return for every unit of risk, indicating efficient risk management.

**Calculation:** Sharpe Ratio = (Portfolio Return - Risk-Free Rate) / Portfolio Standard Deviation.

### Rebalancing

**Definition:** Rebalancing is the process of realigning the proportions of assets in a portfolio back to their target allocations to maintain the desired risk and return profile.

**Example:** If equities outperform and exceed their target allocation, a portfolio manager might sell some equities and buy fixed income to restore balance.

**Frequency:** Rebalancing can be done quarterly, annually, or based on specific thresholds.

### Benchmark

**Definition:** A benchmark is a standard or point of reference against which the performance of a security, mutual fund, or investment manager can be measured.

**Example:** The S&P/TSX Composite Index is a common benchmark for Canadian equity portfolios.

**Purpose:** Benchmarks help assess whether a portfolio is meeting its performance objectives.

### Diversification

**Definition:** Diversification is the strategy of spreading investments across various securities and asset classes to reduce exposure to any single asset or risk.

**Example:** A diversified Canadian portfolio might include domestic and international equities, government and corporate bonds, and real estate.

**Benefit:** Diversification reduces the impact of poor performance in any one investment on the overall portfolio.

### Liquidity Requirements

**Definition:** Liquidity requirements refer to the need for a portfolio to maintain a certain level of liquid assets to meet short-term financial obligations or opportunities.

**Example:** A Canadian investor planning to buy a home in the near future might keep a portion of their portfolio in cash or short-term bonds.

**Consideration:** Balancing liquidity needs with long-term growth objectives is crucial.

### Time Horizon

**Definition:** The time horizon is the length of time over which an investor expects to hold an investment before needing to access the funds.

**Example:** A young Canadian saving for retirement might have a time horizon of 30 years, allowing for a more aggressive investment strategy.

**Impact:** The time horizon influences asset allocation and risk tolerance.

### Tax-Loss Harvesting

**Definition:** Tax-loss harvesting involves selling securities at a loss to offset capital gains taxes on profitable investments within the portfolio.

**Example:** A Canadian investor might sell underperforming stocks to realize losses, reducing their taxable capital gains.

**Strategy:** Tax-loss harvesting can enhance after-tax returns but requires careful planning to avoid wash-sale rules.

### Standard Deviation

**Definition:** Standard deviation is a statistical measure of the dispersion of returns for a given security or portfolio, indicating its volatility.

**Example:** A Canadian equity fund with a high standard deviation is more volatile and riskier than one with a low standard deviation.

**Use:** Standard deviation helps assess the risk associated with an investment.

### Frequency of Rebalancing

**Definition:** The frequency of rebalancing refers to the intervals at which a portfolio is reviewed and adjustments are made to maintain the target asset allocation, such as quarterly or annually.

**Example:** A Canadian pension fund might rebalance its portfolio annually to align with its strategic asset allocation.

**Decision:** The frequency of rebalancing depends on market conditions and transaction costs.

### Cash Flow

**Definition:** Cash flow is the net amount of cash being transferred into and out of a portfolio, including income from dividends and interest, as well as contributions and withdrawals.

**Example:** A Canadian retiree might rely on cash flow from dividends and bond interest to cover living expenses.

**Management:** Monitoring cash flow is essential for maintaining liquidity and meeting financial obligations.

### Volatility

**Definition:** Volatility is the degree of variation of a trading price series over time, measured by the standard deviation of returns, indicating the level of risk associated with an investment.

**Example:** A high-volatility stock might experience large price swings, posing greater risk to investors.

**Implication:** Understanding volatility helps investors assess risk and make informed decisions.

### Capital Gains

**Definition:** Capital gains are the profit earned from the sale of an investment asset at a higher price than its purchase price.

**Example:** A Canadian investor selling shares of a company at a higher price than they paid realizes a capital gain.

**Taxation:** In Canada, capital gains are subject to taxation, with only 50% of the gain being taxable.

### Capital Losses

**Definition:** Capital losses are the loss incurred from the sale of an investment asset at a lower price than its purchase price.

**Example:** Selling a stock for less than its purchase price results in a capital loss, which can offset capital gains for tax purposes.

**Benefit:** Capital losses can reduce taxable income, enhancing after-tax returns.

### Income Requirements

**Definition:** Income requirements refer to the need for a portfolio to generate regular income to meet ongoing financial obligations or lifestyle needs.

**Example:** A Canadian retiree might require a portfolio that generates sufficient income to cover living expenses without depleting capital.

**Strategy:** Income-focused investments, such as dividend stocks and bonds, can help meet income requirements.

### Risk Tolerance

**Definition:** Risk tolerance is the degree of variability in investment returns that an investor is willing to withstand in pursuit of higher returns.

**Example:** A conservative Canadian investor might prefer a portfolio with lower volatility and more stable returns.

**Assessment:** Understanding risk tolerance is crucial for developing an appropriate investment strategy.

### Mutual Funds

**Definition:** Mutual funds are investment vehicles that pool money from multiple investors to purchase a diversified portfolio of securities, managed by professional portfolio managers.

**Example:** A Canadian mutual fund might invest in a mix of domestic and international equities, providing diversification and professional management.

**Advantage:** Mutual funds offer access to a diversified portfolio with relatively low investment minimums.

### Creditor Risk

**Definition:** Creditor risk is the risk that a borrower will default on their debt obligations, impacting the value and yield of fixed-income securities in the portfolio.

**Example:** Investing in high-yield bonds exposes investors to higher creditor risk compared to government bonds.

**Mitigation:** Diversifying fixed-income holdings can reduce exposure to creditor risk.

### Portfolio Drift

**Definition:** Portfolio drift is the gradual shift in a portfolio’s asset allocation that occurs due to differing performance of asset classes, necessitating rebalancing to maintain the target mix.

**Example:** If equities outperform bonds, a portfolio might become overweight in equities, requiring rebalancing.

**Solution:** Regular monitoring and rebalancing help manage portfolio drift.

### Asset Mix

**Definition:** Asset mix refers to the distribution of a portfolio’s assets across different asset classes, such as cash, fixed income, and equities, to achieve the desired balance of risk and return.

**Example:** A balanced Canadian portfolio might have an asset mix of 50% equities, 40% fixed income, and 10% cash.

**Objective:** The asset mix should reflect the investor's risk tolerance and investment goals.

### Market Timing

**Definition:** Market timing is the strategy of making buy or sell decisions of financial assets by attempting to predict future market price movements.

**Example:** An investor might attempt to time the market by selling stocks before an anticipated downturn and buying back at lower prices.

**Risk:** Market timing is challenging and can lead to suboptimal investment outcomes if predictions are incorrect.

### Client Monitoring

**Definition:** Client monitoring is the ongoing process of reviewing and assessing the client’s changing financial situation, goals, and risk tolerance to ensure the portfolio remains aligned with their needs.

**Example:** A Canadian financial advisor might conduct annual reviews with clients to update their IPS and adjust the portfolio as needed.

**Importance:** Regular client monitoring ensures that investment strategies remain relevant and effective.
