## 16.6 Step 6: Evaluate Portfolio Performance

Evaluating portfolio performance is a critical step in the portfolio management process. It involves assessing how well a portfolio has performed in terms of both return and risk. This section will guide you through calculating and interpreting the total return and risk-adjusted rate of return of a portfolio, using practical examples and real-world scenarios relevant to the Canadian financial landscape.

### Calculating Total Return

Total return is a comprehensive measure of an investment's performance, encompassing both capital gains and income from dividends or interest. It provides a holistic view of how much an investment has grown over a specific period.

#### Total Return Calculation

The formula for calculating total return is:

{{< katex >}}
\text{Total Return (\%)} = \left( \frac{\text{Increase in Market Value} + \text{Cash Flows}}{\text{Beginning Value}} \right) \times 100
{{< /katex >}}

**Example:**

Consider a portfolio that begins the year with a market value of $100,000. By the end of the year, the market value has increased to $106,000. Additionally, the portfolio has generated $2,000 in dividends. The total return is calculated as follows:

{{< katex >}}
\text{Total Return (\%)} = \left( \frac{106,000 - 100,000 + 2,000}{100,000} \right) \times 100 = 8\%
{{< /katex >}}

This indicates that the portfolio has achieved an 8% total return over the year.

### Assessing Risk-Adjusted Return

While total return provides a measure of performance, it does not account for the risk taken to achieve that return. Risk-adjusted return metrics, such as the Sharpe Ratio, offer insights into how well a portfolio compensates investors for the risk undertaken.

#### Sharpe Ratio

The Sharpe Ratio is a widely used measure of risk-adjusted return. It evaluates the excess return per unit of risk, helping investors understand if the returns are worth the risk taken.

{{< katex >}}
\text{Sharpe Ratio} = \frac{R_p - R_f}{\sigma_p}
{{< /katex >}}

Where:
- \\( R_p \\) = Portfolio return
- \\( R_f \\) = Risk-free rate (often the yield on Canadian government bonds)
- \\( \sigma_p \\) = Portfolio’s standard deviation

**Example:**

Assume a portfolio has a return (\\( R_p \\)) of 8%, the risk-free rate (\\( R_f \\)) is 2%, and the portfolio's standard deviation (\\( \sigma_p \\)) is 10%. The Sharpe Ratio is calculated as:

{{< katex >}}
\text{Sharpe Ratio} = \frac{8\% - 2\%}{10\%} = 0.6
{{< /katex >}}

A Sharpe Ratio of 0.6 suggests that the portfolio provides 0.6% of excess return for each unit of risk.

### Performance Benchmarks

Benchmarks are essential for evaluating a portfolio's performance. They provide a standard against which the portfolio's returns can be compared, reflecting its asset allocation and investment strategy.

#### Selecting Appropriate Benchmarks

Choose benchmarks that align with the portfolio's investment objectives and asset mix. For instance, a Canadian equity portfolio might be compared against the S&P/TSX Composite Index, while a balanced portfolio could use a blend of equity and bond indices.

#### Evaluating Against Benchmarks

Compare the portfolio's total return and Sharpe Ratio to those of the selected benchmarks. This comparison helps determine whether the portfolio has outperformed or underperformed relative to the market.

### Interpreting Results

Interpreting the results of your performance evaluation involves comparing the calculated metrics with industry averages and historical performance.

- **Total Return:** A higher total return compared to benchmarks indicates strong performance.
- **Sharpe Ratio:** A higher Sharpe Ratio suggests better risk-adjusted performance. Compare it to the benchmark's Sharpe Ratio to assess relative success.

### Practical Example: Canadian Pension Fund

Consider a Canadian pension fund with a diversified portfolio. Over the past year, the fund achieved a total return of 7%, with a Sharpe Ratio of 0.7. The benchmark, a mix of Canadian equities and bonds, returned 6% with a Sharpe Ratio of 0.5. This indicates that the pension fund not only outperformed the benchmark in terms of total return but also provided better risk-adjusted returns.

### Best Practices and Common Pitfalls

- **Best Practices:**
  - Regularly review and adjust benchmarks to ensure they remain relevant.
  - Use multiple metrics to gain a comprehensive view of performance.
  - Consider the impact of Canadian tax laws on investment returns.

- **Common Pitfalls:**
  - Relying solely on total return without considering risk.
  - Using inappropriate benchmarks that do not reflect the portfolio's strategy.
  - Ignoring the impact of fees and expenses on returns.

### Conclusion

Evaluating portfolio performance is a nuanced process that requires a balance of return and risk assessment. By calculating total return and using risk-adjusted metrics like the Sharpe Ratio, investors can gain valuable insights into their portfolio's effectiveness. Regular performance evaluation, aligned with appropriate benchmarks, is crucial for informed investment decision-making.
