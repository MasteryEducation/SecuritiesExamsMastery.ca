## 16.2.1 Step 2: Design an Investment Policy Statement

Designing an Investment Policy Statement (IPS) is a critical step in the portfolio management process. An IPS serves as a strategic guide that outlines the framework for making investment decisions, ensuring that the portfolio aligns with the client's financial goals and risk tolerance. This section will provide a comprehensive guide to creating an effective IPS, focusing on key components such as operating rules, asset allocation, investment objectives, and performance appraisal, with a particular emphasis on the Canadian financial context.

### Operating Rules and Guidelines

Operating rules and guidelines form the backbone of an IPS, providing clear procedures for executing trades, managing cash flows, and handling client communications. These rules ensure that all actions taken within the portfolio are consistent with the client's objectives and regulatory requirements.

- **Trade Execution Procedures:** Define the process for executing trades, including the selection of brokers, trade authorization, and settlement procedures. In Canada, this may involve compliance with CIRO regulations and ensuring that trades are executed at the best possible price.

- **Cash Flow Management:** Outline strategies for managing cash inflows and outflows, including dividend reinvestment, interest payments, and withdrawals. This is particularly important for Canadian investors utilizing Registered Retirement Savings Plans (RRSPs) or Tax-Free Savings Accounts (TFSAs).

- **Client Communication:** Establish guidelines for regular communication with clients, including the frequency and format of reports, updates on portfolio performance, and discussions about changes in investment strategy.

### Asset Allocation

Asset allocation is a critical component of an IPS, specifying the target percentages for each asset class based on the client's objectives and constraints. A well-defined asset allocation strategy helps manage risk and optimize returns.

- **Target Asset Classes:** Identify the primary asset classes to be included in the portfolio, such as cash, fixed-income securities, and equities. For example, a typical Canadian portfolio might allocate 10% to cash, 40% to fixed-income securities, and 50% to equities.

- **Diversification Strategies:** Implement diversification strategies to reduce risk, such as investing in a mix of Canadian and international equities or including alternative investments like real estate or commodities.

- **Rebalancing Guidelines:** Establish rules for rebalancing the portfolio to maintain the desired asset allocation. This may involve periodic reviews and adjustments based on market conditions or changes in the client's financial situation.

### Investment Objectives and Constraints

Clearly stating the client's investment objectives and constraints is essential for guiding portfolio decisions and ensuring alignment with the client's financial goals.

- **Financial Goals:** Define the client's financial goals, such as retirement savings, education funding, or wealth preservation. These goals will influence the portfolio's risk-return profile and investment strategy.

- **Risk Tolerance:** Assess the client's risk tolerance, considering factors such as age, income stability, and investment experience. This assessment will help determine the appropriate level of risk for the portfolio.

- **Time Horizon:** Consider the client's investment time horizon, which will impact the selection of asset classes and investment strategies. A longer time horizon may allow for a higher allocation to equities, while a shorter horizon may necessitate a more conservative approach.

- **Unique Constraints:** Identify any unique constraints that may affect the portfolio, such as ethical considerations, tax implications, or liquidity needs. For example, a client may wish to avoid investments in certain industries or require a portion of the portfolio to be easily accessible for emergencies.

### List of Acceptable and Prohibited Investments

An IPS should outline which types of securities or investment strategies are permitted or restricted within the portfolio, ensuring that all investments align with the client's objectives and constraints.

- **Permitted Investments:** Specify the types of securities that are acceptable for inclusion in the portfolio, such as Canadian government bonds, blue-chip stocks, or index funds.

- **Prohibited Investments:** Identify any prohibited investments, such as high-risk derivatives, speculative stocks, or investments in certain industries. This may be based on the client's risk tolerance, ethical considerations, or regulatory restrictions.

### Performance Appraisal Method

Establishing a performance appraisal method is crucial for evaluating how well the portfolio has performed relative to its objectives and benchmarks.

- **Benchmarks:** Select appropriate benchmarks for each asset class, such as the S&P/TSX Composite Index for Canadian equities or the FTSE Canada Universe Bond Index for fixed-income securities. These benchmarks provide a standard for measuring portfolio performance.

- **Performance Metrics:** Define the metrics used to evaluate portfolio performance, such as total return, risk-adjusted return, or alpha. These metrics should align with the client's investment objectives and risk tolerance.

### Portfolio Review Schedule

Regular portfolio reviews are essential for ensuring that the portfolio remains aligned with the IPS and adapting to changes in market conditions or the client's financial situation.

- **Review Frequency:** Set regular intervals for reviewing the portfolio, such as quarterly or annually. These reviews should assess the portfolio's performance, asset allocation, and alignment with the client's objectives.

- **Adjustment Procedures:** Establish procedures for making adjustments to the portfolio, such as rebalancing asset allocations or changing investment strategies. These adjustments should be based on a thorough analysis of market conditions and the client's evolving needs.

### Glossary

- **Benchmark:** A standard against which the performance of a security, mutual fund, or investment manager can be measured. Benchmarks are used to evaluate the relative performance of a portfolio.

- **Performance Appraisal:** The process of evaluating how well the portfolio has performed relative to its objectives and benchmarks. This involves analyzing performance metrics and making adjustments as needed.

### Practical Example: Canadian Pension Fund

Consider a Canadian pension fund that aims to provide stable retirement income for its beneficiaries. The fund's IPS might include the following components:

- **Operating Rules:** Procedures for executing trades through approved brokers and managing cash flows to meet pension obligations.

- **Asset Allocation:** A diversified mix of 30% Canadian equities, 20% international equities, 40% fixed-income securities, and 10% alternative investments.

- **Investment Objectives:** Achieving a long-term return of 6% per annum with moderate risk, while maintaining sufficient liquidity to meet pension payouts.

- **Acceptable Investments:** Canadian government bonds, large-cap equities, and real estate investment trusts (REITs).

- **Performance Appraisal:** Using the S&P/TSX Composite Index and FTSE Canada Universe Bond Index as benchmarks, with quarterly performance reviews.

By following these guidelines, the pension fund can effectively manage its portfolio to meet its objectives and provide financial security for its beneficiaries.

### Conclusion

Designing an Investment Policy Statement is a vital step in the portfolio management process, providing a clear framework for making investment decisions and ensuring alignment with the client's financial goals. By incorporating key components such as operating rules, asset allocation, investment objectives, and performance appraisal, an IPS can guide portfolio management and help achieve desired outcomes. As you apply these principles to your own financial planning or investment decisions, consider the unique aspects of the Canadian financial landscape and leverage available resources to enhance your understanding.
