## 24.7.2 Transferring Income

Transferring income within a family can be a strategic approach to tax planning, potentially reducing the overall tax burden by shifting income to family members in lower tax brackets. However, the Canadian tax system has specific rules, known as attribution rules, designed to prevent tax avoidance through such transfers. Understanding these rules and their exceptions is crucial for effective financial planning.

### Understanding Income Transferring

Income transferring involves reallocating income-generating assets or income itself from one family member to another. The primary goal is to take advantage of the progressive nature of the Canadian tax system, where higher income levels are taxed at higher rates. By transferring income to family members who are in lower tax brackets, families can potentially reduce their overall tax liability.

### Attribution Rules: Preventing Tax Avoidance

The Canadian government has established attribution rules to prevent individuals from avoiding taxes by transferring income or assets to family members. These rules ensure that the income or capital gains generated from transferred assets are attributed back to the original owner for tax purposes.

#### Key Attribution Rules

1. **Spousal Attribution**: When a taxpayer transfers or loans property to their spouse or common-law partner, any income or capital gains from that property are attributed back to the taxpayer.

2. **Minor Children Attribution**: If a taxpayer transfers or loans property to a minor child, any income (but not capital gains) from that property is attributed back to the taxpayer.

3. **Trusts and Partnerships**: Similar attribution rules apply to income earned through trusts or partnerships where the beneficiaries or partners are family members.

### Exceptions to Attribution Rules

There are specific situations where attribution rules do not apply, allowing for legitimate income transferring without tax repercussions:

1. **Transfers Due to Marriage Breakdown**: Transfers of property between spouses as part of a divorce or separation agreement are not subject to attribution rules. This exception facilitates the equitable division of assets without tax penalties.

2. **Arm's Length Transactions**: If the transfer of property is conducted at fair market value and involves an arm's length transaction, attribution rules may not apply.

3. **Gifts to Adult Children**: Income from property gifted to adult children is not attributed back to the parent, allowing for strategic income shifting.

### Tax Treatment of Income and Capital Gains

When income or capital gains arise from transferred property, their tax treatment depends on the attribution rules:

- **Income**: If attribution rules apply, the income is taxed in the hands of the original owner. If not, it is taxed in the hands of the recipient.

- **Capital Gains**: Generally, capital gains are not subject to attribution when transferred to minor children, meaning they are taxed in the hands of the child. However, gains transferred to a spouse are attributed back to the original owner.

### Practical Examples

#### Example 1: Spousal Income Transfer

John transfers a portfolio of dividend-paying stocks to his wife, Mary. Due to spousal attribution rules, the dividends received by Mary are attributed back to John and taxed at his marginal rate.

#### Example 2: Transfer to Minor Child

Lisa transfers a rental property to her 15-year-old son, Alex. The rental income is attributed back to Lisa and taxed at her rate. However, if the property appreciates and is sold, the capital gains are taxed in Alex's hands.

#### Example 3: Transfer Due to Marriage Breakdown

During their divorce, Sarah transfers her share of the family home to her ex-husband, Tom, as part of their settlement. This transfer is exempt from attribution rules, and any future income or gains from the property are taxed in Tom's hands.

### Best Practices and Considerations

- **Consult a Tax Professional**: Given the complexity of attribution rules, seeking advice from a tax professional can help navigate potential pitfalls and optimize tax strategies.

- **Document Transactions**: Maintain thorough documentation of all transfers, including valuations and agreements, to support the legitimacy of transactions.

- **Consider Long-Term Implications**: Evaluate the long-term impact of income transfers, including potential changes in tax laws and family circumstances.

### Conclusion

Transferring income within a family can be a powerful tool for tax planning, but it requires careful consideration of Canadian attribution rules. By understanding these rules and their exceptions, families can strategically manage their tax liabilities while complying with legal requirements. Always consider consulting with a tax professional to tailor strategies to specific family and financial situations.
