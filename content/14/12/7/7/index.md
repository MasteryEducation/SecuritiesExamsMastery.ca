## 24.7.7 Gifting

In the realm of Canadian tax planning, gifting investments to family members can serve as a strategic tool to manage tax liabilities and facilitate wealth transfer. This section delves into the nuances of gifting as a tax planning strategy, examining the deemed disposition rule, the tax consequences of capital gains or losses, and scenarios where gifting can be advantageous without triggering attribution rules.

### Understanding Gifting of Investments

Gifting involves transferring ownership of an asset, such as stocks, bonds, or mutual funds, to another individual without receiving payment in return. In Canada, gifting investments to family members can be an effective way to reduce taxable income, manage estate planning, and potentially lower the overall tax burden on investment income.

### The Deemed Disposition Rule

When an individual gifts an investment, the Canada Revenue Agency (CRA) considers this a "deemed disposition." This means that the asset is treated as if it were sold at its fair market value (FMV) on the date of the gift. Consequently, the donor may realize a capital gain or loss, depending on the difference between the FMV and the adjusted cost base (ACB) of the asset.

#### Example of Deemed Disposition

Consider an investor, Jane, who purchased shares in a Canadian corporation for $10,000. Over time, the value of these shares appreciates to $15,000. If Jane decides to gift these shares to her son, the CRA will treat this as a deemed disposition at the FMV of $15,000. Jane will realize a capital gain of $5,000 ($15,000 FMV - $10,000 ACB), which will be subject to capital gains tax.

### Tax Consequences of Capital Gains or Losses

Upon deemed disposition, any capital gain is included in the donor's taxable income, with 50% of the gain being taxable. Conversely, if the asset's FMV is lower than its ACB, a capital loss is realized, which can be used to offset other capital gains.

#### Capital Gains Tax Calculation

Using the previous example, Jane's taxable capital gain would be $2,500 (50% of $5,000). This amount is added to her taxable income for the year, potentially increasing her overall tax liability.

### Beneficial Scenarios for Gifting

Gifting investments can be particularly beneficial in scenarios where:

1. **Income Splitting**: By transferring income-generating assets to family members in lower tax brackets, the overall family tax burden can be reduced. However, care must be taken to avoid attribution rules, which can attribute the income back to the donor.

2. **Estate Planning**: Gifting can be a strategic component of estate planning, allowing for the transfer of wealth to heirs during the donor's lifetime, potentially reducing estate taxes and probate fees.

3. **Charitable Giving**: Donating appreciated securities to a registered charity can eliminate the capital gains tax on the appreciated value, while also providing a charitable donation tax credit.

### Avoiding Attribution Rules

Attribution rules are designed to prevent income splitting by attributing income or capital gains back to the original owner when assets are transferred to a spouse or minor children. However, these rules do not apply when gifting to adult children or other relatives, making it a viable strategy for tax planning.

#### Example of Avoiding Attribution

If Jane gifts her shares to her adult daughter, the future income and capital gains from these shares will be taxed in her daughter's hands, not Jane's, thus avoiding attribution.

### Practical Examples and Case Studies

#### Case Study: Gifting to Adult Children

John, a high-income earner, gifts $50,000 worth of mutual funds to his adult son, Alex, who is in a lower tax bracket. The mutual funds generate $2,000 in annual dividends. By transferring the funds, John avoids paying tax on the dividends at his higher rate, and Alex pays tax at his lower rate, resulting in overall tax savings for the family.

#### Case Study: Charitable Gifting

Emily owns shares with a significant unrealized capital gain. She donates these shares to a registered Canadian charity. By doing so, Emily eliminates the capital gains tax on the appreciated value and receives a charitable donation tax credit, enhancing her tax efficiency.

### Best Practices and Common Pitfalls

**Best Practices:**
- Consult with a tax professional to understand the implications of gifting and to ensure compliance with CRA regulations.
- Consider the timing of the gift to optimize tax outcomes, such as gifting before year-end to realize tax benefits sooner.

**Common Pitfalls:**
- Failing to account for the deemed disposition rule, leading to unexpected capital gains tax liabilities.
- Overlooking attribution rules, which can negate the intended tax benefits of gifting.

### Conclusion

Gifting investments can be a powerful tool in Canadian tax planning, offering opportunities for income splitting, estate planning, and charitable giving. By understanding the deemed disposition rule and carefully navigating attribution rules, individuals can effectively leverage gifting to achieve their financial goals.
