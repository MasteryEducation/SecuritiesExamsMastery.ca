## Glossary for Chapter 24: Canadian Taxation

Understanding the intricacies of Canadian taxation is crucial for financial professionals and investors alike. This glossary provides detailed explanations of key terms and concepts that are essential for navigating the Canadian tax landscape, particularly in the context of the Canadian Securities Course (CSC®). Each term is explained with clarity, supported by practical examples and real-world applications to enhance comprehension and facilitate practical application.

### Annuity

An annuity is a financial product that provides a fixed stream of payments to an individual, typically used as a source of income during retirement. Annuities can be purchased from insurance companies and are designed to provide financial security by ensuring a steady income stream. For example, a retiree might purchase an annuity with a portion of their retirement savings to receive monthly payments for the rest of their life, thus mitigating the risk of outliving their savings.

### Past Service Pension Adjustment (PSPA)

The Past Service Pension Adjustment (PSPA) reflects additional pension benefits earned for service in previous years when a pension plan is upgraded. This adjustment is crucial for accurately calculating an individual's RRSP contribution room. For instance, if an employer enhances the pension benefits retroactively, a PSPA is calculated to adjust the employee's RRSP contribution limits accordingly.

### Attribution Rules

Attribution rules are tax regulations designed to prevent income splitting by attributing income back to the transferor under certain conditions. These rules are particularly relevant in family tax planning, where income might be shifted to a lower-income family member to reduce overall tax liability. For example, if a high-income earner transfers investment assets to a spouse, the income generated from those assets may be attributed back to the original owner for tax purposes.

### Pension Adjustment (PA)

The Pension Adjustment (PA) is an amount that reduces an individual's RRSP contribution room based on their participation in a registered pension plan. This adjustment ensures that the tax advantages of RRSPs and pension plans are balanced. For example, if an employee contributes to a defined benefit pension plan, their PA will reflect the value of the pension benefits accrued, reducing their RRSP contribution room.

### Canada Education Savings Grant (CESG)

The Canada Education Savings Grant (CESG) is a government initiative that matches contributions to a Registered Education Savings Plan (RESP), encouraging savings for post-secondary education. The CESG provides a 20% match on annual contributions up to a specified limit, enhancing the growth potential of education savings. For instance, if a parent contributes $2,500 to their child's RESP, they may receive a $500 CESG, boosting the total savings.

### Pooled Registered Pension Plan (PRPP)

A Pooled Registered Pension Plan (PRPP) is a retirement savings plan designed to provide accessible pension benefits with lower management costs through pooling contributions. PRPPs are particularly beneficial for small businesses and self-employed individuals who may not have access to traditional pension plans. By pooling resources, PRPPs offer economies of scale, reducing administrative costs and enhancing investment opportunities.

### Carrying Charges

Carrying charges are expenses related to earning investment income that are tax-deductible. These may include interest on loans used to purchase investments, investment management fees, and certain legal and accounting fees. For example, if an investor borrows money to buy stocks, the interest paid on the loan can be deducted from their taxable income, reducing their overall tax liability.

### Registered Education Savings Plan (RESP)

A Registered Education Savings Plan (RESP) is a savings plan that provides tax-deferred growth for a beneficiary’s post-secondary education expenses. Contributions to an RESP are not tax-deductible, but the investment income grows tax-free until withdrawn. When funds are withdrawn for educational purposes, they are taxed in the hands of the student, who typically has a lower tax rate. This makes RESPs an effective tool for education savings.

### Contribution in Kind

A contribution in kind involves transferring securities or property into a registered plan, such as an RRSP, and is treated as a deemed disposition at fair market value for tax purposes. This means that any capital gains on the transferred assets are realized at the time of the transfer. For example, if an investor transfers shares with a significant unrealized gain into their RRSP, they must report the gain as income in the year of the transfer.

### Registered Pension Plan (RPP)

A Registered Pension Plan (RPP) is a pension plan registered with the Canada Revenue Agency (CRA) that provides retirement benefits to employees. RPPs can be either defined benefit plans, where the retirement benefit is predetermined, or defined contribution plans, where the benefit depends on investment performance. RPPs offer tax advantages, as contributions are tax-deductible and investment income grows tax-free until withdrawal.

### Deemed Disposition

Deemed disposition is a tax concept where the sale of an asset is assumed to have occurred for tax purposes, even if no actual sale took place. This can occur in situations such as the transfer of assets to a registered plan or the death of a taxpayer. Deemed dispositions trigger capital gains or losses, which must be reported on the taxpayer's return. For example, when a taxpayer emigrates from Canada, they are deemed to have disposed of their assets at fair market value, potentially resulting in taxable capital gains.

### Registered Retirement Income Fund (RRIF)

A Registered Retirement Income Fund (RRIF) is a tax-deferred retirement income vehicle that requires minimum annual withdrawals based on age and funds held. RRIFs are typically used to convert RRSP savings into a stream of retirement income. While contributions to a RRIF are not allowed, the investment income continues to grow tax-free until withdrawn. The mandatory withdrawals ensure that the funds are eventually taxed.

### Deferred Annuity

A deferred annuity is an annuity contract where income payments begin at a future date, allowing for tax-deferred growth until withdrawals. Deferred annuities are often used as a long-term investment strategy, providing a guaranteed income stream in retirement. For example, an individual might purchase a deferred annuity at age 50, with payments starting at age 65, allowing the investment to grow tax-deferred for 15 years.

### Registered Retirement Savings Plan (RRSP)

A Registered Retirement Savings Plan (RRSP) is a tax-deferred retirement savings account that allows individuals to save for retirement with tax-deductible contributions. Contributions to an RRSP reduce taxable income, and the investment income grows tax-free until withdrawal. Withdrawals are taxed as income, typically at a lower rate in retirement. RRSPs are a cornerstone of Canadian retirement planning, offering significant tax advantages.

### Defined Benefit Plans (DBP)

Defined Benefit Plans (DBP) are pension plans where the retirement benefit is predetermined based on factors like salary and years of service. DBPs provide a predictable income stream in retirement, as the employer bears the investment risk. For example, a DBP might promise a retiree 2% of their final salary for each year of service, ensuring a stable retirement income regardless of market fluctuations.

### Self-Directed RRSP

A Self-Directed RRSP allows the holder to make their own investment decisions, offering greater control over investment choices. Investors can hold a wide range of assets, including stocks, bonds, mutual funds, and real estate. Self-directed RRSPs are ideal for experienced investors who want to actively manage their retirement savings and tailor their investment strategy to their specific goals.

### Defined Contribution Plans (DCP)

Defined Contribution Plans (DCP) are pension plans where contributions are defined, and benefits depend on investment performance. Unlike DBPs, the retirement benefit is not guaranteed, as the employee bears the investment risk. Contributions are typically made by both the employer and employee, and the accumulated funds are used to purchase an annuity or drawdown in retirement.

### Spousal Registered Retirement Savings Plan (SRRSP)

A Spousal Registered Retirement Savings Plan (SRRSP) is an RRSP registered in one spouse’s name to allow the other spouse to contribute and potentially benefit from income splitting. Contributions to a SRRSP are tax-deductible for the contributing spouse, while withdrawals are taxed in the hands of the plan holder, potentially at a lower rate. This strategy can reduce the overall tax burden in retirement.

### Fiscal Year

The fiscal year is the one-year period used by businesses and organizations for accounting and financial reporting purposes. It may not coincide with the calendar year and is chosen to best reflect the business cycle. For example, a company might choose a fiscal year ending March 31 to align with seasonal business patterns, ensuring accurate financial reporting.

### Superficial Losses

Superficial losses are capital losses that are not deductible for tax purposes because the same or identical securities are repurchased within 30 days before or after the sale. This rule prevents taxpayers from claiming a loss while maintaining their investment position. For example, if an investor sells shares at a loss and repurchases them within 30 days, the loss is deemed superficial and cannot be used to offset capital gains.

### Income Splitting

Income splitting is a strategy to distribute income among family members to minimize overall tax liability by shifting income from higher to lower tax brackets. This can be achieved through various means, such as contributing to a spousal RRSP or transferring investment income. While attribution rules limit some forms of income splitting, strategic planning can still yield significant tax savings.

### Tax-Free Savings Account (TFSA)

A Tax-Free Savings Account (TFSA) is a savings account that allows individuals to earn tax-free investment income and make tax-free withdrawals. Contributions to a TFSA are not tax-deductible, but the investment income and withdrawals are tax-free. TFSAs offer flexibility, as funds can be withdrawn at any time without penalty, making them an excellent tool for both short-term savings and long-term investment growth.

### Marginal Tax Rate

The marginal tax rate is the rate of tax applied to the last dollar of income earned, determining the tax payable on additional income. Understanding marginal tax rates is crucial for effective tax planning, as it influences decisions on income splitting, RRSP contributions, and other tax strategies. For example, if an individual's marginal tax rate is 30%, earning an additional $1,000 would result in $300 in taxes.

### Withholding Tax

Withholding tax is the amount of income tax that is deducted at the source of income before it is paid to the recipient. This ensures that taxes are collected throughout the year, reducing the risk of underpayment. Withholding tax applies to various types of income, including employment income, dividends, and interest. For example, employers withhold tax from employee paychecks based on estimated annual income.

### Money Purchase Plans (MPP)

Money Purchase Plans (MPP) are defined contribution pension plans where contributions are fixed, and retirement benefits depend on investment performance. MPPs offer flexibility, as employees can choose their investment options, but they also bear the investment risk. Contributions are typically made by both the employer and employee, and the accumulated funds are used to provide retirement income.

### Capital Gain

A capital gain is the profit realized from the sale of a capital asset when the sale price exceeds the adjusted cost base. Capital gains are subject to tax, but only 50% of the gain is included in taxable income. For example, if an investor sells shares for $10,000 with an adjusted cost base of $7,000, the capital gain is $3,000, and $1,500 is included in taxable income.

### Capital Loss

A capital loss is the loss incurred when the sale price of a capital asset is less than its adjusted cost base. Capital losses can be used to offset capital gains, reducing taxable income. For example, if an investor sells shares for $5,000 with an adjusted cost base of $7,000, the capital loss is $2,000, which can be applied against capital gains in the current or future years.

### Adjusted Cost Base (ACB)

The adjusted cost base (ACB) is the original cost of an investment adjusted for factors like commissions, reinvested dividends, and additional purchases. ACB is used to calculate capital gains or losses when an asset is sold. For example, if an investor purchases shares for $1,000 and reinvests $100 in dividends, the ACB is $1,100, which is used to determine the capital gain or loss upon sale.

### Contribution Room

Contribution room is the maximum amount an individual can contribute to their RRSP or other registered plans, based on income and previous contributions. Contribution room accumulates annually and can be carried forward if not used. For example, if an individual's RRSP contribution limit is $10,000 and they contribute $5,000, the remaining $5,000 can be carried forward to future years.

### Federal Income Tax Rates

Federal income tax rates are the progressive tax rates applied by the federal government on taxable income across different income brackets. These rates determine the amount of federal tax owed based on an individual's taxable income. For example, in Canada, the federal tax rate might be 15% on the first $50,000 of income and 20% on the next $50,000, resulting in a higher tax rate for higher income levels.

### Provincial Income Tax

Provincial income tax is the additional income tax imposed by Canadian provinces, contributing to the total marginal tax rate. Each province sets its own tax rates and brackets, which are combined with federal rates to determine the overall tax liability. For example, an individual in Ontario might pay a combined federal and provincial tax rate of 30% on their income.

### Contribution Limit

The contribution limit is the maximum allowable contributions to a registered plan in a given year, subject to specific rules and adjustments. Contribution limits are set annually and are based on factors such as earned income and pension adjustments. For example, the RRSP contribution limit is typically 18% of earned income, up to a specified maximum.

### Tax Credits

Tax credits are amounts that reduce the total tax payable, available for various purposes such as dividends and eligible expenses. Tax credits can be non-refundable, reducing tax to zero, or refundable, providing a refund if the credit exceeds tax owed. For example, the dividend tax credit reduces the taxes payable on dividend income, reflecting taxes already paid at the corporate level.

### Net Tax Payable

Net tax payable is the total tax owed after applying all deductions and tax credits. It represents the final amount due to the government after accounting for all eligible reductions. For example, if an individual's gross tax liability is $10,000 and they have $2,000 in tax credits, the net tax payable is $8,000.

### Withholding Tax Rates

Withholding tax rates are the specific rates at which tax is withheld from different amounts of income at the source. These rates vary based on the type of income and the taxpayer's estimated annual income. For example, employment income might be subject to a withholding rate of 20%, while dividends might have a different rate.

### Earned Income

Earned income is income derived from active participation in employment or business, used to determine contribution limits for registered plans. Earned income includes wages, salaries, and business income, but excludes investment income. For example, an individual's RRSP contribution limit is based on their earned income from the previous year.

### Dividend Tax Credit

The dividend tax credit is a non-refundable tax credit that reduces the taxes payable on dividend income, reflecting taxes already paid at the corporate level. This credit ensures that dividend income is not subject to double taxation. For example, if an individual receives $1,000 in eligible dividends, the dividend tax credit reduces the tax owed on that income.

### Employment Income

Employment income is income earned from active participation in employment, including wages, salaries, and benefits. Employment income is subject to withholding tax and is used to determine eligibility for various tax credits and deductions. For example, an individual's RRSP contribution limit is based on their employment income from the previous year.

### Non-Deductible Expenses

Non-deductible expenses are expenses that cannot be deducted from taxable income, such as brokerage fees for capital gains-only investments. These expenses do not reduce taxable income and must be borne by the taxpayer. For example, if an investor incurs $500 in brokerage fees for purchasing stocks, these fees are not deductible against capital gains.

### Tax Deferral Plan

A tax deferral plan is a savings plan that allows taxpayers to delay paying taxes on income until a future date, typically retirement. Tax deferral plans, such as RRSPs and RRIFs, provide tax advantages by allowing investment income to grow tax-free until withdrawal. For example, contributions to an RRSP are tax-deductible, and the investment income is not taxed until it is withdrawn in retirement.

By familiarizing yourself with these terms and concepts, you will be better equipped to navigate the complexities of Canadian taxation and make informed financial decisions. Understanding these key elements is essential for anyone preparing for the CSC® exam or seeking to enhance their knowledge of Canadian financial regulations.
