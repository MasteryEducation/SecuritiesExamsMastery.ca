## 19.3.3 Active Exchange-Traded Funds

Active Exchange-Traded Funds (ETFs) represent a dynamic segment of the investment landscape, offering investors the potential for enhanced returns through strategic portfolio management. Unlike their passive counterparts, active ETFs are managed with the intention of outperforming specific benchmarks. This section delves into the nuances of active ETFs, exploring their management styles, transparency challenges, tax implications, and operational constraints. We will also examine real-world examples from the Canadian market and discuss the benefits and risks associated with these investment vehicles.

### Management Style

Active ETFs differ significantly from passive ETFs in terms of portfolio management and trading frequency. While passive ETFs aim to replicate the performance of a specific index by holding a fixed basket of securities, active ETFs employ a more hands-on approach. Managers of active ETFs actively select securities, adjust allocations, and employ various investment strategies to outperform their benchmarks.

#### Investment Strategies

Active ETF managers utilize a range of strategies to achieve their goals, including:

- **Stock Picking:** Identifying undervalued securities with strong growth potential.
- **Sector Rotation:** Shifting investments between sectors based on economic cycles and market trends.
- **Market Timing:** Adjusting exposure to different asset classes based on market conditions.

These strategies require frequent trading and adjustments, which can lead to higher portfolio turnover compared to passive ETFs.

### Transparency Challenges

One of the key challenges facing active ETFs is transparency. Unlike passive ETFs, which typically disclose their holdings daily, active ETFs may limit disclosure to protect their competitive advantage. This lack of transparency can be a double-edged sword:

- **Competitive Advantage:** By not disclosing holdings frequently, active managers can prevent other market participants from replicating their strategies.
- **Investor Trust:** Limited transparency can lead to concerns among investors who prefer to know the exact composition of their investments.

Active ETF managers must strike a balance between maintaining a competitive edge and providing sufficient information to satisfy investor demands.

### Tax Efficiency

Active ETFs are generally less tax-efficient than passive ETFs due to their higher portfolio turnover. Frequent buying and selling of securities can trigger capital gains, which are distributed to investors and subject to taxation. This can result in higher tax liabilities for investors, particularly in non-registered accounts.

#### Implications of Higher Turnover

- **Capital Gains Distributions:** Active ETFs may distribute capital gains more frequently, impacting after-tax returns.
- **Tax Planning:** Investors need to consider the tax implications of active ETFs, especially when held outside of tax-advantaged accounts like RRSPs or TFSAs.

### Management Expense Ratios (MERs)

Active ETFs typically have higher Management Expense Ratios (MERs) compared to passive ETFs. This is due to the costs associated with active management, including research, trading, and portfolio adjustments.

#### Cost Differences

- **Active Management Costs:** The need for skilled managers and research teams contributes to higher MERs.
- **Impact on Returns:** Higher MERs can erode returns, making it crucial for active managers to outperform their benchmarks to justify the additional costs.

### Operational Constraints

Active ETF managers face unique operational constraints due to the in-kind creation and redemption process. This process, while tax-efficient, can limit the flexibility of active managers in executing trades and adjusting portfolios.

#### Impact on Trading Activities

- **Liquidity Management:** Active managers must ensure sufficient liquidity to accommodate redemptions without disrupting their strategies.
- **Portfolio Adjustments:** Constraints may impact the ability to swiftly adjust portfolios in response to market changes.

### Examples of Active ETFs

In the Canadian market, several active ETFs have gained prominence. One such example is the **Horizons Active Emerging Markets Dividend ETF**. This ETF aims to provide exposure to dividend-paying companies in emerging markets, leveraging active management to identify opportunities and manage risks.

#### Investment Objectives and Strategies

- **Objective:** To achieve long-term capital growth and income by investing in emerging market equities.
- **Strategy:** Active selection of dividend-paying stocks with strong fundamentals and growth potential.

### Benefits and Risks

Active ETFs offer several advantages, including the potential for higher returns and strategic flexibility. However, they also come with risks that investors must consider.

#### Advantages

- **Higher Returns:** The potential to outperform benchmarks through skilled management.
- **Strategic Flexibility:** Ability to adapt to changing market conditions and capitalize on opportunities.

#### Risks

- **Higher Costs:** Increased MERs can impact net returns.
- **Lower Tax Efficiency:** Frequent trading can lead to higher tax liabilities.

### Glossary

- **Active ETF:** An ETF that is actively managed with the goal of outperforming its benchmark index.
- **Portfolio Turnover:** The rate at which securities within a fund are bought and sold.
- **Transparency:** The degree to which an ETF discloses its holdings and investment strategies.
- **Management Expense Ratio (MER):** The total annual fees expressed as a percentage of the average net assets of the fund.

### Conclusion

Active ETFs represent a compelling option for investors seeking to leverage professional management to achieve superior returns. However, they require careful consideration of costs, tax implications, and transparency issues. By understanding the intricacies of active ETFs, investors can make informed decisions that align with their financial goals and risk tolerance.
