## 19.4 Identify and Explain the Risks Specific to ETFs

Exchange-Traded Funds (ETFs) have become a popular investment vehicle due to their flexibility, cost-effectiveness, and diversification benefits. However, like any investment, ETFs come with their own set of risks. Understanding these risks is crucial for investors to make informed decisions and align their investment strategies with their financial goals. In this section, we will delve into the specific risks associated with ETFs, particularly within the Canadian context.

### Tracking Error

**Concept and Causes:**

Tracking error refers to the deviation between the performance of an ETF and its benchmark index. This discrepancy can arise from several factors:

- **Fees:** Management fees and other operational costs can cause an ETF to underperform its benchmark.
- **Sampling Methods:** Some ETFs use a sampling approach rather than full replication of the index, which can lead to differences in performance.
- **Cash Drag:** Holding cash for liquidity purposes can result in underperformance, especially in rising markets.

**Impact on Performance:**

Tracking error can significantly affect an investor's returns, particularly if the ETF consistently underperforms its benchmark. For instance, if a Canadian ETF tracking the S&P/TSX Composite Index has a high tracking error, it may not fully capture the index's gains, impacting long-term investment outcomes.

### Concentration Risk

**Understanding Concentration Risk:**

Concentration risk occurs when a small number of holdings dominate an ETF's portfolio, leading to a lack of diversification. This is particularly prevalent in sector-specific or thematic ETFs.

**Implications:**

- **Diversification:** A concentrated ETF may not provide the diversification benefits typically associated with ETFs, exposing investors to unsystematic risk.
- **Volatility:** High concentration in a few securities can lead to increased volatility, as the performance of these securities can disproportionately affect the ETF's overall performance.

### Composition Risk

**Exploring Composition Risk:**

The specific composition and weighting methodologies of an ETF can introduce unique risks. For example, an ETF that heavily weights certain sectors or securities may be more susceptible to sector-specific downturns.

**Alignment with Investment Objectives:**

Investors should ensure that an ETF's composition aligns with their investment objectives and risk tolerance. For instance, a growth-oriented investor may prefer an ETF with a higher allocation to technology stocks, while a conservative investor might opt for a more balanced approach.

### Securities Lending Risk

**Role and Risks:**

Securities lending involves lending ETF holdings to short sellers to generate additional income. While this can enhance returns, it introduces risks such as:

- **Counterparty Risk:** The risk that the borrower may default on their obligations.
- **Default Risk:** The possibility that the collateral provided may not be sufficient to cover the value of the lent securities.

**Mitigation Measures:**

ETFs often require collateral to mitigate these risks, ensuring that the value of the collateral exceeds the value of the lent securities.

### Derivative and Leverage Risks

**Risks of Derivatives and Leverage:**

ETFs that utilize derivatives or leverage can pose additional risks:

- **Leverage:** Amplifies both gains and losses, increasing the potential for significant volatility.
- **Path Dependency:** Leveraged ETFs may not perform as expected over longer periods due to the compounding effects of daily leverage.

**Example:**

A Canadian leveraged ETF aiming to double the daily return of the S&P/TSX 60 Index may experience significant deviations from this target over time, especially in volatile markets.

### Liquidity and Cash Drag

**Liquidity Concerns:**

Liquidity issues in the underlying securities can affect an ETF's liquidity and tracking accuracy. In times of market stress, this can lead to wider bid-ask spreads and increased trading costs.

**Cash Drag:**

Holding excess cash can lead to cash drag, where the ETF underperforms its benchmark due to uninvested cash not participating in market gains.

### Rebalancing Risks

**Rebalancing Challenges:**

ETFs must periodically rebalance their portfolios to maintain alignment with their benchmark indexes. This process can introduce risks such as:

- **Market Impact:** Large trades required for rebalancing can impact market prices, especially in less liquid markets.
- **Timing:** Inefficient rebalancing can exacerbate tracking error.

### Currency Hedging Risks

**Currency Hedging in ETFs:**

ETFs investing in non-Canadian assets may use currency hedging to mitigate the impact of currency fluctuations. However, this introduces additional risks:

- **Hedging Costs:** The costs associated with hedging can contribute to tracking error.
- **Currency Fluctuations:** Unhedged currency exposure can lead to significant performance deviations from the benchmark.

### Glossary

- **Tracking Error:** The deviation between the return of an ETF and its benchmark index.
- **Concentration Risk:** The risk arising from holding a large proportion of an ETF's assets in a few securities.
- **Composition Risk:** Risks introduced by the specific holdings and their weightings within an ETF.
- **Securities Lending:** The practice of lending ETF holdings to short sellers to generate additional income.
- **Counterparty Risk:** The risk that the other party in a financial contract will default on their obligations.
- **Leverage:** The use of borrowed capital to increase the potential return of an investment.
- **Cash Drag:** The negative impact on performance when a fund holds excess cash instead of being fully invested.
- **Rebalancing:** The process of realigning the proportions of assets in a portfolio to maintain desired asset allocation.
- **Currency Hedging:** Strategies used to reduce the risk of adverse currency movements affecting investment returns.

### Conclusion

Understanding the specific risks associated with ETFs is essential for investors looking to incorporate these instruments into their portfolios. By recognizing and managing these risks, investors can better align their ETF investments with their financial goals and risk tolerance. As the Canadian ETF market continues to evolve, staying informed about these risks will be crucial for successful investment outcomes.
