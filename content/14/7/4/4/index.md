## 19.4.4 Risk Related to Securities Lending

Securities lending is a crucial mechanism within the financial markets, particularly for Exchange-Traded Funds (ETFs), as it provides a means to generate additional income. However, it also introduces specific risks that need careful management. This section delves into the intricacies of securities lending, focusing on its role, associated risks, regulatory frameworks, and strategies for risk mitigation.

### Securities Lending Explained

Securities lending involves the temporary transfer of securities from one party (the lender) to another (the borrower), typically short sellers, in exchange for a fee and collateral. For ETFs, this practice can enhance returns by generating additional income, which can be particularly beneficial in low-yield environments. The borrower, often a short seller, uses the securities to cover short positions, while the ETF benefits from the lending fees.

#### How ETFs Engage in Securities Lending

ETFs lend their holdings to short sellers, who pay a fee for the privilege. This fee is a source of revenue for the ETF, potentially boosting its overall returns. The borrower provides collateral, usually in the form of cash or other securities, to secure the loan. This collateral acts as a safeguard for the ETF, ensuring that it can recover its assets if the borrower defaults.

### Creditworthiness and Default Risk

A critical aspect of securities lending is assessing the creditworthiness of borrowers. The risk of borrower default is a significant concern, as it can impact the ETF's holdings and, consequently, investor returns. If a borrower defaults, the ETF may face challenges in recovering the lent securities, especially if the collateral is insufficient or has depreciated in value.

#### Evaluating Borrower Creditworthiness

ETFs must implement robust credit assessment processes to evaluate the financial stability and reliability of potential borrowers. This involves analyzing the borrower's credit history, financial statements, and market reputation. By setting stringent credit guidelines, ETFs can mitigate the risk of borrower default.

### Collateral Requirements

Collateral is a fundamental component of securities lending, serving as a protective measure for the lender. The quality and concentration of collateral are crucial factors in mitigating lending risks. High-quality, diversified collateral reduces the risk of loss if the borrower defaults.

#### Types of Collateral

Collateral can take various forms, including cash, government securities, or other high-quality assets. The choice of collateral depends on the lender's risk appetite and the borrower's credit profile. ETFs typically require collateral that exceeds the value of the lent securities, providing a buffer against market fluctuations.

### Regulatory Compliance

In Canada, securities lending practices within ETFs are governed by regulatory frameworks such as National Instrument 81-102 (NI 81-102). These regulations set guidelines for the percentage of an ETF's Net Asset Value (NAV) that can be lent out, ensuring that the fund maintains sufficient liquidity and risk management.

#### Key Regulatory Guidelines

NI 81-102 imposes limits on the extent of securities lending, typically capping it at a certain percentage of the ETF's NAV. This regulation ensures that ETFs do not overextend their lending activities, maintaining a balance between income generation and risk exposure.

### Managing Securities Lending Risks

ETFs employ various strategies to manage and mitigate the risks associated with securities lending. One common approach is using third-party lending agents, who facilitate the lending process and provide expertise in borrower assessment and collateral management.

#### Strategies for Risk Mitigation

- **Third-Party Lending Agents:** These agents handle the operational aspects of securities lending, including borrower selection and collateral management, reducing the administrative burden on the ETF.
- **Robust Credit Guidelines:** Setting stringent credit criteria for borrowers helps minimize default risk.
- **Continuous Monitoring:** Regularly assessing borrower creditworthiness and collateral quality ensures that the ETF can respond promptly to changing market conditions.

### Examples of Securities Lending Impact

Securities lending can have varying impacts on ETF performance, particularly during periods of market stress. For instance, during the 2008 financial crisis, some ETFs faced challenges due to borrower defaults and declining collateral values. However, in stable markets, securities lending can enhance ETF returns without significantly increasing risk.

#### Case Study: Canadian Pension Funds

Canadian pension funds, such as the Canada Pension Plan Investment Board (CPPIB), have successfully utilized securities lending to enhance returns. By implementing robust risk management practices and leveraging third-party agents, these funds have managed to balance income generation with risk exposure.

### Glossary

- **Securities Lending:** The practice of loaning securities to short sellers in exchange for fees and collateral.
- **Creditworthiness:** The assessable risk that a borrower will default on their obligations.
- **Default Risk:** The risk that a borrower will fail to meet their debt obligations.
- **Collateral:** Assets pledged by a borrower to secure a loan, reducing the lender’s risk.
- **NI 81-102:** National Instrument regulating mutual funds and ETFs, including securities lending practices.
- **Securities Lending Agent:** A third-party agent that facilitates securities lending on behalf of ETFs.

### Conclusion

Securities lending offers ETFs a valuable opportunity to enhance returns, but it also introduces specific risks that require careful management. By understanding the intricacies of securities lending, assessing borrower creditworthiness, adhering to regulatory guidelines, and employing effective risk management strategies, ETFs can successfully navigate the complexities of securities lending. As investors, understanding these dynamics is crucial for making informed decisions and optimizing portfolio performance.
