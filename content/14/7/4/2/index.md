## 19.4.2 Concentration Risk

Exchange-Traded Funds (ETFs) have become a popular investment vehicle due to their ability to provide diversified exposure to various asset classes. However, one of the risks associated with ETFs is concentration risk. This section will delve into the intricacies of concentration risk, its implications for ETF diversification, and strategies to mitigate this risk.

### Definition and Implications

**Concentration Risk** refers to the potential for an investment portfolio to be overly exposed to a particular asset, sector, or geographic region. In the context of ETFs, concentration risk arises when a significant portion of the fund's assets is invested in a small number of securities. This can lead to amplified exposure to specific sectors or companies, which may result in increased volatility and potential losses if those sectors or companies underperform.

For example, an ETF heavily weighted in technology stocks may experience significant volatility if the technology sector faces downturns. This lack of diversification can undermine the stability of an investor's portfolio, as the performance of the ETF becomes closely tied to the fortunes of a few holdings.

### Regulatory Limits

In Canada, regulatory bodies impose certain limits to manage concentration risk within ETFs. One key regulation is the **10% rule**, which restricts ETFs from investing more than 10% of their net asset value in securities of a single issuer. This rule is designed to prevent excessive exposure to any one company and to promote diversification.

It's important to note the distinction between index-based and actively managed ETFs. Index-based ETFs aim to replicate the performance of a specific index, which may inherently have concentration in certain sectors or companies. Actively managed ETFs, on the other hand, have more flexibility in their holdings but must still adhere to regulatory limits.

### Assessing ETF Concentration

Investors can assess the concentration risk of an ETF by analyzing its holdings. Key metrics to consider include:

- **Top Holdings:** Review the percentage of the ETF's assets allocated to its top holdings. A high percentage indicates potential concentration risk.
- **Sector Allocation:** Examine the ETF's exposure to different sectors. A heavy concentration in one sector can increase risk.
- **Geographic Exposure:** Consider the geographic distribution of the ETF's investments. Concentration in a single region can also pose risks.

During market fluctuations, ETFs with concentrated holdings may experience more pronounced performance swings compared to more diversified funds. For instance, during the 2020 COVID-19 pandemic, ETFs concentrated in travel and hospitality sectors faced significant declines.

### Mitigation Strategies

To mitigate concentration risk, investors can employ several strategies:

1. **Diversification Across Broader Indices:** Consider investing in ETFs that track broader indices, which naturally offer more diversified exposure across sectors and companies.

2. **Investing in Low-Concentration ETFs:** Select ETFs with a more balanced distribution of assets across multiple holdings, reducing reliance on any single security.

3. **Holding Individual Securities:** In some cases, holding individual securities alongside ETFs can provide additional diversification and control over concentration levels.

4. **Regular Portfolio Review:** Periodically review and rebalance your portfolio to ensure it aligns with your risk tolerance and investment goals.

### Examples of Concentration Risk

Let's examine some real-world examples to illustrate concentration risk in ETFs:

- **Technology Sector ETFs:** An ETF heavily weighted in technology giants like Apple, Microsoft, and Amazon may perform well during tech booms but could suffer during sector downturns.

- **Canadian Financial ETFs:** ETFs focused on Canadian banks such as RBC and TD Bank may face concentration risk if the financial sector experiences challenges, such as regulatory changes or economic downturns.

In both cases, the performance of these ETFs is closely tied to the fortunes of a few key players, highlighting the importance of understanding and managing concentration risk.

### Glossary

- **Concentration Risk:** The risk associated with holding a large proportion of an ETF's assets in a small number of securities.
- **Diversification:** The practice of spreading investments across various assets to reduce risk.
- **Unsystematic Risk:** The risk inherent to a specific company or industry, which can be mitigated through diversification.
- **Regulatory Limits:** Rules set by regulatory bodies to limit the concentration of investments within a fund.

### Conclusion

Understanding concentration risk is crucial for investors seeking to optimize their ETF portfolios. By recognizing the implications of concentrated holdings and employing strategies to mitigate this risk, investors can enhance portfolio stability and achieve more consistent returns. Regularly reviewing ETF holdings and staying informed about regulatory changes are essential practices for managing concentration risk effectively.
