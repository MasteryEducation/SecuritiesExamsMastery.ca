## 19.7.2 Rebalancing

In the dynamic world of investing, maintaining a balanced portfolio is crucial for managing risk and achieving long-term financial goals. Rebalancing is the process of realigning the proportions of assets in a portfolio to maintain a desired asset allocation. This section explores the purpose of rebalancing, the role of Exchange-Traded Funds (ETFs) in facilitating this process, and the various strategies and techniques investors can employ to keep their portfolios on track.

### Purpose of Rebalancing

Rebalancing is essential for several reasons:

1. **Risk Management:** Over time, market movements can cause the asset allocation of a portfolio to drift away from its target. For example, if equities outperform bonds, a portfolio initially balanced at 60% equities and 40% bonds might shift to 70% equities and 30% bonds. This shift increases the portfolio's risk profile, potentially exposing the investor to more volatility than intended.

2. **Achieving Long-Term Goals:** Maintaining a target asset allocation helps ensure that a portfolio remains aligned with an investor's risk tolerance and investment objectives. By rebalancing, investors can systematically buy low and sell high, potentially enhancing returns over the long term.

3. **Discipline and Consistency:** Rebalancing enforces a disciplined approach to investing, preventing emotional decision-making that can lead to suboptimal outcomes.

### Role of ETFs in Rebalancing

ETFs are powerful tools for rebalancing due to their inherent characteristics:

- **Liquidity and Tradability:** ETFs trade on exchanges like stocks, offering high liquidity and the ability to buy or sell throughout the trading day. This feature allows investors to quickly adjust their portfolios in response to market changes.

- **Cost-Effectiveness:** ETFs typically have lower expense ratios compared to mutual funds, and their trading costs are generally lower, making them an efficient choice for rebalancing.

- **Diverse Exposure:** With ETFs, investors can gain exposure to a wide range of asset classes, sectors, and geographies, facilitating comprehensive rebalancing across a diversified portfolio.

### Rebalancing Strategies

Investors can choose from several rebalancing strategies, each with its own advantages and considerations:

1. **Periodic Rebalancing:** This strategy involves rebalancing the portfolio at regular intervals, such as quarterly or annually. It is straightforward and ensures consistent maintenance of the target allocation.

2. **Threshold-Based Rebalancing:** In this approach, rebalancing is triggered when asset allocations deviate beyond predefined thresholds (e.g., 5% deviation from the target). This method can be more responsive to market changes than periodic rebalancing.

3. **Automatic Rebalancing:** Some investment platforms offer automatic rebalancing services, where portfolios are adjusted automatically to maintain target allocations. This strategy reduces the need for manual intervention and can be particularly useful for passive investors.

### Implementation Techniques

Implementing rebalancing using ETFs involves several techniques:

- **Selling Overperforming ETFs:** When an asset class outperforms, its weight in the portfolio increases. Investors can sell a portion of these overperforming ETFs to bring the allocation back to the target level.

- **Buying Underperforming ETFs:** Conversely, investors can purchase more of the underperforming ETFs to increase their weight in the portfolio, aligning with the target allocation.

- **Using Limit Orders:** To control the execution price during rebalancing, investors can use limit orders, which specify the maximum price to buy or the minimum price to sell.

### Benefits of ETF-Based Rebalancing

Rebalancing with ETFs offers several benefits:

- **Lower Trading Costs:** Due to their low expense ratios and trading costs, ETFs are a cost-effective option for rebalancing.

- **Tax Efficiency:** ETFs are generally more tax-efficient than mutual funds, as they often have lower capital gains distributions.

- **Ease of Execution:** The continuous trading and real-time pricing of ETFs simplify the rebalancing process, allowing for timely adjustments.

### Examples of Rebalancing with ETFs

Consider a Canadian investor with a portfolio consisting of 60% equities and 40% bonds. Over time, the equities portion grows to 70% due to market performance. To rebalance, the investor sells a portion of the equity ETFs and buys bond ETFs to restore the original allocation.

#### Case Study: Canadian Pension Fund

A Canadian pension fund employs threshold-based rebalancing using ETFs. The fund sets a 5% threshold for asset class deviations. When equities exceed their target allocation by 5%, the fund sells equity ETFs and buys fixed-income ETFs, maintaining its strategic asset allocation and managing risk effectively.

### Glossary

- **Rebalancing:** The process of realigning the proportions of assets in a portfolio to maintain a desired asset allocation.
- **Periodic Rebalancing:** Rebalancing a portfolio at regular intervals, such as quarterly or annually.
- **Threshold-Based Rebalancing:** Rebalancing a portfolio when asset allocations deviate beyond predefined thresholds.
- **Automatic Rebalancing:** Using automated tools or services to maintain target asset allocations without manual intervention.
- **Limit Orders:** Orders placed to buy or sell a security at a specific price or better, used to control the execution price during rebalancing.

### Conclusion

Rebalancing is a critical component of effective portfolio management, ensuring that investment strategies remain aligned with an investor's goals and risk tolerance. By leveraging the liquidity, cost-effectiveness, and diverse exposure of ETFs, investors can implement efficient and strategic rebalancing practices. Understanding and applying these strategies can enhance portfolio performance and risk management, ultimately contributing to long-term financial success.
