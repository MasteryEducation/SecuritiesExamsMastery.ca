## 19.7 Identify the Investment Strategies Involving ETFs

Exchange-Traded Funds (ETFs) have revolutionized the investment landscape by offering a versatile tool for portfolio management. In this section, we will delve into various investment strategies involving ETFs, focusing on their application within the Canadian financial context. We will explore core and satellite strategies, rebalancing, tactical asset allocation, cash management, exposure to hard-to-access markets, and tax loss harvesting.

### Core and Satellite Strategy

The core and satellite strategy is a popular investment approach that combines broad market exposure with targeted investments. 

#### Core Holdings

Core holdings are typically broad market ETFs that form the foundation of a diversified investment portfolio. These ETFs provide exposure to a wide range of asset classes, such as the S&P/TSX Composite Index, which represents the Canadian equity market. By investing in core ETFs, investors can achieve a stable and cost-effective base for their portfolio.

#### Satellite Holdings

Satellite holdings, on the other hand, are specialized or sector-specific ETFs used to enhance portfolio returns or target specific investment themes. For example, an investor might add a technology-focused ETF to capitalize on growth in the tech sector or a clean energy ETF to align with environmental investment goals.

#### Benefits of Core and Satellite Strategy

The combination of core and satellite ETFs allows investors to balance stability with growth potential. Core ETFs provide a solid foundation, while satellite ETFs offer opportunities for higher returns through targeted exposure. This strategy can enhance portfolio performance while managing risk.

### Rebalancing

Rebalancing is the process of realigning the proportions of assets in a portfolio to maintain a desired asset allocation. ETFs facilitate easy and cost-effective rebalancing due to their liquidity and tradability.

#### Strategies for Rebalancing with ETFs

Investors can use ETFs to rebalance their portfolios by selling overperforming assets and buying underperforming ones. This process helps maintain the intended risk level and asset allocation. For example, if Canadian equities outperform and exceed their target allocation, an investor might sell some equity ETFs and buy fixed-income ETFs to restore balance.

### Tactical Asset Allocation

Tactical asset allocation involves adjusting a portfolio’s asset mix based on short-term market forecasts or investment opportunities. ETFs are ideal for this strategy due to their flexibility and ease of trading.

#### Advantages of Tactical Asset Allocation with ETFs

ETFs allow investors to quickly and efficiently shift exposure to different asset classes or sectors. For instance, if an investor anticipates a rise in interest rates, they might reduce exposure to bond ETFs and increase holdings in equity ETFs. This agility can enhance returns and manage risks in response to market conditions.

### Cash Management

ETFs play a crucial role in managing cash within an investment portfolio. They offer liquidity and flexibility, allowing investors to park money temporarily while seeking long-term investment opportunities.

#### Benefits of Using ETFs for Cash Management

ETFs provide a convenient way to manage cash without sacrificing potential returns. For example, a short-term bond ETF can serve as a temporary holding for cash, offering better returns than a traditional savings account while maintaining liquidity.

### Exposure to Hard-to-Access Markets

ETFs provide access to niche or previously inaccessible markets, such as emerging markets, specific sectors, or specialized investment themes.

#### Diversification and Investment Opportunities

By investing in ETFs that target hard-to-access markets, investors can achieve diversification and tap into unique growth opportunities. For example, an emerging markets ETF can provide exposure to economies with high growth potential, while a sector-specific ETF might focus on innovative industries like biotechnology.

### Tax Loss Harvesting

Tax loss harvesting is a strategy that involves selling investments at a loss to offset capital gains and reduce taxable income. ETFs can be effectively utilized in this strategy.

#### Utilizing ETFs for Tax Loss Harvesting

Investors can sell underperforming ETFs to realize a loss and immediately buy a similar but not identical ETF to maintain market exposure. This approach helps avoid the superficial loss rules, which prevent claiming losses if the same investment is repurchased within 30 days.

### Examples of Investment Strategies

Let's explore some practical examples demonstrating the implementation of these strategies using ETFs:

#### Example 1: Core and Satellite Strategy

A Canadian investor might use a broad market ETF like the iShares Core S&P/TSX Capped Composite Index ETF as the core holding. To enhance returns, they could add satellite ETFs such as the BMO Equal Weight Global Gold Index ETF for exposure to the gold sector and the Invesco NASDAQ 100 Index ETF for technology sector growth.

#### Example 2: Tactical Asset Allocation

Suppose an investor expects a downturn in the Canadian real estate market. They might reduce holdings in a real estate ETF and increase exposure to a healthcare ETF, anticipating growth in that sector.

#### Example 3: Tax Loss Harvesting

An investor holds a Canadian equity ETF that has declined in value. To realize a tax loss, they sell the ETF and purchase a similar ETF that tracks a different Canadian equity index, maintaining exposure while complying with tax regulations.

### Glossary

- **Core Holdings:** Broad market ETFs that form the foundation of a diversified investment portfolio.
- **Satellite Holdings:** Specialized or sector-specific ETFs used to enhance portfolio returns or target specific investment themes.
- **Rebalancing:** The process of realigning the proportions of assets in a portfolio to maintain desired asset allocation.
- **Tactical Asset Allocation:** Adjusting the portfolio’s asset mix based on short-term market forecasts or investment opportunities.
- **Cash Management:** Strategies for efficiently managing cash within an investment portfolio to optimize returns and liquidity.
- **Tax Loss Harvesting:** Selling investments at a loss to offset capital gains and reduce taxable income.
- **Superficial Loss Rules:** Tax rules that prevent investors from claiming losses on the sale of an investment if they repurchase the same or identical investment within a short period.

### Conclusion

ETFs offer a versatile and efficient tool for implementing a wide range of investment strategies. By understanding and applying these strategies, investors can optimize their portfolios, manage risk, and enhance returns within the Canadian financial landscape. Whether using ETFs for core and satellite strategies, rebalancing, tactical asset allocation, cash management, or tax loss harvesting, the flexibility and accessibility of ETFs make them an invaluable component of modern investment portfolios.
