## 19.1.1 The Regulation and Structure of Exchange-Traded Funds

Exchange-Traded Funds (ETFs) have become a cornerstone of modern investment portfolios, offering a blend of flexibility, diversification, and cost-effectiveness. In Canada, the regulation and structure of ETFs are governed by specific national instruments and legal frameworks that ensure investor protection and market integrity. This section delves into the regulatory requirements and structural nuances of ETFs, providing a comprehensive understanding for finance professionals and students.

### Application of National Instruments

#### NI 81-102: Mutual Fund Trusts and Corporations

National Instrument 81-102 (NI 81-102) is a key regulatory framework that applies to ETFs structured as mutual fund trusts or corporations. It sets out the rules governing the distribution, management, and operation of mutual funds, including ETFs. 

- **Mutual Fund Trusts:** These are the most common structure for ETFs in Canada. NI 81-102 imposes specific investment restrictions, such as limits on concentration, illiquid assets, and borrowing. This structure offers tax efficiency, as income is distributed to unitholders, avoiding double taxation.

- **Mutual Fund Corporations:** Less common than trusts, these structures allow for the pooling of different funds under a single corporate umbrella. They offer operational flexibility, particularly in managing capital gains distributions. However, they are subject to corporate taxation, which can affect their attractiveness.

#### NI 81-104: Commodities and Derivatives

National Instrument 81-104 (NI 81-104) provides additional provisions for ETFs that utilize commodities and derivatives. This regulation allows for more aggressive strategies, enabling ETFs to engage in commodity-based investments and derivative transactions beyond the hedging purposes permitted under NI 81-102.

### Mutual Fund Trusts vs. Mutual Fund Corporations

The choice between structuring an ETF as a mutual fund trust or corporation depends on various factors, including tax considerations, investment strategy, and operational goals.

- **Regulatory Constraints:** Mutual fund trusts are subject to strict investment restrictions under NI 81-102, which limits their exposure to certain asset classes and leverage. In contrast, mutual fund corporations can offer more flexibility in managing capital gains and losses.

- **Operational Flexibility:** Corporations can manage multiple funds under a single entity, allowing for efficient capital management and potential tax advantages. Trusts, however, are simpler to manage and offer direct pass-through of income to investors.

- **Suitability:** Trusts are generally more suitable for traditional, passive index-tracking ETFs, while corporations may be preferred for actively managed or multi-strategy funds.

### Limits on Leverage and Derivatives

NI 81-102 imposes strict limits on the use of leverage and derivatives for non-hedging purposes within ETFs. These restrictions are designed to protect investors from excessive risk-taking and ensure the stability of the financial system.

- **Leverage Limits:** ETFs are generally prohibited from borrowing more than 5% of their net asset value (NAV) for non-hedging purposes. This ensures that ETFs maintain a conservative risk profile.

- **Derivatives Usage:** While NI 81-102 allows the use of derivatives for hedging, NI 81-104 permits more extensive use of derivatives and commodities, enabling ETFs to pursue more aggressive investment strategies.

### ETF Facts Document Requirements

The ETF Facts document is a critical component of the regulatory framework, providing investors with essential information about an ETF's trading and pricing characteristics.

- **Components:** The document includes details on the ETF's investment objectives, fees, risks, and historical performance. It also highlights the significance of premium and discount to NAV, which can impact an investor's returns.

- **Premium and Discount to NAV:** This refers to the difference between the market price of an ETF and its NAV. Understanding this concept is crucial for investors, as it affects the cost of buying or selling ETF units.

### Amendments to Disclosure Regimes

In December 2018, significant amendments were introduced to the disclosure delivery regime for ETFs, enhancing transparency and investor protection.

- **New Regime:** The amendments require the delivery of the ETF Facts document to investors at the point of sale, ensuring they have access to key information before making investment decisions.

- **Impact:** This change improves the distribution of information, allowing investors to make more informed choices and aligning with global best practices in financial disclosure.

### Creation and Redemption Mechanism

The creation and redemption mechanism is a fundamental aspect of ETF operations, enabling the efficient management of supply and demand for ETF units.

- **Designated Brokers:** These brokers facilitate the creation and redemption of ETF units through in-kind exchanges, maintaining price alignment with the NAV.

- **In-Kind Exchanges:** This process involves exchanging a basket of underlying securities for ETF units, or vice versa, minimizing the impact on the ETF's market price.

#### Examples of Creation and Redemption

Consider a scenario where a designated broker creates ETF units by delivering a basket of securities to the ETF provider. This process helps maintain the ETF's price integrity by aligning the market price with the NAV.

- **Bid-Ask Spreads:** The difference between the bid and ask prices can affect the cost of trading ETF units. Arbitrage opportunities arise when discrepancies occur, allowing traders to profit by buying undervalued units and selling overvalued ones.

### Glossary

- **Full Replication:** An ETF strategy where all securities in the index are held in the same proportions as the index.
- **Sampling:** An ETF strategy where a representative subset of securities from the index is held to mimic the index's performance.
- **Creation Units:** Large blocks of ETF units (typically 10,000, 25,000, or 50,000 units) created by delivering a basket of underlying securities to the ETF provider.
- **Redemption Units:** Large blocks of ETF units redeemed by exchanging them for the underlying securities from the ETF provider.
- **Tracking Error:** The deviation between the ETF's performance and the performance of its underlying index.
- **Designated Broker:** A broker responsible for creating and redeeming ETF units through in-kind exchanges.

### Conclusion

Understanding the regulation and structure of ETFs is crucial for navigating the Canadian investment landscape. By adhering to national instruments and leveraging the creation and redemption mechanisms, investors can optimize their ETF strategies while ensuring compliance with regulatory standards. As the ETF market continues to evolve, staying informed about regulatory changes and best practices will be key to successful investing.
