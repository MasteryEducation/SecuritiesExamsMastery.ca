## Glossary for Chapter 19: Exchange-Traded Funds

Understanding the terminology associated with Exchange-Traded Funds (ETFs) is crucial for navigating the complexities of the financial markets. This glossary provides comprehensive definitions, contextual understanding, and practical examples to help you master ETF-related concepts.

### Active Management

**Definition:** Active management involves fund managers making specific investment decisions with the aim of outperforming a benchmark index.

**Contextual Understanding:** In the context of ETFs, active management contrasts with passive management, where the goal is to replicate the performance of an index. Actively managed ETFs seek to capitalize on market inefficiencies through strategic asset selection.

**Usage Example:** A Canadian ETF actively managed by a team at RBC might adjust its holdings based on economic forecasts, aiming to outperform the S&P/TSX Composite Index.

### Adjusted Cost Base (ACB)

**Definition:** The original value of an investment for tax purposes, adjusted for factors such as distributions and reinvestments.

**Contextual Understanding:** ACB is crucial for calculating capital gains or losses when selling ETF units. It ensures accurate tax reporting and compliance with Canadian tax laws.

**Usage Example:** If an investor purchases ETF units at different times and prices, the ACB helps determine the average cost for tax purposes when units are sold.

### Capital Gains Refund Mechanism (CGRM)

**Definition:** A formula that prevents double taxation of capital gains by allowing funds to retain a portion of gains.

**Contextual Understanding:** In Canada, the CGRM is essential for mutual funds and ETFs to manage tax liabilities efficiently, ensuring investors are not taxed twice on capital gains.

**Usage Example:** A Canadian ETF might use the CGRM to retain some capital gains, reducing the taxable amount distributed to investors.

### Covered Call Strategy

**Definition:** An options strategy where an investor holds a long position in an asset and sells call options on that same asset.

**Contextual Understanding:** This strategy is often used by ETFs to generate additional income from the premiums received from selling call options, enhancing returns in flat or mildly bullish markets.

**Usage Example:** A Canadian ETF might employ a covered call strategy on its holdings of major Canadian banks to boost income.

### Designated Broker

**Definition:** A brokerage firm authorized to create and redeem ETF units.

**Contextual Understanding:** Designated brokers play a critical role in maintaining the liquidity and market efficiency of ETFs by facilitating the creation and redemption process.

**Usage Example:** TD Securities might act as a designated broker for a Canadian ETF, ensuring that the ETF's market price closely tracks its NAV.

### In-Kind Exchange

**Definition:** The process of exchanging securities for ETF units without involving cash transactions.

**Contextual Understanding:** In-kind exchanges help minimize tax liabilities and transaction costs for both the ETF and investors, as securities are transferred directly.

**Usage Example:** An investor might exchange a basket of Canadian stocks for units of an ETF that tracks the same index, avoiding capital gains taxes.

### Management Expense Ratio (MER)

**Definition:** The total annual fees expressed as a percentage of the fund’s average net assets.

**Contextual Understanding:** MER is a critical factor in evaluating the cost-effectiveness of an ETF, impacting net returns to investors.

**Usage Example:** A Canadian ETF with a low MER might appeal to cost-conscious investors seeking to maximize their returns.

### Net Asset Value (NAV)

**Definition:** The value per share of a mutual fund or ETF on a specific date or time.

**Contextual Understanding:** NAV is used to determine the price at which ETF units are bought or sold, reflecting the underlying value of the fund's assets.

**Usage Example:** An ETF tracking the TSX 60 might have a NAV calculated at the end of each trading day, guiding investors on its fair value.

### Sampling

**Definition:** A method of constructing an ETF portfolio by selecting a representative subset of the underlying index components.

**Contextual Understanding:** Sampling is used when full replication of an index is impractical due to cost or liquidity constraints, allowing ETFs to closely track index performance.

**Usage Example:** A Canadian bond ETF might use sampling to hold a representative selection of bonds from the FTSE Canada Universe Bond Index.

### Synthetic ETF

**Definition:** An ETF that uses derivatives to replicate the performance of an index rather than holding the actual securities.

**Contextual Understanding:** Synthetic ETFs can offer cost advantages and access to markets where direct investment is challenging, but they also introduce counterparty risk.

**Usage Example:** A synthetic ETF might use swaps to mimic the performance of the S&P/TSX Composite Index without holding the actual stocks.

### Tracking Error

**Definition:** The deviation between the performance of an ETF and its underlying index.

**Contextual Understanding:** Tracking error measures how closely an ETF follows its benchmark, with lower errors indicating better tracking accuracy.

**Usage Example:** An ETF with a tracking error of 0.1% is closely aligned with its index, providing confidence to investors in its performance consistency.

### Smart Beta

**Definition:** An investment strategy that uses alternative index construction rules to enhance returns or manage risk.

**Contextual Understanding:** Smart beta ETFs aim to outperform traditional market-cap-weighted indices by focusing on factors like value, momentum, or volatility.

**Usage Example:** A Canadian smart beta ETF might overweight undervalued stocks in the TSX 60 to capture potential upside.

### Counterparty Risk

**Definition:** The risk that the other party in a financial contract will default on their obligations.

**Contextual Understanding:** In the context of synthetic ETFs, counterparty risk arises from reliance on derivatives, necessitating careful assessment and management.

**Usage Example:** An investor in a synthetic ETF should consider the creditworthiness of the counterparties involved in the fund's derivative contracts.

### Leveraged ETF

**Definition:** An ETF that uses financial derivatives and debt to amplify the returns of an underlying index.

**Contextual Understanding:** Leveraged ETFs are designed for short-term trading, offering magnified exposure to market movements but also increased risk.

**Usage Example:** A 2x leveraged ETF tracking the TSX 60 aims to deliver twice the daily return of the index, appealing to traders seeking amplified gains.

### Inverse ETF

**Definition:** An ETF designed to deliver the opposite of the performance of a specific index.

**Contextual Understanding:** Inverse ETFs are used for hedging or speculative purposes, allowing investors to profit from declines in an index.

**Usage Example:** An inverse ETF on the TSX 60 might be used by investors anticipating a market downturn to offset potential losses.

### Management Style

**Definition:** The approach employed by fund managers, whether active or passive, in managing an ETF or mutual fund.

**Contextual Understanding:** Management style influences the fund's investment strategy, risk profile, and potential returns, guiding investor decisions.

**Usage Example:** A passive management style in a Canadian ETF might involve replicating the TSX 60, while an active style seeks to outperform it.

### Transparency

**Definition:** The degree to which an ETF or mutual fund discloses its holdings and investment strategies.

**Contextual Understanding:** Transparency is vital for investor confidence, allowing for informed decision-making and risk assessment.

**Usage Example:** A Canadian ETF that provides daily updates on its holdings enhances transparency, attracting investors seeking clarity.

### Diversification

**Definition:** The practice of spreading investments across various assets to reduce risk.

**Contextual Understanding:** Diversification is a core principle of ETF investing, mitigating the impact of individual asset volatility on the overall portfolio.

**Usage Example:** A Canadian ETF might diversify across sectors like financials, energy, and technology to balance risk and return.

### Liquidity

**Definition:** The ease with which an asset can be bought or sold in the market without affecting its price.

**Contextual Understanding:** Liquidity is crucial for ETFs, ensuring that investors can enter or exit positions efficiently without significant price impact.

**Usage Example:** A highly liquid Canadian ETF allows investors to trade large volumes with minimal bid-ask spreads.

### Tax Efficiency

**Definition:** The ability of an investment to minimize tax liabilities through strategic management of gains and distributions.

**Contextual Understanding:** ETFs are generally tax-efficient due to their structure, which allows for in-kind transactions and minimized capital gains distributions.

**Usage Example:** A Canadian ETF might employ tax-loss harvesting strategies to enhance tax efficiency for its investors.
